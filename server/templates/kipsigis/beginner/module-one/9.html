1.5.1 Summary of Numbers & Numbering

Kipsigis Numbers and Their Usage

Kipsigis employs a decimal-based numbering system, reflecting precision and cultural significance in trade, timekeeping, and social organization. Numbers are systematically constructed using an additive approach.

Cardinal Numbers

Basic Numbers (1–10): Agenge (1), Aoeng (2), Somok (3), Ang'wan (4), Mut (5), Lo (6), Tisab (7), Sisit (8), Sogol (9), Taman (10).

Compound Numbers (11–19): Formed by combining "taman ak" (10) with units (e.g., Taman ak Agenge = 11, Taman ak Oeng = 12).

Multiples of Ten (20–90): (20-50) has their own specific namings i.e 20 (tiptem),30 (Sosom),40 (artam),50 (konom) From (60-90) "tamonwokik" is prefixed to numbers (e.g., tomonwokik lo = 60, Tomonwokik tisab = 70, Tomonwokik sisit=80, Tomonwokik sogol = 90).

Hundreds (100–900): Formed using "bogol" (e.g., Bogol oeng = 200, Bogol sogol = 900).

Thousands (1000–9000): Formed using "eliput(one), elipushek(many)" (e.g., Elipushek Somok = 3000, Elipushek Sogol = 9000).

Usage in Sentences

Trade & Counting: Komin kandoik ketik tomonwokik tisab (Elders planted seventy trees).

Family & Social Context: Chepyoset agenge ak muren oeng (One woman and two men).

Examples of Quantification

  • Ng'ororek (goats): Mut (five).
  • Tipik (girls): Sogol(nine).
  • Meng'otoshek(homes): Taman ak mut (fifteen).
  • Lagok (children): Taman ak agenge (eleven).
  • korik(houses): Taman (ten).

Ordinal Numbers:

Ordinal numbers in Kipsigis denote order, rank, and sequence, formed by prefixing nebo to cardinal numbers, except for netai (first) and neletu(last).

Structure and Formation

Key Ordinal Numbers: Netai (First), Nebo oeng (Second), Nebo somok (Third), Nebo ang'wan (Fourth), Nebo mut (Fifth), etc.

The prefix nebo establishes relational meaning, ensuring clarity and consistency in sequencing.

Linguistic and Cultural Relevance

Order of Events: Used in storytelling and genealogy (Nebo somok lakwet ko mokisor – "The third child did not survive").

Rank and Leadership: Expressing hierarchy (Boyon neboo oeng koimuch kondoo – "The second elder may act as a leader").

Education and Rituals: Essential in classroom instructions and calendrical events (Arawetab lo ko nyoljin kinetengei boisyek – "The sixth month is suitable for learning from elders").

Examples in Context

Tondet nebo ang'wan ko kagoit ("The fourth guest has arrived").
Chito nebo mut nekosor lakwet ("The fifth person rescued the child").
Moimukogsei kochililen tugul arawet ab sogol ("They cannot all survive in ninth place").

Days of the Week

The Kipsigis weekly calendar consists of seven days, each rooted in work, economy, and spirituality.

  • Juma-tatu (Monday): "First day of work," marking the resumption of labor. (Juma-tatu betut nekiwekyinigei boisyonik.)
  • Kasitab-oeng(Tuesday): "Second workday," reinforcing labor continuity. (Kasitab oeng kikimitee ngalekab boisyonik.)
  • Kasitab somo (Wednesday): "Third workday," a mid-week agricultural marker. (Kasitab somok betut neboo kwen nebo kobotishet.)
  • Kasitab angw'an(Thursday): "Fourth workday," preparation for market activities. (Kasitab angwan,kichobchingei ngalekab ndonyo.)
  • Kasitab mut (Friday): "Fifth workday," a significant day before market interactions. (Kasitab mut,betut neo yon kikonyee mungaret.)
  • Jumamos (Saturday): Market Day for trade and economic transactions. (betutab ndonyo ak mungaret.)
  • Juma-mpili (Sunday): A rest day dedicated to worship. (Juma-mpili betut neboo komunget ak saet.)

Months of the Year

The Kipsigis twelve-month system aligns with the Gregorian calendar, structured through ordinal numeration with Arawet ab (Month of).

  • January – Arawet Netai: "First month,"
  • February – Arawet ab oeng: "Second month,"
  • March – Arawet ab somok: "Third month,"
  • April – Arawet ab Ang'wan: "Fourth month,"
  • May – Arawet ab mut: "Fifth month,"
  • June – Arawet ab lo: "Sixth month,"
  • July – Arawet ab tisab: "Seventh month,"
  • August – Arawet ab sisit: "Eighth month,"
  • September – Arawwet ab sogol: "Ninth month,"
  • October – Arawet ab taman: "Tenth month,"
  • November – Arawet ab taman ak agenge: "Eleventh month,"
  • December – Arawet ab taman ak oeng: "Twelfth month,"

Time Expressions

Kipsigis employs a rich temporal lexicon to articulate past, present, and future timeframes.

General temporal markers include Ingunii (now), Amut (yesterday), Raini(today), Koron (tomorrow), and Koyoin (the day after tomorrow).

The language structures time through a four-part day division: Koronet (morning), koskoleng' (afternoon), Langat (evening), and Kemoo (night), with additional nuances like Korenet (early morning) and Kemoo kwen (midnight).

Time expressions follow distinct syntactic structures, such as Amut ko koger kwanda ("Yesterday, he saw his father") or Awendi tokor ("I will go later").

These linguistic patterns demonstrate Kipsigis' structured approach to temporality, blending sequential logic with cultural context.
