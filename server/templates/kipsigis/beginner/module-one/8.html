1.4.5 Telling Time

I. Overview of Temporal Systems in Kipsigis

  • Time as a Lexical System: The Kipsigis language expresses time through a rich array of temporal markers that distinguish past, present, and future tenses, as well as specific segments of the day.
  • Cultural Timekeeping: Time is perceived cyclically and functionally, tied to nature, activity, and social interaction rather than mechanical clocks.

II. General Temporal Expressions in Kipsigis

These expressions provide a temporal frame of reference for both speech and daily activities:

  1. Ng'uni – Now
    • Denotes the present moment, used to express current actions.
    • Example: Atinye boisyet newoo ing'uni. → "I have a lot of work now."
  2. Ng'uni ng'uni – Right Now
    • An emphatic form of "now," signaling immediacy or urgency.
  3. Toor – Later
    • Projects future action with unspecified timing.
    • Example: Toor, ketoretii en boisyet. → "Later, we will help with the work."
  4. Amut – Yesterday
    • Marks the recent past.
    • Example: Komochee koger kwandaa amut. → "He wanted to see their father yesterday."
  5. Oiin – The Day Before Yesterday
    • Indicates a more distant past, two days ago.
  6. Raa/Raaini – Today
    • Contextual anchor for present-day activities or events.
  7. Koron – Tomorrow
    • Refers to the immediate future, often associated with planned actions.
  8. Koyoin – The Day After Tomorrow
    • Indicates a distant upcoming day, useful for scheduling or promises.
    • Example: Koyoin kebendii gaa. → "The day after tomorrow, we will go home."

III. Temporal Segmentation of the Day

The Kipsigis day is divided into distinct periods, each encoded with a specific term:

  1. Koronet – Morning
    • Associated with light, beginnings, and freshness.
    • Example: Karonet, kong'ololen biik lobkeyet. → "In the morning, people talk about light."
  2. Koskoleng' – Afternoon
    • Refers to the midday period, often linked with labour or heat.
  3. Lang'at – Evening
    • Denotes twilight or early night, often marking the end of activity.
    • Example: Koimen kotokorue chito. → "It became dusk when someone was sleeping."
  4. Kemoo – Night
    • Signifies the dark hours, associated with rest, dreams, or mystery.

IV. Supplementary Time Expressions

These lexemes express precision in time references:

  1. Kariron – Very Early in the Morning
    • Reflects the pre-dawn period, often tied to rituals or travel.
  2. Kemoo kwen – Midnight
    • Represents deep night, culturally linked to silence, alertness, or supernatural awareness.
    • Example: Kemoo kwen, kogas koraa tugoshek. → "At midnight, she still heard voices."

V. Sentence Structure in Time Expressions

Kipsigis utilizes flexible syntactic patterns to embed time markers within clauses:

  1. Structure A: Time Marker + Subject + Verb + Object
    Example: Amut ko kogeer kwanda. → "Yesterday, he saw his father."
  2. Structure B: Subject + Verb + Time Marker
    Example: Ogerin kor langat. → "(I) will see you later."
  3. Structure C: Embedded Temporal Adverbials
    Example: Kemoo kwen, kogas koraa tugoshek. → "At midnight, she still heard voices."

VI. Concluding Remarks

  • Temporal Literacy in Kipsigis: The language demonstrates a highly nuanced and culturally embedded approach to expressing time.
  • Semantic Richness: Terms are not mere indicators of clock-time but convey social function, emotional tone, and environmental cues.
  • Structural Versatility: Sentence construction permits dynamic placement of time expressions, enhancing expressive depth and clarity.
