2.5.4 Distributive Pronouns

Distributive pronouns in English are words like each, every, either, and neither. They refer to individuals within a group, rather than the group as a whole. This means they highlight each member being considered separately, emphasizing individual actions or characteristics.

1. "Each One" (referring to a person):

Ng'ato ka ng'ato

  • Literally: person by person
  • Used when referring to every single individual separately
  • Structure:
    • Ng'ato = a person
    • Ka ng'ato = each individual person
  • Example:
    • Ng'ato ka ng'ato biro chiemo — Each one will eat.
    • Ng'ato ka ng'ato osegoyo mbaka — Each person has made his story.

2. "Each One" (referring to a thing):

Moro ka moro

  • Literally: thing by thing
  • Refers to each individual item in a group
  • Structure:
    • Moro = a thing/something
  • Example:
    • Moro ka moro nyalo tiyo — Each one (thing) can work.
    • Moro ka moro nigi tiendgi — Each thing has its meaning.

3. "Any One" (referring to a person):

Ng'ato ang'ata

  • Used to refer to any one person, non-specific
  • Expresses randomness or unspecified individual
  • Structure:
    • Ng'ato = a person
    • Ang'ata = any one
  • Example:
    • Ng'ato ang'ata nyalo bedo jaloyo — Anyone can be a winner.
    • Ng'ato ang'ata nyalo wuok — Any person can go out.

4. "Any One" (referring to a thing):

Moro a mora

  • Used to refer to any one item, random or unspecified thing
  • Structure:
    • Moro = one
    • A mora = any one thing
  • Example:
    • Ket moro a mora e mesa — Put any one on the table.
    • I nyal yiero moro a mora — You may choose any.

5. "All"

Duto or Duto tee!

  • Refers to all things or all people, collectively
  • Duto tee! is emphatic — "Absolutely all!"
  • Structure:
    • Duto = all
    • Tee = intensifier (adds emphasis)
  • Example:
    • Jowetegi duto biro — All their relatives will come.
    • Chiemo duto tee ne ocham! — Absolutely all the food was eaten!

6. "Anything"

Gimoro amora

  • Refers to any possible thing, open-ended meaning
  • Emphasizes that no specific limit is set
  • Structure:
    • Gimoro = something
    • Amora = any kind/type
  • Example:
    • Kel gimoro amora mane ihero — Bring anything you like.
    • Gimoro amora inyalo yudo kanyo — Anything could be found there.

7. "Something"

Gimoro

  • Refers to a non-specific item — "something"
  • Used when the speaker has an idea or suspicion of the item but it is not stated
  • Structure:
    • Gi- = class prefix (for things)
    • Moro = one
  • Example:
    • Awinjo gimoro e odno — I heard something in that house.
    • Gimoro ne oyud e dhoot — Something was found at the door.
