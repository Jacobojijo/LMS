2.7.2 Noun Declension in Dholuo

In Dholuo grammar, noun declension refers to how the form of a noun changes depending on its syntactical role (subject, object, etc.) in a sentence. Below are key details about noun declensions in Dholuo:

1. Cases in Dholuo:

  • Nominative Case (Subject Form): This is the form of the noun when it is the subject of a sentence. It remains unchanged in Dholuo.
  • Accusative Case (Object Form): Nouns in the accusative case also retain the same form in Dholuo. There is no special alteration for accusatives.
  • Genitive Case (Possessive Form): This is the only case where the noun changes. The genitive case in Dholuo indicates possession and often involves a change in the ending of the noun.
  • Dative Case (Indirect Object Form): Like the nominative and accusative, the dative case does not cause any change in the noun form.
  • Oblative Case (Prepositional Form): Similarly, to nominative, accusative, and dative cases, nouns in the oblique (oblate) case remain unchanged.

2. Genitive Declension (Possessive):

  • Genitive Case: The genitive is the form used to show possession. In Dholuo, the genitive case changes the form of the noun. Notably, this change involves alterations in the ending or structure of the noun.
  • Examples of Genitive Declension:
    • Lum (grass) → Lumb
    • Diel (goat) → Diend
    • Lowo (land/soil) → Lop
    • Buk (book) → Bug
    • Kidi (stone) → Kit
    • Oboke (leaf) → Obok
    • Puodh (land) → Puoth
    • Got (mountain) → God
  • Pronunciation Changes: Some nouns do not change in spelling, but their pronunciation alters in the genitive case. For instance:
    • Lak (tooth) remains Lak in the genitive case but the accent shifts from the "L" to the "K" sound in Lak (tooth) vs. Lak (of the tooth).

3. Key Nouns and Their Genitive Forms:

  • Um (nose) → Genitive: Unchanged, but with a shift in accent or emphasis.
  • Kom (chair) → Genitive: Kom is pronounced with a different accent or stress in possession.
  • Kidi (stone) → Genitive: Kit.
  • Oboke (leaf) → Genitive: Obok.
  • Puodh (land) → Genitive: Puoth.
  • Got (mountain) → Genitive: God.

4. Noun Changes and Common Patterns:

  • Consonant endings: Nouns in the genitive case often end in a consonant, which is a notable pattern in Dholuo declension.
    • Example: Puodh becomes Puoth, Got becomes God.
  • Non-Changing Nouns: Some nouns, like Lak (tooth), do not change in spelling but are differentiated by pronunciation.

5. Important Notes:

  • Focus on Genitive Declension: The genitive case requires the most attention from learners because it involves changes in form and sometimes pronunciation.
  • Consonant-Based Changes: In many cases, the noun's form in the genitive case ends in a consonant, making it easy to identify.
  • No Change in Other Cases: Except for the genitive case, nouns do not change in the accusative, dative, or oblique cases, keeping their basic forms.
