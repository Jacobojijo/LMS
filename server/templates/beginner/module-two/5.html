2.1.5 Parts of the Body

Introduction

In Dholuo, "parts of the body" refers not only to human anatomy but also forms a significant part of language structures â€” influencing vocabulary, idioms, expressions, pronoun references, and possessive constructions. Body parts are common nouns and are countable (for most external body parts) or uncountable (for some internal or generalized references).

Grammatical Roles of Body Parts

The grammatical treatment of body parts in Dholuo highlights possession, countability, and syntactic positioning.

A. Noun Usage

1. Countable Nouns

External body parts are treated as countable entities.

  • Example:
    • "I have two hands."
      • An gi lwedo ariyo.
    • Notes: Plural markers are used (ariyo = "two").

2. Uncountable or Collective Usage

Internal parts, particularly fluids like blood or organs conceptually understood as singular, are treated as uncountable.

  • Example:
    • "Blood flows through the body."
      • Remo ringo e del.

Important: The singularity or collectivity affects verb agreement and quantification.

B. Possessive Structures

Possession in Dholuo strongly marks body parts. Unlike English, where definite articles may sometimes stand alone (e.g., "the hand"), Dholuo requires possessive pronouns to denote ownership unless contextually obvious.

English Dholuo Gloss
My hand Lweta "My hand"
Your hand Lweti "Your hand"
His/her hand Lwete "His/her hand" (context or pronominal clitic specifies gender)
  • Correct Usage:
    • "She broke her arm."
      • Ne oturo lwete.
  • Incorrect Usage:
    • "She broke the arm." (ambiguous without possessive.)

Key Rule: Always insert a possessive marker unless generalizing or using a set expression.

C. Subject and Object Roles

Body parts can serve both as grammatical subjects and objects:

1. Body Parts as Subjects

  • Example:
    • "His head hurt after the accident."
      • Wiyo norame bang' masira.
    • Analysis: The head (wiyo) serves as the subject; norame (was hurting) is the predicate.

2. Body Parts as Objects

  • Example:
    • "She injured her knee during the match."
      • Ne ohinyo chonge ka gitugo.

Analysis: The knee (chonge) is the object of the verb ohinyo ("injured").
