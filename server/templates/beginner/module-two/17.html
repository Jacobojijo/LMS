2.6.1 Summary of Pronouns (Part 01)

Demonstrative Pronouns (Dholuo)

Definition: Pronouns used to point to or refer to specific nouns, indicating proximity (near or far).

Singular Demonstratives:

  • Ma (mani) → This (near the speaker)
  • Example: Ma en otanda → This is a bed.
  • Mano → That (near the listener)
  • Example: Mano en kom-a → That is my chair.
  • Macha → That over there (far from both speaker and listener)
  • Example: Macha e jakwath → That is a herdsman over there.

Plural Demonstratives:

  • Magi → These (near the speaker)
  • Example: Magi gin yawuoi → These are boys.
  • Mago → Those (near the listener)
  • Example: Mago ang'o? → What are those?
  • Maka → Those (far from both)
  • Example: Maka gin jokwath → Those are herdsmen.

Demonstratives in Questions:

  • Ma ang'o? → What is this?
  • Mago ang'o? → What are those?
  • Macha ang'o? → What is that (far over there)?

Demonstratives with Nouns:

Used before or after nouns to specify them:

  • Ma en pacho (dala) → This is a home.
  • Ma en ogut-a → This is my hat.

Demonstratives + Possessive Pronouns:

Combines with possessive pronouns for specificity:

  • Man en kom-a → This is my chair.
  • Maka gin dhog-u → Those are your cows.

Specific Demonstrative Nouns:

  • Yadhni → This tree
  • Wuoyini → This boy
  • Jagoni → This fighter
  • Yawoni → This pond

Demonstratives Indicating Place:

  • Ka → Here
  • Kae → This place
  • Ka-cha → That place (far)
  • Examples: Odni → This house, Yorni → This path.

Demonstratives Indicating Time:

  • Sani → Now
  • Kawuono → Today
  • Nyoro → Yesterday
  • Kiny → Tomorrow

Demonstratives for Speed/Action:

  • Piyo → Quickly
  • Mos → Slowly

Distributive Pronouns

Definition: Pronouns that refer to individuals within a group, emphasizing each member separately.

"Each One" (Person):

  • Ng'ato ka ng'ato → Each person individually
  • Example: Ng'ato ka ng'ato biro chiemo → Each one will eat.

"Each One" (Thing):

  • Moro ka moro → Each item individually
  • Example: Moro ka moro nigi tiendgi → Each thing has its meaning.

"Any One" (Person):

  • Ng'ato ang'ata → Any one person (unspecified)
  • Example: Ng'ato ang'ata nyalo bedo jaloyo → Anyone can be a winner.

"Any One" (Thing):

  • Moro a mora → Any one thing (random)
  • Example: Ket moro a mora e mesa → Put any one on the table.

"All":

  • Duto / Duto tee! → All (emphatic form)
  • Example: Chiemo duto tee ne ocham! → Absolutely all the food was eaten.

"Anything":

  • Gimoro amora → Anything, open-ended
  • Example: Kel gimoro amora mane ihero → Bring anything you like.

"Something":

  • Gimoro → Something (non-specific)
  • Example: Awinjo gimoro e odno → I heard something in that house.

Introduction to Dholuo Pronouns

Definition of Pronouns:

Pronouns replace nouns to simplify speech and avoid repetition.

Types of Pronouns in Dholuo:

  • Personal Pronouns: Refer to specific people or things (e.g., I, you, he).
  • Possessive Pronouns: Indicate ownership (e.g., mine, yours).
  • Demonstrative Pronouns: Point to specific things (e.g., this, that).
  • Interrogative Pronouns: Used for asking questions (e.g., who, what, which).
  • Relative Pronouns: Link clauses and refer to previously mentioned nouns.

Personal Pronouns (Subject Form):

  • An = I
  • In = You (singular)
  • En = He/She/It (gender-neutral)
  • Wan = We
  • Un = You (plural)
  • Gin = They

Object and Possessive Pronouns:

  • Often implied or context-driven.
  • Can be formed using suffixes or word placement.

Pronoun Use in Sentences:

  • Subject pronouns are typically included in verbs (e.g., Idhi = "You go").
  • Emphasis can be added by explicitly stating the pronoun before the verb (e.g., An abiro = "I will come").

Interrogative Pronouns (For asking questions):

  • Ng'ano = Who
  • Ang'o = What
  • Nang'o = Why
  • Karang'o = When
  • Kanye = Where

Personal Pronouns in Dholuo

What Are Personal Pronouns?:

  • Personal pronouns represent specific people or groups.
  • Often used in Dholuo, but can be dropped if context or verb form makes the subject clear.

The Six Core Personal Pronouns:

Dholuo Pronoun English Equivalent Usage Example
An I / me 1st person singular An e pacho → I am at home
In You (singular) 2nd person singular In e dala → You are at home
En He / She 3rd person singular En e ot → He/She is in the house
Wan We / us 1st person plural Wan e piny → We are in the land
Un You (plural) 2nd person plural Un e chokruok → You (all) are at the ceremony
Gin They / them 3rd person plural Gin e dala → They are at home

Demonstratives + Pronouns:

  • Combine demonstratives with personal pronouns to specify things or people.
  • An ma = I, this (emphasizing "I am the one")
  • En macha = He/She is that one (far)
  • Gin mano = They are that/those ones (near you)

Simple Sentence Structure:

Basic structure: Subject Pronoun + Verb + (Location/Object/Demonstrative)

Examples:

  • An dhi dala → I am going home.
  • In biro sani → You are coming now.
  • En nyalo tedo → He/She can cook.
  • Wan wabedo kae → We are sitting here.
  • Un uwuog kacha → You (all) leave that place.
  • Gin gidonjo ot → They are entering the house.

Possessive Pronouns and Cases in Dholuo

Overview:

  • Possession is indicated by adding a suffix to the noun.
  • Changes depend on:
    • Person (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
    • Number (singular/plural)
  • "Mar" (prefix) = "of/belonging to" (e.g., mar Adhiambo = Adhiambo's).

Singular Possessive Forms:

  • First Person Singular (I): Suffix -na or -wa
    • Nyathi-na = my child
    • Puodho en mara = the garden is mine
  • Second Person Singular (You): Suffix -ni or -i
    • Nyathi-ni = your child
    • Puodho en mari = the garden is yours
  • Third Person Singular (He/She/It): Suffix -ne or -e
    • Nyathi-ne = his/her child
    • Puodho en mare = the garden is his/hers/its

Plural Possessive Forms:

  • First Person Plural (We): Suffix -wa (inclusive/exclusive)
    • Nyathi-wa = our child
    • Puodho en marwa = the garden is ours
  • Second Person Plural (You all): Suffix -u
    • Nyathi-u = your (plural) child
    • Puodho en maru = the garden is yours (plural)
  • Third Person Plural (They): Suffix -gi
    • Nyathi-gi = their child
    • Puodho en margi = the garden is theirs

Use of "Mar":

  • "Mar" = shows possession ("of/belonging to").
  • Examples:
    • Nyathi mar Adhiambo = Adhiambo's child
    • Mara = mine
    • Mari = yours (singular)
    • Mare = his/hers
    • Marwa = ours
    • Maru = yours (plural)
    • Margi = theirs

Real-Life Sentences (using "Puodho" – Garden):

  • Puodho en mara → The garden is mine
  • Puodho en mari → The garden is yours (singular)
  • Puodho en mare → The garden is his/hers/its
  • Puodho en marwa → The garden is ours
  • Puodho en maru → The garden is yours (plural)
  • Puodho en margi → The garden is theirs

Important Notes:

  • Gender Neutrality: The third-person singular form (-ne or -e) is gender-neutral.
  • Flexibility: Choose between suffixes (e.g., nyathi-ne) or "mar" + pronoun (e.g., marwa) depending on clarity and emphasis.
  • Context: Some suffixes (e.g., -wa) appear in both singular and plural forms. Context and verb agreement help distinguish them.
