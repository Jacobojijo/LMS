3.6 Verbs (Part 02)

3.6.1 Transitive Verbs

In Dholuo, transitive verbs are those that take a direct object, indicating the action of the verb is directed towards something or someone. Most Dholuo verbs can be both transitive and intransitive, with some verbs marked for antipassivity by internal changes or tone. Dholuo also uses verbal extensions like the applicative to change intransitive verbs into transitive ones.

Key points about transitive verbs in Dholuo:

  • Direct Object:
    Transitive verbs require a direct object to complete their meaning, indicating the recipient of the action.
  • Verb-Initial Structure:
    Dholuo follows a verb-initial structure, with the verb typically appearing at the beginning of the sentence.
  • Pronoun Suffixes:
    Many transitive verbs in Dholuo have pronominal subject suffixes, indicating the subject of the verb.
  • Antipassivity:
    Dholuo verbs can also be marked for antipassivity, which transforms a transitive verb into an intransitive verb.
  • Verbal Extensions:
    The applicative and locative extensions can increase the number of arguments within a verbal matrix, potentially changing an intransitive verb into a transitive one.

Examples

The cat chased the mouse. (The cat chased what? - The mouse.)
      Paka ne olawo oyieyo. (Paka nolawo ang'o? - Oyieyo.)

She read a book. (She read what? - A book.)
      Ne osomo buk moro. (Ne osomo ang'o? - Buk.)

He ate the pizza. (He ate what? - The pizza.)
     Ne ochamo pizza. (Ne ochamo ang'o? - Pizza.)

We bought a car. (We bought what? - A car.)
     Ne wadhi ng'iewo mtoka. (Ne wanyiewo ang'o? - Mtoka.)

3.6.1.1 The Subjective Pronoun

1. Introduction to Subjective Pronouns in Dholuo

  • Definition: Subjective pronouns in Dholuo indicate the doer of the action in a sentence.
  • Function: They are always the agent (subject) of the verb, performing the action.
  • Position in Sentence: The subject typically appears before the verb in standard sentence construction.
  • Distinction from Objects:
    • The subject carries out the action.
    • The object receives the action.

Example with Intransitive Verb:
Wa-wer → "We sing"
Wa (we) = subject
Wer (sing) = intransitive verb

2. Subject Pronoun Forms in Dholuo

Person Pronoun Meaning
1st person singular a I
2nd person singular i you
3rd person singular o he/she/it
1st person plural wa we
2nd person plural u you (pl)
3rd person plural gi they

3. Verb Structure with Auxiliary "To Be"

Construction Pattern:
Subject Pronoun + Auxiliary Verb + Main Verb + Object

Example:
Olum goyo bul → "He is beating the drum"
Olum = subject noun (Olum)
Goyo = transitive verb (to beat)
Bul = object (drum)

4. Transitive Verb Conjugation in Dholuo: Case Study of "Goyo" (to beat)

4.1 Verb Stem and Morphological Structure

  • Verb Stem: -goyo- = to beat
  • Transitive verbs are conjugated on both sides:
    • Prefix: indicates the subject (doer)
    • Suffix: indicates the object (receiver)

4.2 Subject and Object Integration in Verb Form

  • The subject prefix is attached to the verb root at the beginning.
  • The object suffix is added to the verb root at the end.
  • The resulting verb is agglutinative—a single word encodes subject, action, and object.

4.3 Conjugation Examples (Subject + Verb + Object):

Sentence Structure Gloss Translation
Agoye A-go-ye I-beat-him/her I beat him/her
Ogoye O-go-ye He-beat-him/her He has beaten him/her
Olum goye Olum + go + ye Olum + beat + him/her Olum is beating him/her

Note: In Olum goye, "Olum" is the subject noun, and goye is the verb-object combination.

5. Summary: Conjugation Principles for Transitive Verbs in Dholuo

  • Prefix = Subject Marker
    Denotes the person/agent performing the action
  • Suffix = Object Marker
    Denotes the person/thing receiving the action
  • Verb Root stays central and consistent
  • Conjugated verbs form a single word, integrating both subject and object
  • Verb Morphology in Dholuo reflects a highly synthetic/agglutinative structure, efficiently encoding grammatical relations

6. Implications for Language Learning and Structure

Understanding subjective pronouns is essential for:

  • Mastering verb conjugations
  • Accurate sentence formation

Knowledge of prefix-suffix dynamics enhances:

  • Reading fluency
  • Speaking precision

This structure reflects the deep integration of grammar and meaning in Dholuo verbal morphology.
