3.2 Review & Practice

3.2.1 Summary of Adjectives

1. Morphosyntactic Structure of Dholuo Adjectives

Function: Modify nouns to express qualities, states, or attributes.

Structural Formation:

Noun + ma- + Adjective Root

E.g., Kom ma-kwar = "Chair that is red"

Prefix "ma-":

  • Acts as a linking particle meaning "that is/which is".
  • Converts descriptor roots into reduced relative clauses, anchoring the adjective to the noun.

2. Morphological Marker of Adjectives

Key Affix: Prefix "ma-"

  • Universally used to form adjectives (except a few lexical exceptions).
  • Function: Binds descriptive semantics to nominal reference.

Examples of Root Transformation:

  • duong' → maduong' ("big")
  • ber → maber ("good")
  • rach → marach ("bad")
  • pek → mapek ("heavy")

3. Semantic Classification of Adjectives

A. Physical Appearance & Size

  • maduong' – big
  • matin / manok – small/few
  • mabor – tall
  • machiek – short
  • malach – wide
  • madiny – narrow

B. Color

  • machar – white
  • marateng' – black
  • masilwal – brown (male)
  • malando – brown (female)
  • madichol – black (female)

C. Temperature & Taste

  • maliet – hot
  • mang'ich – cold
  • mamit – sweet
  • makech – bitter

D. Weight & Quantity

  • mapek – heavy
  • mayot – light (not heavy)
  • mang'en – many
  • matin / manok – few/little

E. Quality & Character

  • maber – good, morally upright, attractive
  • marach – bad, ugly, immoral
  • mool – tired
  • marieny – bright, intelligent
  • marapudo – slim (female)
  • marandere – slender (male)

4. Formation of Adjectives from Verbs (Adjective Clauses)

Adjectives may derive from verbs, indicating aspectual nuance:

  • ma + verb root → ongoing/habitual state (present participle)
    • mawer – "who is singing"
    • matho – "which is dying"
  • ma + o-verb → completed state (past participle)
    • molosi – "already made"
    • motho – "which has died"

Temporal Distinctions:

matho ≠ motho; represents difference between process and result.

5. Examples of Adjectives in Natural Syntax (Ranyisi)

  • Nyuka maliet – hot porridge
  • Yien mabor – tall tree
  • Dhano marach – bad person
  • Ji mang'en – many people
  • Roya matin – small calf
  • Diel maodhero – thin goat
  • Paro marach ahinya – very bad thought

6. Adjectives of Quality in Dholuo

6.1 Maber / Maber Ahinya

Gloss: good, beautiful, morally upright

Semantics: Conveys aesthetic, ethical, and social approval.

Example: Nyako maber – "Beautiful girl"

6.2 Marach / Marach Ahinya

Gloss: bad, loathsome, immoral

Example: Dhano marach – "A bad person"

6.3 Manumu

Gloss: uncooked, unripe

Note: Irregular; does not follow the ma- prefix pattern.

Use: Describes incomplete developmental or culinary states.

Example: Rech manumu – "Uncooked fish"

7. Adjectives of Quantity in Dholuo

7.1 Maduong'

Gloss: big

Function: Indicates size or metaphorical importance

Example: Ng'ato maduong' – "Important person"

7.2 Mang'en

Gloss: many

Usage: Plural quantifier for count nouns

Example: Kitepe mang'en – "Many books"

7.3 Matin / Manok

Gloss: few, little

Irregularity: Manok does not follow standard ma- prefixation

Example: Chak manok – "A little milk"

7.4 Maoromo → Moromo

Gloss: enough

Usage: Sufficiency; ma- often dropped in speech

Example: Pi moromo – "Enough water"

7.5 Mathoth

Gloss: numerous, abundant

Semantic Strength: Exceeds mang'en in magnitude

Example: Ji mathoth – "A multitude of people"

7.6 Maogundho → Mogundho

Gloss: plenty

Cultural Nuance: Suggests ample provision or wealth

Example: Chak mogundho – "Plenty of milk"

8. Interrogative Quantifiers in Dholuo

8.1 Adi?

Gloss: How many?

Usage: Quantitative inquiry

Example: Kitepe adi? – "How many books?"

8.2 Oromo nade?

Gloss: How big?

Usage: Measures size or magnitude

Example: Kom oromo nade? – "How big is the chair?"

9. Linguistic and Cultural Insights

  • Descriptive Accuracy: ma- structures operate as relative clauses, anchoring adjectives to their referents.
  • Cultural Weight: Adjectives such as maber and marach carry moral, aesthetic, and evaluative implications.
  • Lexical Exceptions: Adjectives like manumu bypass standard prefix rules.
  • Aspectual Semantics: Verb-derived adjectives reflect tense/aspectual distinctions, supporting rich temporal reference within descriptions.
  • Communicative Clarity: Dholuo avoids "bare adjectives"; descriptors must be grammatically and contextually anchored.

I. Formation and Usage of Color Adjectives

A. Morphological Structure

  • Prefixation of "ma-" to color roots is the primary morphological process for deriving adjectives.
  • The resulting form functions as a descriptive modifier within the noun phrase.
  • Occasionally analyzable as "ma-" + root color, or "mar-" + color root due to phonological elision.

B. Representative Color Adjectives

  • Makwar (Red): From rakwar
    Example: Kom marakwar ("Red chair")
  • Marateng' (Black): From rateng'
    Example: Law marateng' ("Black cloth")
  • Marachar (White): From rachar
    Example: Ng'ato marachar ("A white person")
  • Marambulu (Blue): From rambulu
    Example: Kom marambulu ("Blue chair")
  • Maratong' (Yellow): From ratong' gweno ("egg yolk color")
    Example: Law maratong' ("Yellow garment")
  • Maralum (Green): From ralum / majan
    Example: Yien maralum ("Green tree")
  • Marapir (Indigo): From rapir
    Example: Law marapir ("Indigo clothing")
  • Maralik (Purple): From ralik
    Example: Buk maralik ("Purple books")
  • Maradier (Black and White): From radier
    Example: Diel maradier ("Black and white goat")
  • Marabuor (Brown): From rabuor
    Example: Nyako marabuor ("Brown-skinned girl")

C. Semantic Considerations

  • Color terms in Dholuo frequently reflect cultural symbolism (e.g., rachar for purity).
  • Some derive from environmental or material referents (e.g., ratong' gweno = yolk).
  • Dual-tone and patterned colors (e.g., radier) represent complex perceptual categories.

II. Pluralization of Adjectives in Dholuo

A. General Rule of Agreement

  • Adjectives must morphologically agree with the number of the noun they modify.
  • Plural adjectives generally adopt a -o suffix, producing systematic variations.

B. Morphological Patterns of Pluralization

Singular Adjective Plural Adjective Gloss
maber mabeyo good/beautiful
marach maricho bad
maduong' madongo big
matin matindo small/few
mabor maboyo tall/long
machiek machieko short
marateng' maratenge black
marachar marachere white
— mang'en many (plural-only)

C. Syntactic Concord

Both noun and adjective change form to reflect plurality:

  • Kom maduong' ("A big chair") → Kombe madongo ("Big chairs")
  • Nyako maber ("A beautiful girl") → Nyiri mabeyo ("Beautiful girls")

D. Invariant Adjectives

  • Some adjectives do not undergo inflection in the plural.
  • Typically belong to the color, taste, or emotion semantic fields.

Examples:

  • Ndiga makuar → Ndigni makuar ("Red bicycle(s)")
  • Rabolo mamit → Rabolni mamit ("Sweet banana(s)")
  • Japuonj makwiny → Jopuonj makwiny ("Cruel teacher(s)")

III. Semantic Categorization of Adjectival Inflection

A. Adjectives That Inflect

  • Align with standard grammatical agreement in plural constructions.
  • Often describe qualitative or measurable attributes:
    • maber, matin, marachar, machiek

B. Adjectives That Do Not Inflect

  • Typically, lexicalized or fossilized forms.
  • Include color terms, tastes, and affective descriptors:
    • makuar (red), mamit (sweet), makwiny (cruel)

IV. Structural and Functional Insights

A. Syntactic Regularity

  • Pluralization reinforces grammatical consistency and euphony.
  • Adjective–noun concord is obligatory, particularly in formal registers.

B. Morphological Productivity

  • Plural forms illustrate rich inflectional morphology, combining prefixation and suffixation strategies.

C. Cultural and Lexical Dimensions

  • Certain color adjectives reflect cultural or environmental realities (e.g., ratong' gweno).
  • Invariant forms often carry symbolic, sensorial, or emotive weight, resisting grammatical transformation.

V. Broader Linguistic Implications

  • The plural agreement system aligns Dholuo with broader Niger-Congo morphosyntactic typologies.
  • It exemplifies the interplay between grammatical structure and lexical meaning, highlighting the semantic depth of color and descriptive terms.
  • The resistance of some adjectives to pluralization underscores the lexicalization of culturally embedded expressions.
