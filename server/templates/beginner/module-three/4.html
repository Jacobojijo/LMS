3.1.4 Adjectives & their Plural

Plural Adjectives in Dholuo: A Morphosyntactic Analysis

In Dholuo, adjectives exhibit inflectional morphology that agrees with the number of the noun they modify. Singular and plural forms of adjectives must align with their respective noun forms in both syntax and morphology. Below is a detailed analysis of plural adjective formation and use:

1. General Rule of Pluralization

  • Dholuo adjectives generally change form when modifying plural nouns.
  • Most plural adjectives end in -o (e.g., maber → mabeyo, maduong' → madongo).
  • Both the noun and the adjective must be inflected for plurality.

2. Morphological Transformation

Singular Form Plural Form Meaning
maber mabeyo good, beautiful
marach maricho bad
maduong' madongo big
matin matindo small, few
mabor maboyo tall, long
machiek machieko short
marateng' maratenge black
marachar marachere white
(none) mang'eny many (plural only form)

3. Concordial Agreement in Plural Forms

When a noun becomes plural, its modifying adjective must also change into the plural form for grammatical and semantic agreement.

Examples:

  • Kom maduong' — "A big chair" → Kombe madongo — "Big chairs"
  • Nyako maber — "A beautiful girl" → Nyiri mabeyo — "Beautiful girls"
  • Puga matin — "A small gourd" → Puke matindo — "Small gourds"

4. Adjectives That Do Not Change in the Plural

Certain adjectives in Dholuo remain invariant regardless of the singular or plural status of the noun they describe. This is typically the case with adjectives derived from color roots and certain descriptive qualities.

Examples:

  • Ndiga makuar — "A red bicycle" → Ndigni makuar — "Red bicycles" (makuar = red; unchanged)
  • Japuonj makwiny — "A cruel teacher" → Jopuonj makwiny — "Cruel teachers" (makwiny = wild/cruel; unchanged)
  • Rabolo mamit — "Sweet banana" → Rabolni mamit — "Sweet bananas" (mamit = sweet; unchanged)

5. Semantic Categories of Adjective Agreement

A. Adjectives That Inflect for Plurality

These adjectives follow standard agreement patterns:

  • maber → mabeyo
  • marach → maricho
  • matin → matindo
  • mabor → maboyo
  • machiek → machieko
  • marachar → marachere

B. Adjectives That Do Not Inflect

These adjectives remain invariant and typically describe:

  • Coloration (especially in fossilized or culturally rooted forms): makuar
  • Taste/Emotion: mamit (sweet), makech (bitter), makwiny (cruel)

6. Notes on Usage and Syntax

  • Adjective–noun agreement is mandatory in standard Dholuo grammar, especially in formal or written usage.
  • This rule reinforces number agreement, clarity, and euphony within noun phrases.
  • The noun changes morphologically (e.g., kom → kombe, nyako → nyiri) alongside its adjective.

8. Linguistic Implication

  • The system of plural adjective agreement in Dholuo mirrors the syntactic richness found in many Niger-Congo languages.
  • It reveals a productive morphological strategy where both prefixation and suffixation are used to maintain grammatical consistency.
  • The invariant forms demonstrate a lexicalized resistance to inflection, often tied to cultural, emotional, or sensory descriptors.
