3.5 Review & Practice

3.5.1 Summary of Adjectives & Verbs

Introduction to Comparatives in Dholuo

Comparative constructions in Dholuo are used to express:

  • Relative comparison (e.g., taller, sweeter)
  • Equality comparison (e.g., as tall as, as sweet as)

Core comparison strategies:

  • Direct comparative forms (shortened adjective forms)
  • "machal kod" for similarity comparisons (similar to)
  • "kaka" for equality expressions (as...as)

Comparative Forms of Adjectives

Morphological changes occur in forming comparatives:

  • Often involves syllabic reduction from the original adjective:
    • mabor → bor (tall → taller)
    • mamit → mit (sweet → sweeter)
    • marach → rach (bad → worse)
    • maber → ber (good → better)
    • mariek → riek (wise → wiser)

Standard Comparative Construction:

Structure: [Subject A] [comparative adjective] moloyo [Subject B]

moloyo or ma oloyo means "than"

E.g.: Mae bor moloyo macha → "This is taller than that one."

Special note on 'bor' (tall/long):

  • Does not undergo morphological change in comparative use.
  • Its comparative force depends on structure, not form.

Equality Comparisons in Dholuo

Two main forms used:

  • "machal kod" → meaning "similar to"
  • "kaka" → meaning "as...as"

Standard constructions:

  • ma [adjective] machal kod [comparison entity]
  • ma [adjective] kaka [comparison entity]

Examples:

Mae mit kaka macha → "This is as sweet as that one."

Ma riek machal kod macha → "This is as wise as that one."

"kaka" functions as a flexible syntactic tool for equality in quality across entities.

Superlative Constructions in Dholuo

Formed with: [adjective] moloyo te → meaning "more than all" or "the most"

moloyo = more than

te = all

Structure: [Subject] [adjective] moloyo te

E.g.: Chiege riek moloyo te → "His wife is the most intelligent."

Examples of Superlative Forms:

  • maber moloyo te → the best
  • mariek moloyo te → the most intelligent
  • mamit moloyo te → the sweetest
  • mabor moloyo te → the tallest / longest

Intensifiers: Expressing High Degree or Extremes

Intensifiers are context-dependent phrases used to express extreme quality:

  • Rachar thiriri → very white
  • Kwar ha → very red
  • Rateng' ti → very black/dark
  • Ngich thi → very cold
  • Riek ndii → very clever

Illustrative Usage:

Ng'atno riek ndii → "He is very clever."

Chieng' liet ha! → "The sun is very hot!"

Cultural-Linguistic Insights

Comparative expression in Dholuo extends beyond physical traits to include:

  • Moral attributes (e.g., marach – bad, maber – good)
  • Intellectual traits (e.g., mariek – wise, riek ndii – very clever)
  • Sensory/emotional descriptors (e.g., mamit – sweet)

The superlative form ("moloyo te") reflects a communal standard of excellence, where moral and intellectual virtues are emphasized alongside physical qualities.

These structures reveal deep cultural values rooted in:

  • Character and wisdom
  • Social respectability
  • Community-based evaluation of individual merit

1. Introduction to Dholuo Verbs

  • Most Dholuo verbs end in -o (e.g., chiemo "to eat").
  • Exception: dhi ("to go") is a shortened form of dhiyo.
  • Verbs exhibit rich morphological structures, accommodating various verbal extensions:
    • Applicative (instrument/agent)
    • Locative (place/direction)
    • Reflexive (self-directed action)
    • Reciprocal (mutual action)
    • Stative (state/condition)

2. Infinitive Verbs in Dholuo

  • Characteristically end in -o or -yo.
  • Used to express actions or states without tense or subject.
  • Common Examples:

    timo (to do), kayo (to keep), bedo (to sit), paro (to think), somo (to study).

3. Indicative Mood and Sentence Structure

  • Used for declarative statements or factual assertions.
  • Structure: [Subject Pronoun] + [Verb] + [Object/Complement]

    • E.g., An e jatend piny → "I am the president"

4. Irregular Verb: "To Be" (Copular N)

  • Highly irregular: does not follow -o/-yo pattern.
  • Represented by N, modified with personal pronoun prefixes:
    • An e..., In e..., En e... (singular)
    • Wan n..., Un n..., Gin n... (plural)
  • Used to state identity, role, or classification.

Subject Pronouns and Copular Verb "To Be" (N):

5. Expressing Presence: Verb "Tie"

  • Denotes being present or existing in a location.
  • Pronoun-based conjugation:
    • antie (I am here), intie (you are here), entie (he/she is here), etc.
  • Often used with locative adverbials: ka (here), ko (there)

    • Examples:

    An ka → I am here

    Wantie ka → We are here

    Gin ka → They are here

6. Presence with Overt Subject: "Nitie"

  • Used when subject is named or explicit, not a pronoun.

    • Chiemo nitie? → Is there food?

    Paul nitie. → Paul is present.

7. Negation of Presence: "Onge"

  • Used to express absence or non-existence.

    • Chiemo onge → There is no food

    Oonge → He/She is not present

8. Expression of Possession: "To Have"

  • No direct equivalent of English "have"
  • Expressed using:
    • [Pronoun] + gi / kod (with)
    • [Overt Subject] + ni + gi

    • Examples with Pronouns:

    An gi chiemo → I have food

    En gi guok → He has a dog

    Gin gi nyithindo → They have children

    An kod sufuria → I have a cooking pot

    Examples with Overt Subject:

    Nyieka nigi nyithindo → My co-wife has children

10. Cultural-Linguistic Insights

  • The verbs tie, nitie, and onge are foundational for expressing existence, identity, and absence.
  • Expressions like Wan Jo Luo (We are Luos) assert ethnic and communal identity.
  • Possession indicates social roles, responsibilities, and relationships.
  • These structures reflect the social fabric of Luo culture, where roles, belonging, and presence are linguistically emphasized.
