3.4 Verbs (Part 01)

3.4.1 Introduction

In Dholuo, verbs typically end in "-o", like "chiemo" (to eat). However, "dhi" (to go) is an exception, which is a shortened form of "dhiyo". Dholuo verbs often have a complex morphology with various verbal extensions like applicative, locative, reflexive, reciprocal, and stative, which influence their meaning and usage.

Verb Infinitives:

Dholuo verbs usually end in "-o," indicating the infinitive form.

Examples: "ndiko" (to write), "uso" (to sell), "somo" (to study), "biro" (to come), "hero" (to like).

Verb Phrase Structure:

Dholuo verb phrases can be quite complex, incorporating verbs, adjectives, nouns, prepositions, and adverbs.

For instance, "Riembo gari manyien go kinyi e lam matek" translates to "Driving a new vehicle in the morning on a tarmac road very fast," showcasing the various constituents that can be included in a Dholuo verb phrase.

Verbal Extensions:

Dholuo verbs can be modified with various extensions, which change their meaning and use.

These extensions include:

  • Applicative: Indicates the agent or instrument involved in an action.
  • Locative: Indicates location or direction.
  • Reflexive: Indicates the action is performed on the subject itself.
  • Reciprocal: Indicates an action performed between two or more people.
  • Stative: Indicates a state or condition.

3.4.2 Infinitive verbs

1. Introduction to the Indicative Mood in Dholuo

The indicative mood is used to state facts, describe reality, or give declarative information.

In Dholuo, the indicative mood often pairs personal pronouns with the verb "to be" (N) or other infinitive verbs to make clear statements.

2. Infinitive Verbs in Dholuo

Most infinitive verbs in Dholuo end in "-o" or "-yo".

These are the base forms of verbs, used for actions or states without indicating tense or subject.

Example endings:

  • timo – to do
  • kayo – to keep
  • bedo – to sit/stay
  • paro – to think

3. Exception: The Verb "To Be" (N)

The verb "to be" in Dholuo is highly irregular.

  • It does not follow the "-o" or "-yo" ending pattern of other infinitive verbs.
  • It is marked simply by the consonant "N", with a personal pronoun prefix that varies depending on the subject.
  • Despite changes in the prefix, "N" remains constant in all present tense forms.

4. Present Tense Conjugation of "To Be" (N)

The structure is:

[Subject Pronoun] + [e/N] + [Object]

Person Pronoun Verb "to be" Example Phrase Translation
First person sg. An e An e jatend piny I am the president
Second person sg. In e In e japuonj You are the teacher
Third person sg. En e En e japuonj He/She is the teacher
First person pl. Wan n Wan Jo Luo We are Luos
Second person pl. Un n Un Jo Kenya You (pl.) are Kenyans
Third person pl. Gin n Gin jo Nairobi They are people of Nairobi

Note: The "e" or "n" serves as the copula (linking verb).

5. Subject Pronouns and Their Verb Markers

In Dholuo, personal pronouns influence the form of the verb, especially the verb "to be" (N).

Subject Pronoun Person Verb Prefix Notes

I- You (2nd sg.) In Used with e (In e...)
E- He/She/It (3rd sg.) En Used with e (En e...)
Wa- We (1st pl.) Wan Used with n (Wan Jo Luo)
U- You (2nd pl.) Un Used with n (Un Jo Kenya)

6. Structure of Simple Indicative Sentences

Pattern:

[Subject] + [Verb "to be"] + [Predicate/Object]

Examples:

  • An e jatend piny → I am the president
  • In e japuonj → You are the teacher
  • En e japuonj → He is the teacher
  • Wan Jo Luo → We are Luos
  • Un Jo Kenya → You (pl.) are Kenyans
  • Gin jo Nairobi → They are people of Nairobi

7. Cultural-Linguistic Insight

  • In Dholuo, identity statements (e.g., "I am a teacher") are fundamental in introducing oneself or others.
  • These constructions using "to be" (N) express roles, professions, ethnicities, and group belonging.
    Example: Wan Jo Luo → We are Luos (ethnic identity)
  • The verb "to be" functions as a core element in self-representation and social description.
