3.8.6 Future Tense & Future Continuous Tense

1. General Overview of Future Tense in Dholuo

The future tense in Dholuo is constructed primarily using auxiliary verbs that precede the main verb.

The most common auxiliary used to express the immediate or definite future is "bi", conjugated as "biro" ("will come").

The main verb in the construction remains in the infinitive and is not conjugated.

This structure semantically aligns with the English "will" or "shall" to express future intention, certainty, or prediction.

2. Primary Structure of Immediate Future Tense

Construction Pattern:

Subject prefix + biro + verb (infinitive form)

Infinitive Verb: Does not change based on subject or tense.

"Biro" Auxiliary: This is the only element that undergoes subject agreement (conjugation).

2.1 Examples Using "Chiemo" (to eat)

English Dholuo Contracted Form
I will eat abiro chiemo abochiemo
You will eat ibiro chiemo ibochiemo
He/She will eat obiro chiemo obochiemo
We will eat wabiro chiemo wabochiemo
You (pl.) will eat ubiro chiemo ubochiemo
They will eat gibiro chiemo gibochiemo

3. Morphological Structure of "Biro" Construction

"Biro" is derived from the root "bi" (to come).

Conjugates per subject:

a- (I), i- (you sg.), o- (he/she/it), wa- (we), u- (you pl.), gi- (they)

Followed directly by the infinitive verb:

E.g., abiro chiemo, ibiro dhi, gibiro neno.

4. The "Bo-" Contraction Form

Contraction of "biro" with the verb is common in casual or spoken Dholuo.

Merges the subject prefix + bo (shortened biro) + verb:

abochiemo → "I'll eat"

ibochiemo → "You'll eat"

These forms are phonologically fused but retain full syntactic function.

5. Future of "To Be": Using "Bedo"

"Bedo" = to be, and remains uninflected in future constructions.

Future form: Subject prefix + biro + bedo

Abiro bedo gi nyithindo ariyo → "I will have (be with) two children."

Literal: "I come to be with children two."

6. Alternate Expressions of Future

These express various degrees of futurity, using different temporal markers before the verb.

6.1 Immediate or Imminent Future

Abiro dok → "I'm about to return"

Literally: "I come to return"

6.2 Near Future (Later Today)

Ang'nadogi → "Later today I will return"

Derived from: Wang' ni adogi ("Today that I return")

6.3 Tomorrow

Kiny nadogi → "Tomorrow I'll return"

From: Kiny ni adogi ("Tomorrow that I return")

6.4 Indefinite Future

Chieng' nadogi → "Sometime in the future I'll return"

Derived: Chieng' ni adogi

7. Future Continuous Tense

Formed using the structure: Biro + ga + verb

Expresses ongoing future actions (parallel to English "will be doing").

Abiro ga dhi lime → "I will be going to visit him/her"

Gibiro ga chiemo maber → "They will be eating well"

8. Expressing Obligation: "Nyaka" Construction

"Nyaka" = must / should / have to

Used to mandate action or express moral, social, or grammatical necessity.

Structure:

Nyaka + subject prefix + verb

Examples:

Nyaka iwuo → "You must/should speak"

Nyaka ichiem → "You must eat"

Nyaka wanene → "We must see him"

Note:

This construction carries strong obligation (akin to "must") but is also used for softened directives (akin to "should") depending on tone and context.

9. Syntactic Observations

  • The verb following "biro" or "bo" is always in the infinitive, and not marked for subject agreement.
  • The auxiliary verb "biro" or "nyaka" carries all agreement and tense information.
  • Temporal adverbials like chieng', kiny, and wang' are often used to specify the time frame of the future action.

10. Pedagogical Notes for Learners

10.1 Learner Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Do not conjugate the main verb in the future tense; only conjugate the auxiliary "biro".
  • Avoid confusing contracted forms (e.g., obochiemo) with single-word verbs — recognize them as fusion of auxiliary + infinitive.
  • Understand that biro ≠ bi; the former is an auxiliary used for tense marking, the latter is simply "come".
