3.7 Review & Practice

3.7.1 Summary of Verbs

Transitive Verbs in Dholuo

1. Nature of Transitive Verbs in Dholuo

Transitive verbs require a direct object to complete their meaning.

These verbs direct an action toward an entity (person or object).

Most verbs in Dholuo can function as both transitive and intransitive, depending on syntactic context.

Antipassivity is used to shift focus from the object, effectively turning a transitive verb into an intransitive form.

Verbal extensions such as applicative and locative can add arguments, allowing intransitive verbs to become transitive.

2. Syntactic and Morphological Properties

Verb-Initial Syntax: Dholuo exhibits a verb-initial word order, although nouns and subjects may precede for emphasis or clarity.

Agglutinative Structure: Verb forms integrate subject (prefix) and object (suffix) into a single morphological unit.

Conjugation Principle:

  • Prefix marks the subject.
  • Suffix marks the object.
  • The verb root remains central and consistent.

Example (Verb "Goyo" – to beat):

Agoye = A-go-ye → "I have beaten him/her"

Ogoye = O-go-ye → "He has beaten him/her"

Olum goye = Subject noun + verb-object compound → "Olum is beating him/her"

3. Subjective Pronouns in Dholuo

Denote the agent (doer) of the action.

Always appear as prefixes attached to the verb root.

Function independently in non-conjugated contexts or as auxiliaries.

Forms:

a (I), i (you sg), o (he/she/it), wa (we), u (you pl), gi (they)

Example (Intransitive Verb):

Wa-wer → "We sing"

4. Objective Pronouns in Dholuo

Indicate the recipient of an action.

Always appear as suffixes attached to the verb root.

Format: Subject Prefix + Verb Root + Object Suffix

Forms:

-a (me), -i (you sg), -e (him/her/it), -wa (us), -un (you pl), -gi (them)

Example:

Puonj-e → "Teach him/her/it"

5. Conjugation Case Studies

Verb "Nego" – to kill:

Ginegowa → "They have killed us"

Onegi → "He has killed them"

Inega → "You have killed me"

Verb "Hero" – to love:

Aheri → "I love you"

Giherou → "They love you (pl)"

Verb "Nyuomo" – to marry:

Anyuomi → "I am marrying you"

Gikendore → "They are married"

Okende → "He married her" (Note: Kendo can be polysemous)

6. Syntax of Transitive Verb Conjugation

Canonical word order: Subject Pronoun + Verb Root + Object Pronoun

Verbs are fully fused (single unit encodes full clause: subject, verb, and object).

Reflects synthetic morphological typology with high grammatical density.

7. Pedagogical and Linguistic Implications

Mastery of subjective and objective pronouns is essential for:

  • Accurate verb conjugation
  • Sentence clarity and interpretation
  • Listening comprehension and fluent communication

The prefix-suffix verb morphology enhances:

  • Linguistic efficiency
  • Compact encoding of syntactic roles
  • Deep integration of grammar with meaning

Intransitive Verbs in Dholuo

Definition:

Intransitive verbs do not take a direct object; the action terminates with the subject itself.

Characteristics:

  • Stand independently without requiring another noun.
  • Emphasize the subject's performance of an action without transferring the action to an object.

Examples of Common Intransitive Verbs:

Dholuo Verb English Translation
Tiyo To work
Wer To sing
Nindo To sleep
Miel To dance
Ringo To run
Bet To sit

Illustrative Sentences:

Abet e kom – I sit on the chair.

Gitiyo matek – They work hard.

2. The Verb "To Have" in Dholuo

A. Possession with "gi" (with)

Structural Construction:

Expressed as: Personal Pronoun + gi (meaning "with")

Indicates possession, analogous to "to have" in English.

Conjugation Table:

Pronoun Dholuo Form English Translation
I an gi I have
You (sg.) in gi You have
He/She en gi He/She has
We wan gi We have
You (pl.) un gi You (pl.) have
They gin gi They have

Examples:

An gi kom – I have a chair.

Gin gi lowo – They have land.

En gi abila – He/She has a hut.

Wan gi guok – We have a dog.

Important Note:

An kod guok literally means I and the dog, and is not appropriate for expressing possession.

Use gi construction for correct possessive statements.

3. The Verb "To Have" as an Auxiliary Verb – se

A. Imperfect Past Tense Use

Functionality:

Denotes completion of an action in the recent past (perfect aspect).

Conveys the equivalent of the English construction "have + past participle".

Form and Use:

Se is incorporated as a prefix in compound verbs.

The prefix is sensitive to subject and tense agreement.

Examples:

Dholuo Form English Translation
Asekadho penj I have passed the exam
Asedhi I have gone
Asechiemo I have eaten
Osenindo He/She has slept
Wase budho We have stayed
Usedhi Kisumu You have gone to Kisumu
Osedhi katedo She is married (gone to cook)
Osetho He/She/It has died

4. Reflexive Verbs in Dholuo

A. Morphological Structure

Formed by attaching the suffix -re to the base infinitive verb.

Denotes self-directed actions or mutual actions among subjects.

B. Infinitive vs Reflexive Form Examples:

Base Verb Meaning Reflexive Form Reflexive Meaning
Tedo To cook Tedore To cook itself
Neno To see Nenore To see oneself/each other
Puonjo To teach Puonjore To learn (teach oneself)
Kano To keep Kanore To be kept

C. Conjugation Table of Puonjore (to learn):

Subject Dholuo Form English Translation
I Apuonjora I am learning (myself)
You (sg.) Ipuonjori You are learning
He/She Opuonjore He/She is learning
We Wapuonjore We are learning
You (pl.) Upuonjoru You (pl.) are learning
They Gipuonjore They are learning

5. Reflexive Verbs as Nouns – -ruok Nominalization

The suffix -ruok nominalizes reflexive verbs into abstract nouns or verbal nouns.

Used to express states or concepts derived from reflexive action.

Examples:

Action Dholuo English Translation
Washing oneself Luokruok ber Washing oneself is good
Not washing Ok odwar luokore He does not want to wash himself
Fighting each other Goruok rach Fighting one another is bad
Refusing to fight Ok gidwar gore They do not want to fight each other

6. Additional Linguistic Notes

Cultural Semantics:

The verb nyuomo (to marry) is culturally male-exclusive in Dholuo usage; only men are grammatically positioned as the marrying subject.

Absence of Object Markers:

Dholuo does not use separate object pronouns.

Subject prefixes (e.g., an, in, en) imply both the actor and the action's directionality.

Grammatical Weight of Subject Prefixes:

Subject prefixes serve as both pronouns and functional markers in verb constructions.

They carry tense, aspect, and subject agreement features.

Morphological Compactness:

Possessive constructions such as an gi kom are functionally and orthographically treated as units.

These are syntactically fused and should be interpreted holistically.
