3.8.7 Other Tenses

1. The "Should" Construction

  • Function: Expresses moral or expected obligation in the present or near future.
  • Structure: Onego (should) + Subject Prefix + Subjunctive Verb (often without final -o)
  • Subjunctive Marking: Often involves removal of the final -o in the verb stem.
Examples:
  • Onego andik → I should write
  • Onegandik (Contracted)
  • Onego wandik → We should write
  • Onegowandik (Contracted)

2. The "May" Construction (Possibility/Permission)

  • Function: Expresses possibility, permission, or potential action.
  • Structure: Dipop (may) + Subject Prefix + Full Verb
  • Note: No subjunctive is applied here; the verb retains its full form including final -o.
Examples:
  • Dipop andiko → I may write
  • Dipopandiko (Contracted)
  • Dipop wandiko → We may write
  • Dipopwandiko (Contracted)

3. The "Would" Construction (Hypothetical or Conditional Past)

  • Function: Used to express hypothetical or conditional intentions/actions.
  • Structure: De (would) + Subject Prefix + Verb (with final -o)
  • Contracted: The "De" merges with subject prefix.
Examples:
  • De andiko → I would write
  • Dandiko (Contracted)
  • De wandiko → We would write
  • Dewandiko (Contracted)

4. The "Let Me/Us" Imperative (Subjunctive Mood)

  • Function: Expresses a polite imperative or request, especially for oneself or a group including the speaker.
  • Structure: Subject Prefix + Verb (Subjunctive — typically loses final -o)
  • No auxiliary used.
Examples:
  • Andik! → Let me write!
  • Wandik! → Let us write!

5. The "Should Have" Construction (Past Obligation)

  • Function: Expresses an action that was expected or required in the past but did not occur.
  • Structure: Ne (past marker) + Onego (should) + Subject Prefix + Subjunctive Verb
  • Contracted: "Ne" + "Onego" + subject + verb merges into one unit.
Examples:
  • Ne onego andik → I should have written
  • Nonegandik (Contracted)
  • Ne onego wandik → We should have written
  • Nogenowandik (Contracted)

6. The "Had Done" Construction (Past Perfect)

  • Function: Expresses a completed action in the past before another past event.
  • Structure: Ne (past auxiliary) + Subject Perfective Marker + Verb
  • Subject Perfective Markers:
    • ase- (I had), wase- (we had), etc.
  • Contracted: Full clause is phonetically merged.
Examples:
  • Ne asendiko → I had written
  • Nasendiko (Contracted)
  • Ne wasendiko → We had written
  • Newasendiko (Contracted)

7. The Causative Construction ("To make someone do something")

  • Function: Expresses coercion, instruction, or influence over another person's actions.
  • Structure: Subject Prefix + ami / wami (make) + Object Prefix + Verb
  • Negation: "Ok" negates the entire causative clause.
Examples:
  • Ami ondiko → I make him write
  • Wami ondiko → We make him write
  • Wami indik → We make you write
  • Ok ami ondik → I don't make him write
  • Ok wami ondik → We don't make him write

8. Morphological Features Summary

  • Subjunctive mood often signaled by removal of final -o in the verb (e.g., andik instead of andiko).
  • Contracted forms are phonological compressions, not semantic shifts.
  • Modal auxiliaries like onego, dipop, de are the primary mood markers.
  • "Ne" marks past-tense or past perfect actions.

9. Syntactic Properties

  • Subject-verb agreement is marked by prefixes (a-, wa-, etc.), while the verb typically remains uninflected in modal constructions.
  • Causative verbs follow a Subject + ami/wami + Object + Verb model.
  • Contracted forms are common in both colloquial and fluent literary usage but require internal parsing to identify each component.

10. Pedagogical Recommendations

  • Subjunctive Practice: Learners should internalize the dropping of final -o for commands and obligations.
  • Contrastive Drills: Use side-by-side examples of full vs. contracted forms.
  • Mood Emphasis: Teach the subtle mood distinctions between "may," "should," "would," and their contracted/expanded versions.
  • Causative Mastery: Emphasize object marking in causative clauses (e.g., ondiko vs indik).
