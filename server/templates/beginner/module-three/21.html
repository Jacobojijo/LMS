3.9 Review & Practice

3.9.1 Summary of Tenses

1. Tense Overview

Dholuo verbs are categorized into past, present, and future, each with:

  • Simple
  • Continuous (Progressive)
  • Perfect
  • Perfect Continuous

Present tense (Kinde Masani) refers to:

  • Current actions (e.g., Olum chiemo – Olum is eating)
  • Habitual actions (e.g., Paul chiemoga gokinyi – Paul usually eats in the morning)

2. Verb Structure and Conjugation

Verbs retain infinitive endings: typically -o or -yo
(e.g., chiemo – to eat, kwayo – to graze)

Conjugation by prefixing subject markers to verb root:

Pronoun Prefix Example Translation
an a- achiemo I eat/am eating
in i- ichiemo You eat
en o- ochiemo He/She eats
wan wa- wachiemo We eat
un u- uchiemo You (pl.) eat
gin gi- gichiemo They eat

3. Overt Subject Rule

With proper nouns or explicit subjects, subject prefix is omitted:

  • Olum chiemo – Olum is eating (not achiemo)
  • Mama dhi kar thieth – Mum is going to hospital
  • Achieng' uso – Achieng' sells

4. Present Continuous vs. Simple Present

No morphological distinction:

  • Olum wuotho – Olum walks / is walking
  • Contextual cues determine aspectual interpretation (e.g., time expressions or discourse)

5. Habitual Aspect – Use of "-ga"

"-ga" suffix marks habitual/repeated actions:

  • Paul chiemoga gokinyi – Paul usually eats in the morning
  • Nyathi nindoga bang' chiemo – The child usually sleeps after eating
  • Morphological clarity reduces context dependency

6. Morphological & Syntactic Features

Agglutinative structure:

  • Subject + tense often embedded in a single verb form
    (e.g., achiemo = a- (I) + chiemo (eat))
  • Infinitives used both in overt-subject constructions and present tense conjugations

7. Pedagogical Insights

Learner focus:

  • Mastering subject prefixes
  • Using "-ga" for habitual clarity
  • Interpreting aspect through contextual clues
  • Challenge: Absence of explicit progressive marker requires contextual inference

1. Simple Past Tense (Kinde Machon)

Function: Marks completed past actions using the auxiliary "nene" (or abbreviated "ne").

Structure: Subject + ne/nene + Verb + Object

Examples:

  • Olum nene odhi kwath – Olum went to graze cattle
  • Nene achamo rech – I ate fish
  • Mama nene onudo nyuka ni jotich – Mother bought soda for the workers

Word Order Sensitivity: Pronoun and object positioning shifts meaning:

  • Nene oromo kode – He met him
  • Nene giromo kodgi – They met them

2. Contracted Past Forms

"ne" merges with subject prefixes for concise forms.

Examples:

  • nene andiko → nandiko – I wrote
  • ne ondiko → nondiko – He wrote

3. Immediate Past (Prefix "a-")

Used for just-completed actions.

Example: A apuro – I have just dug (now stopped)

4. Yesterday Past (Prefix "nyo-/nyoro-")

Indicates actions that occurred specifically yesterday.

Example: Nyoro natuo – I was sick yesterday

5. Extended Past Markers

Marker Meaning Example Translation
nyocha Two days ago Nyocha atuo I was sick two days ago
yande Recently Yande atuo I was sick recently
a- Just now A tuo I was just sick (now better)

6. Recent Past Perfective ("-se-" Infix)

Structure: Subject Prefix + -se- + Verb

Function: Denotes recently completed actions (Present Perfect equivalent).

Examples:

  • asechiemo – I have eaten
  • osechiemo – He/she has eaten

7. Past Continuous / Habitual (Kinde Manenedhi Nyime)

Structure: nene + Subject Prefix + Verb + -ga

Function: Repeated or ongoing past action.

Examples:

  • Nene achiemoga – I was eating
  • Nene asomoga Dholuo e ot – I used to study Dholuo at home
  • Ochieng' nene oheroga yuoro tum – Ochieng used to enjoy dances

8. Morphological Insights

  • "nene" = Auxiliary (unchanging)
  • Subject agreement still applies.
  • Agglutinative nature: Meaning encoded in particles/prefixes/suffixes.
  • Temporal interpretation is highly morphological.

Past Perfect Tense (Bedo Construction)

1. Definition & Function

  • Expresses actions completed before another past event.
  • Used for backgrounding and sequencing in narratives.

2. Morphological Structure

Formula: nene + subject prefix + se + bedo

nene = Past marker, -se- = Perfective aspect, bedo = Stable participle "to have been"

3. Subject Concord Patterns

English Dholuo
I had been Nene asebedo
You had been Nene isebedo
He/She had been Nene osebedo
We had been Nene wasebedo
You (pl.) had been Nene usebedo
They had been Nene gisebedo

4. Semantic Features

  • Marks an action or state completed prior to a past reference point.
  • Often introduces contextual or background information in storytelling.

5. Examples

  • Nene asebedo ka ariti – I had been waiting for you
  • Nene osebedo ka koth chue – It had been raining
  • Nene gisebedo ka gilimore – They had been visiting one another
  • Achieng' nene osebedo ka lime – Achieng had been visiting her

6. Linguistic Characteristics

  • bedo is non-conjugatable, static in form.
  • -se- acts like English "have" – indicates completion.
  • nene fixes the action to the past.

7. Pedagogical Insights

  • Emphasize strict structure: nene + subject + se + bedo
  • Avoid misuse of bedo (no standalone conjugation).
  • Practice with temporal clauses (e.g., ka...) for narrative depth.
  • Differentiate:
    • osebedo (has been – present perfect)
    • nene osebedo (had been – past perfect)

A. Future Tense in Dholuo

General Overview

  • Future tense is formed using auxiliary verbs, primarily "bi" ("to come").
  • Most common future auxiliary: "biro" ("will come").
  • The main verb remains in the infinitive, uninflected.
  • Semantically equivalent to English "will" or "shall".

Immediate Future Structure

Formula: Subject Prefix + biro + Infinitive Verb

  • abiro chiemo → "I will eat"
  • ibiro chiemo → "You will eat"

Contracted Future Form

Spoken Dholuo contracts biro to bo:

  • abochiemo (I'll eat), obochiemo (He'll eat)

Conjugation of "Biro"

Subject prefixes: a- (I), i- (you sg.), o- (he/she), wa- (we), u- (you pl.), gi- (they)

e.g., gibiro chiemo → "They will eat"

Future "To Be" with "Bedo"

Bedo = to be (remains uninflected).

Structure: Subject Prefix + biro + bedo

Abiro bedo gi nyithindo ariyo → "I will have two children"

Degrees of Futurity

  • Immediate: Abiro dok → "I'm about to return"
  • Near Future: Ang'nadogi → "Later today I'll return"
  • Tomorrow: Kiny nadogi → "Tomorrow I'll return"
  • Indefinite: Chieng' nadogi → "Sometime I'll return"

Future Continuous Tense

Structure: Biro + ga + Verb

  • Abiro ga dhi lime → "I will be going to visit him/her"
  • Gibiro ga chiemo maber → "They will be eating well"

Obligation with "Nyaka"

Nyaka = must/should

  • Nyaka ichiem → "You must eat"
  • Nyaka wanene → "We must see him"

B. Other Tense and Modal Constructions

"Should" Construction with "Onego"

Expresses moral/expected obligation.

Structure: Onego + Subject Prefix + Subjunctive Verb (drop final -o)

  • Onego andik → "I should write"
  • Onegandik (contracted)

"May" Construction with "Dipop"

Expresses possibility or permission.

Structure: Dipop + Subject Prefix + Full Verb

  • Dipop andiko → "I may write"
  • Dipopwandiko → "We may write"

"Would" Construction with "De"

Indicates hypothetical/conditional past actions.

Structure: De + Subject Prefix + Verb (with -o)

  • De andiko → "I would write"
  • Dewandiko (contracted)

Subjunctive Imperatives ("Let Me/Us")

Structure: Subject Prefix + Verb (subjunctive)

  • Andik! → "Let me write!"
  • Wandik! → "Let us write!"

"Should Have" Past Obligation

Structure: Ne + Onego + Subject + Subjunctive Verb

  • Ne onego andik → "I should have written"
  • Nonegandik (contracted)

Past Perfect ("Had Done")

Structure: Ne + Subject Perfective Marker + Verb

  • Ne asendiko → "I had written"
  • Nasendiko (contracted)

Causative Construction

Structure: Subject Prefix + ami/wami (make) + Object + Verb

  • Ami ondiko → "I make him write"
  • Wami indik → "We make you write"
  • Negation: Ok ami ondik → "I don't make him write"

C. Morphosyntactic and Pedagogical Notes

Infinitive Usage

Main verb in future/modal constructions stays uninflected.

Subject-Verb Agreement

Agreement marked on auxiliaries (biro, nyaka, de), not main verb.

Subjunctive Mood

Formed by dropping final -o: andik, wandik

Contractions

Common in speech/writing; maintain grammatical integrity:

abochiemo, nonegandik, dandiko

Temporal Markers

e.g., chieng', wang', kiny help indicate futurity.

Teaching Recommendations

  • Use contrastive drills: full vs. contracted forms.
  • Emphasize subjunctive transformations.
  • Highlight modal distinctions: may vs. should vs. would.
  • Reinforce causative structures with object marking.
