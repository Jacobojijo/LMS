3.1.2 Adjective of Quality and Quantity

1. Adjectives of Quality in Dholuo

These adjectives express inherent characteristics, aesthetic features, or moral evaluations of nouns. They often carry strong cultural and semantic nuance, particularly in value-laden or evaluative contexts.

1.1 Maber / Maber Ahinya

  • Gloss: good, beautiful, nice, attractive
  • Morphological Structure: ma- (adjectival prefix) + ber (root for goodness/beauty)
  • Expanded Meaning: Carries both moral and aesthetic value; may imply physical attractiveness, ethical uprightness, or social approval.
  • Example: Nyako maber — "Beautiful girl" Yoo maber — "Good path" Maber ahinya — "Very good" or "Extremely beautiful"

1.2 Marach / Marach Ahinya

  • Gloss: bad, ugly, loathsome, undesirable
  • Morphological Structure: ma- + rach (root for badness)
  • Expanded Meaning: Indicates negative character, undesirable appearance, or moral failing. The form ahinya adds intensification.
  • Example: Dhano marach — "A bad person" Paro marach ahinya — "A very bad thought"

1.3 Manumu

  • Gloss: uncooked, unripe
  • Note: Does not follow the ma- prefix pattern; appears as an adjective inherently.
  • Expanded Meaning: Describes incomplete natural or culinary states. It may apply to both food and natural development.
  • Example: Rech manumu — "Uncooked fish" Mapera manumu — "Unripe guava"

2. Adjectives of Quantity in Dholuo

These adjectives specify amount, extent, or degree in terms of count or mass. They are crucial for quantification, comparison, and measuring sufficiency.

2.1 Maduong'

  • Gloss: big
  • Morphological Structure: ma- + duong' (large)
  • Semantic Note: Refers to size, but can also metaphorically indicate importance.
  • Example: Rombo maduong' — "Big sheep" Ng'ato maduong' — "Important person"

2.2 Mang'eny

  • Gloss: many, a lot
  • Morphological Structure: ma- + ng'eny (many/much)
  • Usage: Plural quantifier; used with count nouns.
  • Example: Ji mang'eny — "Many people" Kitepe mang'eny — "Many books"

2.3 Matin / Manok

  • Gloss: few, small, little
  • Morphological Structures:
    • ma- + tin (few) = matin
    • manok is irregular and does not follow standard prefixation
  • Usage: Describes small quantity or number
  • Examples: Roya matin — "Small calf" Chak manok — "A little milk"

2.4 Maoromo → Moromo

  • Gloss: enough
  • Morphological Structure: ma- + oromo (suffice)
    • Spoken usage often drops the prefix, yielding moromo
  • Semantic Use: Indicates sufficiency
  • Example: Pi moromo — "Enough water" Chak moromo — "Sufficient milk"

2.5 Mathoth

  • Gloss: numerous, abundant, many
  • Morphological Structure: ma- + thoth (abundance)
  • Semantic Emphasis: Stronger than mang'eny; implies overflowing or excess
  • Example: Rabuon mathoth — "Numerous potatoes" Ji mathoth — "A large multitude"

2.6 Maogundho → Mogundho

  • Gloss: plenty
  • Morphological Structure: ma- + ogundho (plentiful)
    • Like moromo, the prefix is often dropped: mogundho
  • Semantic Usage: Highlights richness or ample provision
  • Example: Chak mogundho — "Plenty of milk" Nyuka mogundho — "A lot of porridge"

3. Interrogative Quantifiers in Dholuo

These forms elicit quantitative information, either in terms of number or dimension.

3.1 Adi?

  • Gloss: How many?
  • Usage: Seeks a numerical response.
  • Example: Kitepe adi? — "How many books?"

3.2 Oromo nade?

  • Gloss: How big?
  • Usage: Measures size or extent.
  • Example: Kom oromo nade? — "How big is the chair?"
