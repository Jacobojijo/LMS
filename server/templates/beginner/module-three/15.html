3.8 Tenses

3.8.1 Introduction

In Dholuo grammar, tenses indicate the time of an action or event, classifying verbs into past, present, and future forms. Each of these three main tenses is further divided into four aspects: simple, continuous, perfect, and perfect continuous. This results in a total of twelve distinct tenses.

Here's a more detailed breakdown:

  • Present Tense: Describes actions happening now, habitual actions, or facts. (Gik matimore)
  • Past Tense: Describes actions that have already occurred. (Gik mosetimore)
  • Future Tense: Describes actions that will happen in the future. (Gik mabiro timore)

Within each main tense, there are four forms:

  • Simple: Expresses a general or habitual action.
  • Continuous (Progressive): Shows an ongoing action at a particular time.
  • Perfect: Indicates an action completed before a specific time or a past action with present relevance.
  • Perfect Continuous (Perfect Progressive): Describes an action that began in the past, continues until the present, or was completed just before a specific point in time.

Understanding tenses is crucial for accurate and effective communication in Dholuo, ensuring that actions and events are described in the correct temporal context.

3.8.2 Present Tense

1. Definition and Function of the Present Tense in Dholuo

Kinde Masani (Present Tense) describes actions that:

  • Are happening now (present progressive)
  • Happen habitually (simple present)

In Dholuo, there is no distinction between:

  • He eats (simple present)
  • He is eating (present progressive)

➝ Both are translated with the same verb form.

2. General Verb Form in Present Tense

  • Dholuo verbs retain their infinitive endings (typically -o or -yo), even when conjugated.
  • Present tense conjugation involves prefixation to the infinitive verb stem.
  • The infinitive form (e.g., nindo, chiemo) serves as the root for conjugation.

3. Present Tense Prefix Formation

Pronoun Prefix Conjugation (using chiemo - to eat) Meaning
an a- achiemo I eat / I am eating
in i- ichiemo You eat
en o- ochiemo He/She eats
wan wa- wachiemo We eat
un u- uchiemo You (pl.) eat
gin gi- gichiemo They eat

Rule: Remove the final -n from the pronoun and use that as the verb prefix.

Exception: "en" becomes "o-" in conjugation.

4. Overt Subject Rule

When a proper noun or overt subject (e.g., Odipo, Olum, Mama) is used:

  • The subject prefix is dropped.
  • The verb appears in its infinitive form without a subject prefix.

Examples:

Sentence Structure Meaning
Olum kwayo dhoggi Overt subject (Olum) + kwayo Olum is grazing the cows
Odipo nindo Odipo + nindo Odipo is sleeping
Onyango tiyo Onyango + tiyo Onyango is working
Nyithindo dhi e skul Children + go to school Children go to school
Mama dhi kar thieth Mama + go to hospital Mum is going to hospital
Achieng' uso Overt subject + infinitive verb Achieng' sells

Compare with:

Ouso → She sells (No overt subject ➝ prefix used)

6. Verb Root Observations

Most verbs in their infinitive form end in:

  • -o (e.g., nindo, tiyo, chiemo)
  • -yo (e.g., kwayo)

These endings are retained in all present tense forms.

8. Complex Present Tense Usage in Narrative Context

Narrative example (from source text):

Olum ose chiew kendo ose luoko wang'e. Oduaro dhi e pap mondo okwa dhok.

Translation:

Olum has grown and washed his face. He wants to go to the field to herd cattle.

Even within present-tense structure, aspectual markers like ose- (perfective marker) are used for completed actions.

9. Morphological Insight

Dholuo's present tense system illustrates:

  • Prefixation for subject pronouns
  • Infinitive root retention
  • No morphological distinction between simple and progressive forms

The agglutinative nature of Dholuo is evident in:

  • Embedded grammatical information (subject + tense) within a single verb form

10. Implications for Language Learning

Learners must focus on:

  • Correct prefixation based on the pronoun
  • Recognizing when not to use prefixes (with overt subjects)
  • Understanding that context determines whether the verb implies habitual or ongoing action
