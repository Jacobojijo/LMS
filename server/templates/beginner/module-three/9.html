3.4.3 Verb Conjugation (Verb to be) & (to have)

1. Introduction to the Verb "To Be" in Dholuo

  • Dholuo does not have a direct equivalent of the verb "to be" in present-tense declarative statements (e.g., "The food is tasty" becomes "Chiemo mamit!" without a verb).
  • However, identity and location are conveyed through special constructions and verb forms like "tie", "nitie", and copular structures with pronouns.

2. Personal Pronouns with "To Be" (Identity and Role)

Dholuo uses personal pronouns + contextual cues (often without a verb) to express identity.

Person Pronoun Translation
First Person Singular an I am
Second Person Singular in you are
Third Person Singular en he/she/it is
First Person Plural wan we are
Second Person Plural un you (pl) are
Third Person Plural gin they are

Examples:

  • An e jatend piny – I am the president.
  • In e japuonj – You are the teacher.
  • Wan Jo Luo – We are Luos.

3. Subject-Pronoun Initial Consonants as Person Indicators

Dholuo verb conjugation often uses initial letters of personal pronouns to indicate the subject:

Prefix Person
A- First person singular
I- Second person singular
E- Third person singular
Wa- First person plural
U- Second person plural
Gi- Third person plural

Examples in Coordinated Subjects:

  • An gi en – I and him
  • En gi gin – He and they
  • Gin gi un – They and you (pl.)

4. Expressing Presence and Location: The Verb "Tie"

  • The verb "tie" means "to be located" or "to be present".
  • When a personal pronoun is the subject, it is used in full form before the verb.
Pronoun + tie Form Meaning
an antie I am present
in intie You (sg) are present
en entie He/she is present
wan wantie We are present
un untie You (pl) are present
gin gintie They are present

Location Examples:

  • An ka – I am here
  • Wantie ka – We are here
  • Gin ka – They are here

5. Special Case: "Nitie" for Overt Subjects

  • "Nitie" is used when the subject is explicit/named, not pronominal (e.g., not "I", "you", "he").

Examples:

  • Chiemo nitie? – Is there food?
  • Ee, chiemo nitie. Entie. – Yes, there is food. He is here.
  • Paul nitie. – Paul is here.

6. The Opposite of Tie: "Onge" (Negation of Presence)

  • Onge is used to indicate non-existence or absence.

Examples:

  • Chiemo onge. – There is no food.
  • Oonge. – He/she is not present.

7. Expressing Possession: The Verb "To Have"

  • Dholuo expresses "to have" using the personal pronoun + gi/kod, meaning "with".

Forms:

Structure Meaning
[Pronoun] + gi I/you/he has…
[Pronoun] + kod I/you/he has… (alt)
[Overt Subject] + ni + gi Subject has…

Examples with Pronouns:

  • An gi chiemo. – I have food.
  • En gi guok. – He has a dog.
  • Gin gi nyithindo. – They have children.
  • An kod sufuria. – I have a cooking pot.

Examples with Overt Subject:

  • Nyieka nigi nyithindo. – My co-wife has children.

Note: "ni" is used only when the subject is explicit.

8. Cultural-Linguistic Note

  • These forms convey identity, location, and possession—core aspects of social and cultural representation.
  • In Luo society, self-identification (e.g., Wan Jo Luo), presence (antie), and possession (gi nyithindo) express not only grammar but status, role, and community belonging.

9. Summary Table: Key Verb Forms

Function Form Explanation
Identity Pronoun + e/n "An e japuonj" → I am a teacher
Presence Pronoun + tie "Entie" → He is present
With overt subject Nitie "Chiemo nitie" → Food is present
Absence Onge "Chiemo onge" → No food
Possession (pronoun) gi/kod "An gi chiemo" → I have food
Possession (overt subj.) ni gi "Paul nigi chiemo" → Paul has food
