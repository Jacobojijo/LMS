4.15.3 Subjunctive Mood

I. Definition and Core Function of the Subjunctive Mood

Subjunctive mood in Dholuo expresses non-factual or non-indicative actions such as:

  • Politeness
  • Probability
  • Doubt
  • Desire
  • Supplication (prayer or pleading)
  • Exhortation (urging or encouraging)

II. Common Contexts for Subjunctive Usage

Expressions of Possibility/Probability
"May" or "Might" in English = "Nyalo" in Dholuo
"Nyalo" means "can/may/be able to" and is conjugated with the subject:

Koth nyalo chue — It may rain.
Wanyalo dhi kiny — We may go tomorrow.
Aparo ni ginyalo biro sa asaya — I think they may come anytime.

Requests and Inquiries (Polite/Subjunctive Tone)

Apenji ka inyalo konya — I ask if you can help me.

Prayers and Pleadings (High Subjunctive Use)

Ruoth ka iyie to kel koth! — Lord, if You will, bring rain! (Subjunctive of divine pleading)

III. The Imperative and Its Tone-Based Duality

Tone-Dependent Mood Interpretation
Identical verb forms may imply:

  • A command if said in authoritative tone
  • A request if said in gentle or polite tone
Example:
Lam Wuonwa manie polo! — "Pray our Father in heaven!"
Can be both imperative or polite subjunctive, depending on tone

IV. Imperative Verb Forms

Derived by removing final vowel or with modified tones:

Chiemo → Chiem! (Eat!)
Nindo → Nind! (Sleep!)
Tiyo → Ti! (Work!)
Tedo → Ted! (Cook!)
Ndiko → Ndik! (Write!)
Lemo → Lem! (Pray!)
Lamo → Lam! (Pray!)

Command strength varies by tone, not only morphology

V. The "A-n-A" Imperative Future Pattern

Used to issue a strong command with future reference
Verb loses its final -o or -yo ending in imperative conjugation
Conjugated with both subject prefix and final vowel

Ana dhi! — Will I go!
Ini dhi! — Will you go!
Eno dhi! — Will he/she go!
Wana dhi! — Will we go!
Unu dhi! — Will you (pl) go!
Gini dhi! — Will they go!

VI. Subjunctive Verb Morphology

Rule 1: Drop the Final -o (common subjunctive form)

Agombo → Agomb — That I desire
Ochiemo → Ochiem — That he may eat

Rule 2: Add -i in Some Verbs

Aluok → Aluoki — That I may wash
Inindo → Inindi — That you may sleep
Athietho → Athiethi — That I may be treated
Gipuonjo → Gipuonji — That they may teach
Watedo → Watedi — That we may cook
Amotho → Amodhi — That I may drink

Special Irregular Verbs

Biro → Bi — That he/she may come
Neno → Ne — That I may see

VII. Subjunctive Forms with Pronoun Suffixes

When verbs end in -yo, subjunctive drops the "y":

Ati — That I may work
Ami — That I may give
Amiyi — That I may give you

For verbs ending in -nyo, the subjunctive becomes:

Akony — That I help
Akonyi — That I help you
Akonu — That I help you all (note: drops "y" before plural suffix)

VIII. Core Functions and Use-Cases of the Subjunctive

Expressing Potential/Intention

Miya pi amodhi — Give me water that I may drink
Owinjore ikonya — It's necessary that you help me

Posing Purpose-Driven Questions

Ere poudho apur? — Where is the garden that I may dig?
Waduog kode chieng' machielo? — Which day shall we return with him?

Issuing Commands (subtle subjunctive)

Donj ot, mos minu — Enter the house, greet your mother
Kik ibi — Do not come
Konya ka aeto kongi — Help me, then help them
Wachiem! — Let's eat!

Compound Instructions

Imos minu kendo inyise ni obi — Greet your mother and tell her he is coming
Dhi imosna jaodi — Go greet my wife
Wachne mondo ong'iewna buk — Tell him to buy me a book

IX. Subjunctive with Potential Particle "d"

Hypothetical or polite supposition

Dakonyi puro koso? — Could I possibly help you dig?

X. Subjunctive after Obligational Expressions

Often follows "Owinjore" (It is necessary that...)

Owinjore ikonya — You ought to help me

XI. Subjunctive in Negation and Future Tense

Negative Future:

Ok abi chiemo kiny — I will not eat tomorrow

Negative Present:

Ok adwar chiemo — I don't want food

General Future Tense Examples (Non-immediate):

Kiny nadogi — Tomorrow I will return
Ang' nadogi — I will return sometime today

XII. Summary of Structural Markers

Marker/Form Meaning Context
-nyalo- May / Might Probability, Potential
A-n-A Future Command Emphatic, forceful tone
-i suffix Subjunctive variant Select verbs
Dropped final -o/yo Core subjunctive form Across verb types
d- prefix Potential marker Hypotheticals

Conclusion

The subjunctive mood in Dholuo is a linguistically rich and multifunctional tool crucial for:

  • Social politeness
  • Hypothetical and intentional discourse
  • Commands, exhortations, and spiritual appeals

Proper tone, verb morphology, and subject-verb agreement are essential for correct usage.
Mastery of the subjunctive enhances fluency in prayer, academic writing, storytelling, and persuasive communication in Dholuo.
