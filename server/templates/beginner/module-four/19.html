4.15 Moods

4.15.1 Introductions

In Dholuo grammar, mood refers to a verb's function in a sentence, indicating the speaker's attitude or intention towards the action or state described. There are three primary moods: indicative, imperative, and subjunctive,

  1. Indicative Mood: This mood expresses a fact or statement, whether a question or a declaration. It's the most common mood used in everyday conversation.
    Example: "The cat is sleeping." (Statement)
    Example: "Do you like coffee?" (Question)
  2. Imperative Mood: This mood expresses a command or request. It often implies an implied "you" as the subject.
    Example: "Close the door." (Command)
    Example: "Please help me." (Request)
  3. Subjunctive Mood: This mood expresses a wish, a hypothetical situation, a suggestion, or a condition contrary to fact. It's less common in everyday speech but frequently used in formal writing and literature.
    Example: "I wish I were there." (Wish)
    Example: "If I were you, I would..." (Hypothetical situation)
    Example: "I suggest that he be there." (Suggestion)

4.15.2 Indicative Mood

I. Definition of the Indicative Mood

The Indicative Mood in Dholuo is the grammatical mood used to express facts, real events, and certainties across three-time frames:

  • Past (what has happened)
  • Present (what is happening)
  • Future (what will happen)

This mood is the default or neutral mode of expression, used in narration, description, reporting, and stating truths.

II. Structural Overview

  • The verbs are regular in form, allowing predictable conjugation.
  • Temporal distinction is marked by the presence or absence of specific auxiliaries:
    • Past Tense Marker: nene
    • Present Tense: No auxiliary; root verb used directly
    • Future Tense Marker: abiro + verb (will + verb)

V. Morphological Notes

  • The past tense consistently uses the auxiliary "nene" followed by the 1st person singular verb.
  • The present tense verbs begin directly with a-, representing 1st person singular, with no auxiliary.
  • The future tense uses "abiro" (from the root biro, "to come") as an auxiliary that precedes the main verb, indicating an impending action.

Linguistic Insight:
The future construction mimics movement toward the action, reflecting the Luo conceptualization of time as approaching events.

VI. Pedagogical Strategies for Mastery

Pattern Recognition Exercise
Identify and memorize the consistent structure of:

  • Nene + [Present Verb] = Past
  • [Present Verb] = Present
  • Abiro + [Verb Stem] = Future

Drill Practice Suggestion:
Practice by creating triplet sets (past–present–future) for each verb, e.g.:

Nene andiko → Andiko → Abiro ndiko
Nene awang'o → Awang'o → Abiro wang'o

Time-Tense Anchoring Activity:
Create sentence pairs using each tense with time expressions:

Kiny nene anindo. – Yesterday I slept.
Kata kata, anindo. – Right now, I'm sleeping.
Kiny abiro nindo. – Tomorrow I will sleep.

VII. Summary of Key Takeaways

  • ✅ Indicative Mood = Reality/Fact-Based Statements
  • ✅ Tense is Regular and Predictable
  • ✅ "Nene" = Past, No Marker = Present, "Abiro" = Future
  • ✅ Consistent Verb Roots Across Tenses
  • ✅ Highly Useful in Narration, Daily Speech, Academic Writing
