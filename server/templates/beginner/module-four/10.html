4.7 Question & Question Tags

4.7.1 Introductions

1. General Characteristics of Dholuo Questions

  • Dholuo questions frequently employ the subjunctive tense, especially in seeking intentions or preferences.
  • Interrogatives rely on a rich inventory of question words, each targeting specific informational domains such as identity, location, quantity, and cause.

2. Essential Interrogative Vocabulary

Function Dholuo Term Translation / Use
How nadi In what manner?
How many adi Numerical inquiry
Why/How nang'o Cause or reasoning
What ang'o Object/content inquiry
When kar ang'o Temporal inquiry
Where kanye Spatial inquiry
Where are? eke Location of plural subjects
Where is? ere Location of singular subject
Which (sing.) mane Selection among options (singular)
Which (plural) mage Selection among options (plural)
Who (sing?) ng'a/ng'ano/ng'awa Personal identity
Who (plural) ng'a gini Group identity
Why (formal) mar ang'o Cause/reason inquiry

3. Interrogative Sentence Structures: Form and Function

  • Object-focused Interrogatives:
    • Macha ang'o? — What is that?
    • Ma ang'o? — What is this? (For objects)
  • Person-focused Interrogatives:
    • Ng'a ma nitie e kilas? — Who is in class?
    • Ma ng'a? — Who is this?
  • Name Inquiry:
    • Nyingi ng'a? — What is your name?
  • Preference Inquiry with Subjunctive:
    • Diher chiemo mane? — Which food would you like?
    • Chiemo mane madiher? — (Lit: Food which you would like?)
  • Plural Object Inquiry:
    • Ihero kombe mage? — Which chairs do you like?
  • Location-based Interrogatives with "ni":
    • Buge ni kanye? — Where are the books?
    • Kalam ni kanye? — Where is the pen?
    • Use of copula "ni" indicates specific placement or attribution of objects.
      • Buge ni kucha — The books are over there.
      • En kucha — It's over there (Note: "ni" dropped with pronouns like en)

4. Question Tags in Dholuo: Confirmatory & Contradictory Extensions

  • Tags provide a means to confirm, challenge, or elicit validation from a conversational partner.
  • Structure: Main clause + tag (often with "koso" or "ok")
Example Translation
Bende ing'eye, koso? You know him, don't you?
Ing'eyo japuonj, ok kamano? You know the teacher, isn't that so?
Idhi koso ok idhi? Are you going or not?
Imer koso ok imer? Are you drunk or not?
Nene igoye, ok kamano? You beat him, didn't you?
  • Responses incorporate affirmation/negation particles:
    • Kamano/Ee — Yes
    • Ooyo — No
    • Ok — Not (appears before verb to negate)

5. Syntax of Negation within Interrogative Structures

  • "Ok" as the negation particle, directly precedes the verb.
  • Changes verb to negative form in all relevant tenses:
    • Ok adhi — I am not going.
    • Ok ang'eye — I do not know him.
  • Subjunctive negation examples:
    • Ooyo, ok abi biro — No, I will not come.
    • Ooyo, ok ubi nyalo — No, you will not manage.

6. The Role of "Bende" in Questions

  • Bende is a flexible interrogative particle used to:
    • Signal yes/no questions.
    • Emphasize inquiry tone.
    • Occasionally optional but helpful in framing direct questions.
  • Example:
    • Bende idhi koso ok idhi? — Will you go or not?

Conclusion: Functional Diversity in Dholuo Questions

Dholuo interrogatives reflect a highly nuanced grammatical system, combining:

  • Subjunctive mood for respectful inquiries and hypotheticals,
  • Morphosyntactic negation via "ok",
  • Copulative structures for location queries,
  • Affirmation/Negation particles for response clarity,
  • and Tag questions for conversational cooperation.

This system ensures linguistic precision, contextual awareness, and a high degree of interpersonal sensitivity in communication.
