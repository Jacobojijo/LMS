4.3 Compliment (Part 01)

4.3.1 Introductions

1. Definition and Function

  • In Dholuo syntax, a complement is the element of a sentence that receives the action of a verb directly.
  • It typically manifests as a direct object, completing the meaning of a transitive verb.
  • The complement is essential for expressing semantic completeness in action-based clauses.

2. Key Characteristic: Action Passes Directly to Object

  • In all examples, the verb's effect or result is immediately felt by the noun that follows — this is the complement.
  • This structure illustrates verb-object unity, where the action performed by the subject is transferred directly to the object.

3. Structural Pattern: Subject + Verb + Complement (Object)

The construction adheres to the standard SVO (Subject–Verb–Object) sequence:

  • Subject (Doer of the action)
  • Verb (Action performed)
  • Complement/Object (Receiver of the action)

4. Illustrative Examples & Analyses

Odipo tong'o yien
Translation: Odipo is cutting the tree
Subject: Odipo
Verb: tong'o (cut)
Complement: yien (tree)
✔ The action of cutting directly affects the tree.

Odipo beto lum
Translation: Odipo is carrying grass
Subject: Odipo
Verb: beto (carry)
Complement: lum (grass)
✔ A classic transitive action — what is being carried? Grass.

Odipo gi Olum chamo kuon
Translation: Odipo and Olum are eating Ugali
Subject: Odipo gi Olum (Odipo and Olum)
Verb: chamo (eat)
Complement: kuon (ugali)
✔ The plural subject performs a single shared action on a single object.

Olum yawo dhoot
Translation: Olum is opening the door
Subject: Olum
Verb: yawo (open)
Complement: dhoot (door)
✔ The door receives the full action of opening.

Odipo loro dhoot
Translation: Odipo is closing the door
Subject: Odipo
Verb: loro (close)
Complement: dhoot (door)
✔ The door once again is the recipient of a transitive action — this time, closing.

5. Syntactic Importance

  • The complement is not optional in transitive constructions.
  • Without a complement, the sentence becomes incomplete or semantically ambiguous.
  • Complements maintain grammatical integrity by completing the valency of transitive verbs.

6. Semantic Role of the Complement

  • Complements answer the question: What is the verb acting upon?
  • They provide clarity, precision, and context to the event being described.
  • The choice of complement often defines the verb's scope and meaning.

4.3.2 Indirect Compliment

Definition and Grammatical Function

  • An indirect complement in Dholuo represents a recipient or beneficiary of an action, rather than the direct object of the verb.
  • It defines a mediated relationship between the subject (agent) and the object (theme).
  • This structure reflects transfer, communication, or exchange, where the object is given, sent, or delivered to someone.

2. Structural Composition: Subject + Verb + Indirect Complement + Direct Object

General Pattern:
Subject → Verb → Recipient (Indirect Complement) → Thing Given (Direct Object)

  • The indirect complement answers the question: To whom? or For whom?
  • The direct object answers: What?

3. Key Characteristics

  • Dual-Object Construction: Involves both a direct object (the item) and an indirect object (the recipient).
  • The verb often implies transfer: verbs like miyo (give), kawo (receive/get), wuoyo (tell), etc.
  • Word order is critical in distinguishing who is giving/receiving and what is being given/received.

4. Illustrative Examples and Analyses

Odipo miyo Olum luth
Translation: Odipo gives Olum the stick
Subject: Odipo
Verb: miyo (give)
Indirect Complement: Olum (recipient)
Direct Object: luth (stick)
✔ The action (giving) is directed at Olum, who receives the stick.

Amiyo Olum barua
Translation: I gave Olum the letter
Subject: A- (I)
Verb: -miyo (give)
Indirect Complement: Olum
Direct Object: barua (letter)
✔ The action and possession are separated; the letter is transferred to Olum by me.

Nyathi miyo mingi adita
Translation: The child gives the mother a basket
Subject: Nyathi (child)
Verb: miyo (gives)
Indirect Complement: mingi (his/her mother)
Direct Object: adita (basket)
✔ Emphasizes the social-relational layer — the child contributes to the mother.

Olum kawo luth kuom Odipo
Translation: Olum receives the stick from Odipo
Subject: Olum
Verb: kawo (receives/takes)
Direct Object: luth (stick)
Prepositional Indirect Complement: kuom Odipo (from Odipo)
✔ The inverse of giving — Olum becomes the recipient, and Odipo becomes the source.

5. Semantic Functions of Indirect Complements

  • Recipient Role: Identifies the end-user or final destination of an action.
  • Beneficiary Role: Sometimes marks who benefits from an action, especially in dative constructions.
  • Source Role: In receptive constructions (kawo), marks origin (kuom Odipo).

6. Morphosyntactic Considerations

  • Prepositional Phrase: When using receptive verbs (kawo), the indirect complement is introduced by "kuom" (from).
  • Object Positioning: The indirect complement typically precedes the direct object when placed inside the verb clause (e.g., miyo Olum barua).
  • Subject-Verb Concord: The subject is morphemically embedded in the verb prefix.
