4.3.3 The Compliment of Terminal & Specification

1. The Complement of Terminality

Definition and Core Function

The Complement of Terminality identifies a destination or receiver of an action.

The action verb is directed towards a target, either in the form of a person, group, or location.

This structure highlights the endpoint of a verb's effect.

2. Structural Formula

Subject + Verb + Object + Preposition ("ni") + Terminal Complement (Receiver/Destination)

3. Key Features

The preposition "ni" serves as the grammatical marker that governs the terminal element (destination/receiver).

Not all verbs require "ni" — some inherently imply terminality.

4. Illustrative Examples and Analysis

• Odipo pimo adiera ni jong'ad bura

Translation: Odipo is narrating the truth to the judges

Subject: Odipo

Verb: pimo (narrate)

Object: adiera (truth)

Preposition: ni

Terminal Complement: jong'ad bura (judges)

✧ The action (narrating) is transmitted to a judicial audience.

• Olum chiwo barua ni wuon-go

Translation: Olum offers the letter to the owner

Verb: chiwo (offer)

✧ Verb chiwo typically requires the preposition "ni" when a recipient is specified.

Object: barua (letter)

Terminal Complement: wuon-go (owner)

✧ Here, the verb's trajectory terminates at the receiver of the offering.

• Amiyo nyaminwa njugu

Translation: I am giving my sister groundnuts

Verb: miyo (give)

✧ The verb miyo does not require the preposition "ni."

Recipient/Terminal Complement: nyaminwa (my sister)

✧ The terminality is implied within the verb's transference semantics.

5. Grammatical Insight

"Chiwo" vs. "Miyo":

  • Chiwo often pairs with "ni" to express offerings to someone.
  • Miyo, being an inherently transitive verb, encodes terminality within its structure.

6. The Complement of Specification ("Mar")

Definition and Function

The Complement of Specification indicates possession, origin, or characteristic attribution.

It defines what belongs to whom, or what characteristic is associated with what object.

7. Structural Formula

Noun Phrase (Head) + "mar" + Specifier (Possessor/Source)

8. Morphological and Semantic Features

"Mar" functions similarly to a genitive or possessive marker in English ("of").

The phrase after mar identifies ownership, association, or origin.

Inanimate, animate, abstract, or natural elements can be specifiers.

9. Illustrative Examples and Analysis

• Ler mar chieng'

The light of the sun

Ler (light) is possessed by chieng' (sun)

✧ Indicates natural attribution of a phenomenon to its source.

• Apaka mar nam

The waves of the lake

Apaka (waves) originate from nam (lake)

✧ Signifies a natural possession or source-based specification.

• Teko mar liech

The strength of the elephant

Emphasizes inherent attributes possessed by the noun liech (elephant).

• Agwecho mar punda

The kick of the donkey

Signifies typical behavioral traits possessed by a creature.

• Sulwe mar nam

The star of the lake

Could represent a figurative or poetic possession, possibly a reflection or metaphor.

• Ndalo mag pasaka

The Easter holidays

"Mag" is a plural possessive determiner derived from "mar"

Indicates the holidays belonging to or associated with Easter.

10. Comparative Observations: "Mar" vs. "Mag"

"Mar": Singular possessive particle

"Mag": Plural possessive (often paired with plural heads like ndalo)

Both forms convey association, possession, or relational linkage.
