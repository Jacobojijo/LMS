4.11 Price Asking

4.11.1 Introductions

I. Conceptualizing Value: "Nengo" as a Core Economic Lexeme

"Nengo" is the Dholuo term for price, denoting monetary value assigned to an item.

Commonly used in both direct commercial exchanges and informal market interactions.

Central to buyer-seller dialogue in everyday Luo economic life.

II. Asking for the Price: Penjo Nengo — Functional Expressions of Inquiry

A. Standard Inquiry Construction:

The most formal and widely accepted format is:

Nengone en adi? – How much does it cost?

"En adi" translates to "is how much?" and is attached to the item of interest.

B. Applied Examples of Nengo Inquiries:

Expression in Dholuo Translation in English
Nengo rabolo en adi? What is the price of the banana?
Nengo tiang' en adi? What is the price of the sugar-cane?

These expressions establish a specific reference to the object, placing emphasis on the noun before the price question.

C. Alternative Syntax: Emphasizing Currency over Value

Another common interrogative approach rephrases the structure to highlight monetary cost using the word pesa (money):

Expression in Dholuo Translation in English
Rabolo en pesa adi? How much does the banana cost?
Tiang' en pesa adi? How much does the sugar-cane cost?

This form places "pesa" in focus, situating the item as a subject of economic valuation.

III. Goyo Nengo: The Art of Price Negotiation

Goyo nengo directly translates to "bargaining the price".

It is a cultural and linguistic act of negotiation, emphasizing:

  • Mutual respect
  • Economic fairness
  • Communicative politeness

Bargaining often involves:

  • Expressing surprise or dissatisfaction at initial pricing.
  • Requesting a discount or more reasonable offer.

Culturally, goyo nengo is not seen as confrontational but as a ritualized form of engagement that sustains market relationships and reflects social intelligence.

IV. Linguistic & Cultural Insight: Functional Significance of Penjo Nengo

The ability to ask and negotiate prices is a marker of linguistic competence and pragmatic awareness in Dholuo.

Reflects the interactive economy where meaning, politeness, and commerce intersect.

Penjo nengo expressions show how economic language use is embedded in social behavior and community norms.

V. Summary: The Economics of Expression

Dholuo offers flexible syntactic pathways for asking prices, both item-focused (Nengo X en adi?) and money-focused (X en pesa adi?).

Goyo nengo reflects a culturally rich tradition of negotiation, emphasizing value sensitivity, relational discourse, and economic literacy.

Mastery of these expressions enhances one's market fluency, interpersonal negotiation skills, and cultural integration within Luo-speaking contexts.
