4.17 Review & Practice

4.17.1 Summary of Moods, Writing and Reading.

Mood in Dholuo: Definition and Classification

Mood reflects the speaker's attitude or intention toward the action/state described by the verb.

Three Core Moods in Dholuo:

  • Indicative Mood – States facts or asks questions.
    e.g., "The cat is sleeping." / "Do you like coffee?"
  • Imperative Mood – Issues commands or polite requests.
    e.g., "Close the door." / "Please help me."
  • Subjunctive Mood – Expresses wishes, hypotheticals, suggestions.
    e.g., "I wish I were there." / "If I were you..."

4.15.2 Indicative Mood in Dholuo

A. Functional Definition
Expresses real events, facts, or certainties across:

  • Past: What has occurred.
  • Present: What is occurring now.
  • Future: What will occur.

B. Structural Mechanics

  • Past Marker: nene
  • Present Tense: Root verb only.
  • Future Marker: abiro + verb stem

C. Tense Conjugation Chart (Sample Verbs)

Infinitive Past (Nene) Present Future (Abiro)
To eat nene achiemo achiemo abiro chiemo
To sleep nene anindo anindo abiro nindo
To bring nene akelo akelo abiro kelo
To dance nene agoyora agoyora abiro goyora
To write nene andiko andiko abiro ndiko
etc. ... ... ...

D. Morphological Insights

  • Verb stems are stable across tenses.
  • "nene" always precedes the past form.
  • "abiro" introduces the future, capturing the sense of motion toward time.
  • First person marker: "a-" in both past and present forms.

E. Pedagogical Strategies

Pattern Memorization:

nene + [verb] = past, [verb] = present, abiro + [verb] = future

Drills: Generate verb triplets for mastery.

Anchoring with Time Markers:

Kiny nene anindo (Yesterday I slept)
Anindo kata kata (I'm sleeping now)
Kiny abiro nindo (Tomorrow I'll sleep)

F. Key Takeaways

  • ✅ Indicative = Fact-based mood
  • ✅ "Nene" = Past, "Abiro" = Future, Root only = Present
  • ✅ Used in narration, reporting, and daily conversation
  • ✅ Structure is predictable and regular

Conditional Mood in Dholuo

A. Core Definition
Expresses hypothetical, dependent, or cause-effect scenarios.
Uses "KA" (if) and optionally "TO" (then).

B. Structural Format

  • Present Tense in KA clause
  • Future Tense (BIRO form) in consequence clause
  • Pattern: KA + Present Verb + (TO) + Future Verb

C. Examples

Ka ibiro, to wabirodhi. → If you come, we will go.
Ka itedo, to wabiro chiemo. → If you cook, we will eat.

D. Negative Conditionals
Negates both clauses with ok:

Ka ok ichula, to ok abitiyoni. → If you don't pay me, I won't work for you.

E. Past Conditional (Unrealized Past Events)
Constructed with "nene bedi ni..." + "nene di..."

Ka nene bedi ni ang'eyo ni koth biro chwe, to nene di ating'o mabul.
→ If I had known it would rain, I would have carried an umbrella.

F. Remote Hypothetical Reflections
For retrospective or moral/reflective discourse

Ka nene bedi ni oseng'eyo, to nene di ok oyie tedone.
→ If she had known, she wouldn't have agreed to marry him.

G. Future Conditional Forms
Predict future dependencies or outcomes:

Ka obiro biro, to abiro nyise. → If he will come, I will tell him.
Ka obiro yie kwayona, to obiro yiena mondo adhi.
→ If he accepts, he will let me go.

H. Summary Table of Key Conditional Patterns

Structure Type Dholuo Structure English Equivalent
Basic Conditional Ka + Present, (to) Future If + Present, then + will (verb)
Negative Conditional Ka ok + Present, (to) ok + Future If not..., then will not...
Past Conditional Ka nene bedi ni..., to nene di... If had..., would have...
Remote Hypothetical Same as past, in reflective/moral contexts Retrospective "what if"
Future Hypothetical Ka obiro..., to obiro... If he will..., he will...

I. Concluding Insights

  • The KA...TO construction is fundamental to logical reasoning in Dholuo.
  • Enables complex expressions of cause, consequence, planning, regret, and hypothetical thought.
  • Vital in:
    • Narrative writing
    • Argumentation
    • Emotional discourse
    • Sociocultural communication

Subjunctive Mood in Dholuo

I. Definition and Core Function
Denotes non-factual or non-indicative actions.
Conveys politeness, probability, doubt, desire, supplication, and exhortation.

II. Contextual Applications

Probability/Possibility: "Nyalo" = may/might/can.

Koth nyalo chue – It may rain.
Wanyalo dhi kiny – We may go tomorrow.

Polite Inquiries/Requests:

Apenji ka inyalo konya – May I ask if you can help me?

Prayers and Pleading:

Ruoth ka iyie to kel koth! – Lord, if You will, bring rain!

III. Tone-Based Duality in Imperatives
Tone alters mood interpretation: command vs. request.

Example: Lam Wuonwa manie polo! – Command or polite plea depending on tone.

IV. Imperative Verb Forms
Derived by removing final vowels:

Chiemo → Chiem!, Tiyo → Ti!, Ndiko → Ndik!

V. A-n-A Pattern (Future Imperative)
Dual subject-marking for future-oriented commands:

Ana dhi! – Will I go!
Gini dhi! – Will they go!

VI. Subjunctive Verb Morphology

Rule 1: Drop final -o

Agombo → Agomb, Ochiemo → Ochiem

Rule 2: Add -i

Aluok → Aluoki, Inindo → Inindi, Gipuonjo → Gipuonji

Special Irregulars:

Biro → Bi, Neno → Ne

VII. Pronoun Suffix Variants

-yo verbs lose "y" in subjunctive:

Ati, Ami, Amiyi

-nyo verbs:

Akony, Akonyi, Akonu

VIII. Functional Use Cases

Potential/Intentions:

Miya pi amodhi – Give me water that I may drink.

Purpose-driven Questions:

Ere poudho apur? – Where is the garden that I may dig?

Instructions/Commands:

Wachiem! – Let's eat!, Donj ot! – Enter the house!

Compound Instructions:

Imos minu kendo inyise ni obi – Greet your mother and tell her he is coming.

IX. Potential Particle "d-"
Used in polite hypotheticals:

Dakonyi puro koso? – Could I possibly help you dig?

X. Obligational Structures
With Owinjore – necessity expression:

Owinjore ikonya – You ought to help me.

XI. Subjunctive in Negative and Future Tense

Negative Future:

Ok abi chiemo kiny – I will not eat tomorrow.

General Future:

Kiny nadogi – I will return tomorrow.
