1.4.5 Telling Time

Dholuo employs different lexical items to describe time-related concepts, including past, present, and future tenses, as well as specific time frames of the day.

General Temporal Expressions

English Dholuo
Now Sani
Right Now Sani Sani/Tinende
Later Bang'e
Yesterday Nyoro
The day before yesterday Nyocha
Today Kawuono (Gonyo)
Tomorrow Kiny
The day after tomorrow Orucha

Examples:

An gi tije mang'enyo sani. (I have a lot of work now.)
Ne odwaro neno wuon-gi nyoro. (He wanted to see their father yesterday.)
Bang'e wabiro konyo gi tich. (Later, we will help with the work.)
Orucha wabiro dhi e dala. (The day after tomorrow, we will go home.)

Time of Day in Dholuo

The Luo people classify the day into four major segments, each with distinct lexical representations:

Time Dholuo
Morning Okinyi
Afternoon Odiochieng'
Evening Odhiambo
Night Otieno

Additional time-related expressions:

  • Very early in the morning – Kok gwen (contracted to KOGWEN)
  • Midnight – Dier otieno
  • Exactly 3 PM – Saa Onga Achiel (Saa Ochiko ewang'e ma Odiochieng')

Examples:

Okinyi, ji dwoko gi wach mar ler. (In the morning, people talk about light.)
Ne ochopo dier otieno ka ng'ato nindo. (It reached midnight when someone was sleeping.)
Saa ochiko ni en e chandruok mar higa. (Exactly 3 PM marks the hardest part of the year.)

Sentence Structure in Time Expressions

Time marker + Subject + Verb + Object
Nyoro (yesterday) + ne + oneno + wuon-gi. (Yesterday, he saw his father.)

Subject + Verb + Time marker
Anadhi bang'e. (I will go later.)

Temporal adverbs in sentences
Dier otieno, ne pod owinjo dwonde. (At midnight, she still heard voices.)
