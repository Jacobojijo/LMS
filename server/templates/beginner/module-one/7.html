1.5.1 Summary of Numbers & Numbering

Dholuo Numbers and Their Usage

Dholuo employs a decimal-based numbering system, reflecting precision and cultural significance in trade, timekeeping, and social organization. Numbers are systematically constructed using an additive approach.

Cardinal Numbers

Range Formation Examples
Basic Numbers (1–10) Individual terms Achiel (1), Ariyo (2), Adek (3), Ang'wen (4), Abich (5), Auchiel (6), Abiriyo (7), Aboro (8), Ochiko/Ongachiel (9), Apar (10)
Compound Numbers (11–19) Combining "apar" (10) with units Apar gachiel = 11, Apar gariyo = 12
Multiples of Ten (20–90) Prefix "Piero" to numbers Piero ariyo = 20, Piero abich = 50, Piero ochiko = 90
Hundreds (100–900) Using "mia" Mia ariyo = 200, Mia ochiko = 900
Thousands (1000–9000) Using "alufu" Alufu adek = 3000, Alufu ochiko = 9000

Examples of Quantification

Ordinal Numbers

Ordinal numbers in Dholuo denote order, rank, and sequence, formed by prefixing mar to cardinal numbers, except for mokuongo (first) and mogik (last).

Key Ordinal Numbers

  • Mokuongo (First)
  • Mar ariyo (Second)
  • Mar adek (Third)
  • Mar ang'wen (Fourth)
  • Mar abich (Fifth)

Linguistic and Cultural Relevance

  • Order of Events: Used in storytelling and genealogy
    • Example: Nyathi mar adek ne ok bedo mangima ("The third child did not survive")
  • Rank and Leadership: Expressing hierarchy
    • Example: Jaduong' mar ariyo no nyalo bedo kaka jatelo ("The second elder may act as a leader")
  • Education and Rituals: Essential in classroom instructions and calendrical events
    • Example: Due mar adek owinjore mondo wapuojre ("The sixth month is suitable for learning from elders")

1.6 General Ways of Communication

1.6.1 Introduction

The Luo people have a rich oral culture where communication is deeply intertwined with social customs and etiquette. Greetings (Mosruok) form an essential part of daily interactions, reflecting respect, kinship, and social cohesion.

1.6.2 General Forms of Greetings

Temporal Greeting Structures in Dholuo

a) Morning Greeting (Mos Mar Okinyi)

Context Greeting Response
One person Oyawore Oyawore ahinya
Many people Oyaworeuru Oyaworeuru ahinya

Used exclusively when the sun is rising, signifying the beginning of a new day.

b) Midday/General Greeting (Mos Mar Odiechieng')

Context Greeting Response
One person Misawa Misawa ahinya
Many people Misawauru Misawauru ahinya

Serves as both a general and situational greeting, adaptable across different contexts.

c) Evening Greeting (Mos Mar Odhiambo)

Context Greeting Response
One person Oyimore Oyimore ahinya
Many people Oyimoreuru Oyimoreuru ahinya

d) Night Greeting (Mos Mar Otieno)

Night greeting: Oriti inind maber! – "Goodnight! Sleep well."
