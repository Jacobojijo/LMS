1.4.4 Days of the Week and Months

Days of the Week

The Luo weekly calendar consists of seven days, each with a specific meaning linked to labor, economic activities, and spiritual life.

  1. Wuok-tich (Monday) – The First Day of Work

    Derived from "Wuok," meaning "to wake up or leave," and "tich," meaning "work."

    Historically, this day marked the beginning of farm work or other economic activities after the weekend's rest.

    Example Sentence:

    "Wuok-tich en ndalo ma jopiny chako tich mar puodho."

    Translation: "Monday is the day when people start farm work."

  2. Tich-ariyo (Tuesday) – The Second Day of Work

    "Tich" (work) and "Ariyo" (two), translating to "the second day of work."

    Reinforces the concept of labor continuity throughout the week.

    Example Sentence:

    "E Tich-ariyo, jotich nyalo tiyo maler."

    Translation: "On Tuesday, workers can work very well."

  3. Tich-adek (Wednesday) – The Third Day of Work

    "Tich" (work) and "Adek" (three), meaning "the third day of work."

    Mid-week marker in traditional and modern labor schedules.

    Example Sentence:

    "Tich-adek nyalo bedo ndalo maber mar yiero go puodho."

    Translation: "Wednesday can be a good day for selecting the farm."

  4. Tich-ang'wen (Thursday) – The Fourth Day of Work

    "Tich" (work) and "Ang'wen" (four), referring to "the fourth day of work."

    Traditionally a continuation of labor with preparations for market day.

    Example Sentence:

    "Tich-ang'wen, jonyuol tiyo e puodho."

    Translation: "Parents are working on the farm on Thursdays."

  5. Tich-abich (Friday) – The Fifth Day of Work

    "Tich" (work) and "Abich" (five), denoting "the fifth day of work."

    Traditionally, a crucial workday before the weekend's market and social interactions.

    Example Sentence:

    "Joma ok nyal tiyo tich abich chalo ni nyalo bedo matuo dala."

    Translation: "Those who cannot work on Friday might be sick at home."

  6. Chieng' ngeso (Saturday) – Market Day

    "Chieng'" (day/sun) and "Ngeso" from Swahili "Ongeza" (to add), referring to bargaining and trading.

    Traditionally, a market day for purchasing supplies and engaging in barter trade.

    Example Sentence:

    "Chieng' ngeso en ndalo ma ji dhi e chiro nyiepo."

    Translation: "Saturday is the day when people go to markets to buy goods."

  7. Jumapil (Sunday) – Day of Prayer and Rest

    "Jumapil" (from Arabic/Swahili)

    Traditionally, a rest day devoted to communal prayers and religious reflection.

    Example Sentence:

    "Jumapil en ndalo mar duoko piny e kido mar ler."

    Translation: "Sunday is a day for returning to the ways of righteousness."

Months of the Year

The twelve-month calendar in Dholuo reflects a systematic adaptation of the Gregorian calendar, incorporating both the linguistic structure and cultural practices of the Luo people.

Formation of Month Names

The months in Dholuo are formed by combining the prefix Dwe mar (month of) with the corresponding cardinal numbers. This structure emphasizes the ordinal progression of the months, where each number designates its respective position in the yearly cycle.

March Dwe mar Adek Third month (Month of Three)

May Dwe mar Abich Fifth month (Month of Five)
June Dwe mar Auchiel Sixth month (Month of Six)
July Dwe mar Abiriyo Seventh month (Month of Seven)
August Dwe mar Aboro Eighth month (Month of Eight)

Monthly Descriptions and Example Sentences

January (Dwe mar Achiel)

"Dwe mar Achiel chalo gi chakruok manyien."

Translation: "The first month feels like a new beginning."

February (Dwe mar Ariyo)

"Dwe mar Ariyo en dwe ma piny chalo gi hera."

Translation: "The second month is when the world seems full of love."

March (Dwe mar Adek)

"Dwe mar Adek ni osebedo mana maliet kendo."

Translation: "The third month is already getting warmer."

April (Dwe mar Ang'wen)

"Koth mogik chwee e Dwe mar Ang'wen."

Translation: "The last rains usually fall in the fourth month."

May (Dwe mar Abich)

"Dwe mar Abich en kinde mar pounjruok gi dongruok."

Translation: "The fifth month is a season of learning and growth."

June (Dwe mar Auchiel)

"Dwe mar Auchiel luongo jii mondo oket chenro mage maler."

Translation: "The sixth month reminds people to set clear goals."

July (Dwe mar Abiriyo)

"E Dwe mar Abiriyo, koyo chako bedo mag'ich."

Translation: "In the seventh month, the cold becomes intense."

August (Dwe mar Aboro)

"Dwe mar Aboro en dwe ma jopuonjre timo chenro mar lokruok."

Translation: "The eighth month is when students prepare for change."

September (Dwe mar Ochiko)

"E Dwe mar Ochiko, puonj bedo moridore ahinya."

Translation: "In the ninth month, learning becomes more serious."

October (Dwe mar Apar)

"Dwe mar Apar nyalo bedo kinde maber mar keto chenro manyien."

Translation: "The tenth month is a good time for setting new plans."

November (Dwe mar apar gachiel)

"E Dwe mar apar gachiel, jodongo chano tieko higa."

Translation: "In the eleventh month, elders prepare for the year's end."

December (Dwe mar Apar gariyo)

"Dwe mar Apar gi ariyo en dwe mar gorogoro kod mor."

Translation: "The twelfth month is a season of celebration and joy."
