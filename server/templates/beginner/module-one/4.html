1.4.3 Ordinal Numbers

Introduction

Ordinal numbers play a critical role in languages as they facilitate the expression of order, rank, and sequence. In Dholuo, ordinal numbers are constructed by prefixing the term [*ma]r* to the corresponding cardinal numbers, creating a systematic and logical framework for sequencing.

Structure and Formation of Ordinal Numbers in Dholuo

Ordinal numbers in Dholuo are derived directly from cardinal numbers by adding the prefix [*mar]*, which signifies "belonging to" or "associated with." The resulting structure emphasizes the numerical sequence while maintaining a connection to the cardinal base.

Key Ordinal Numbers:

  1. First – Mokuongo: Derived uniquely and not from a cardinal base.
  2. Second – Mar ariyo: Based on the cardinal number ariyo (2).
  3. Third – Mar adek: Based on adek (3).
  4. Fourth – Mar ang'wen: Based on ang'wen (4).
  5. Fifth – Mar abich: Based on abich (5).
  6. Sixth – Mar auchiel: Based on auchiel (6).
  7. Seventh – Mar abiriyo: Based on abiriyo (7).
  8. Eighth – Mar aboro: Based on aboro (8).
  9. Ninth – Mar ochiko: Based on ochiko or ongachiel (9).
  10. Tenth – Mar apar: Derived from the term apar (10).

Linguistic Characteristics

The prefix [*mar]* functions as a relational marker, linking the ordinal form to its cardinal counterpart. Its consistent use creates clarity and uniformity in the numerical system.

Ordinal numbers in Dholuo are precise and context-specific, used explicitly for ranking or sequencing rather than counting.

While most ordinal numbers follow the pattern of prefixing mar to the cardinal number, mokuongo (first) and mogik (Last) stands out as an exception, reflecting the unique linguistic treatment of the initial position.

Practical Applications of Ordinal Numbers

Social and Cultural Contexts

Ordinal numbers are employed in various cultural practices:

  • Order of Events: For instance, in storytelling or narrating ancestral lineages, ordinal numbers indicate the sequence of generations.
    • Example: Nyathi mar adek ne ok bedo mangima ("The third child did not survive").
  • Rank and Position: Used to express hierarchy, such as in leadership structures.
    • Example: Jaduong' mar ariyo no nyalo bedo kaka jatelo ("The second elder may act as a leader").

Educational Usage

Ordinal numbers are taught early in language learning, instilling an understanding of order and sequence:

  • Classroom scenarios often involve phrases like Nyathi mar adek nyalo luwe ("The third students may come next").

Calendrics and Rituals

  • Ordinal numbers are crucial in scheduling rituals, assigning ranks to participants, and marking ceremonial stages.
    • Example: Due mar adek owinjore mondo wapuojre koa kuom jodongo. ("The sixth month is suitable for learning from elders").

Examples in Sentences

  1. "Wendo mar ang'wen odonjo."
    • Translation: "The fourth guest has arrived."
    • Number: Mar ang'wen (Fourth).
  2. "Ng'at mar abich ne okonyo nyathi."
    • Translation: "The fifth person rescued the child."
    • Number: Mar abich (Fifth).
  3. "Ok ginyalo bedo mangima e okang' mar ochiko."
    • Translation: "They cannot all survive in ninth place."
    • Number: Mar ochiko (Ninth).
