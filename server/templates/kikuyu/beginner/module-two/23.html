2.9.1 Summary of Pronouns & Noun Declension

A. Interrogative Pronouns — core paradigm

Definition: question-words used to replace unknown nouns and request information (manner, reason, quantity, time, person, thing, place).

1. Key items (compact table)

Kikuyu English Use Example
Atĩa? (Atia?) How? manner / method Ũgũthiĩ atĩa? — "How will you go?"
Nĩkĩ? Why? reason / cause Nĩkĩ ũrĩũgama? — "Why do you stand?"
Aigana? How many? quantity (countable) Nĩ andũ aigana? — "How many people are they?"
Rĩ? (Rĩĩ?) When? time / event Nĩũgũũka rĩ? — "When will you come?"
Ũũ? (Nũũ?) Who? person identity Ũũ nĩ mũthamaki? — "Who is the chief?"
Kĩ? (Gĩkĩ?) What? thing / action Gĩkĩ nĩ kĩ? — "What is this?"
Kũ? (Kũũ?) Where? location Mũciĩ waku nĩ kũ? — "Where is your home?"

2. Present-tense question paradigm (verb-centred forms)

  • Ũgũthiĩ? — Are you going?
  • Arĩĩthire? — Is he/she going?
  • Mũgũthiĩ? — Are you (pl.) going?
  • Tũgũthiĩ? — Are we going?
  • Magũthiĩ? — Are they going?

Emphatic pattern: place independent pronoun before the verb for emphasis (e.g., Wĩ ũgũthiĩ? — "Are you really going?"; Nĩwe arĩĩthire? — "Is he/she really going?").

3. Tag questions (confirming / denying)

Structure: [Statement] + kana ti? (or kana gũtũrĩ?)

Examples:

  • Nĩwĩĩthiĩ, kana ti? — "You will go, won't you?"
  • Ũkũre, kana ti? — "You are tall, aren't you?"
  • Nĩwe nĩ mũthuri, kana gũtũrĩ? — "He's a man, isn't he?"

4. Location-related interrogatives — variations

  • Kũ? = Where? (general) → Mũciĩ waku nĩ kũ?
  • E kũ? = Where is he/she?
  • Me kũ? = Where are they?
  • Emphatic/distal locative: -rĩa adds "over there" sense → Mũciĩ waku nĩ kũrĩa?

B. Noun Declension — how cases are realised

High-level point: Kikuyu does not change noun stems to mark case. Grammatical role is shown by word order, noun-class agreement, and prepositions rather than morphological case endings.

1. Main cases — quick reference

Case Function Does the noun change?
Nominative Subject ❌ No — noun form unchanged; position marks subject
Accusative Direct object ❌ No — object role inferred by verb position
Dative Indirect object (to/for) ❌ No — expressed with prepositions (e.g., kũrĩ)
Ablative / prepositional From/by/with ❌ No — expressed with prepositions
Genitive Possession ✅ No change to noun itself; possessive prefix (agreement) changes

2. Genitive / possession — mechanics (point form)

  • Possession is the only "case-like" change noted: the possessed noun itself remains constant but the possessive element shows agreement.
  • Mechanism: attach a possessive adjective/prefix that agrees with the noun's class (not with the possessor).
  • Genitive connector: wa (and class-specific variants like ya, gĩa, rĩa) functions like English "of" (e.g., Mũndũ wa Kamau).

Worked examples (from source):

  • Mũndũ → mũndũ wakwa — my person
  • Nyũmba → nyũmba yakwa — my house
  • Gĩthaka → gĩthaka gĩakwa — my farm

3. Teaching summary — what learners must master

  • Genitive is the key case to practice: students must predict the possessive prefix by noun-class, not by changing the noun.
  • Other cases are stable: reinforce that nouns retain their citation shape in subject/object/dative/ablative roles.
  • Prepositions carry oblique meanings (dative/ablative); teach kũrĩ for "to/for" and common locative constructions.

C. Introduction to Adjectives & Possessive Adjectives

1. Adjectives — essential facts

  • Definition: words that describe or modify nouns (qualities: size, colour, shape, state).
  • Agreement: adjectives must agree in noun class with the noun they modify — the prefix of the adjective changes to match the noun's prefix.
  • Types (as provided): descriptive (e.g., nene, mwerũ), comparative (via kũrĩ = "than"), superlative (periphrastic: gũgĩkĩra othe = "surpasses all"), demonstrative, possessive, interrogative adjectives, proper adjectives.
  • Illustration (agreement): "big" appears as mũnene (person), nene (house), kĩnene (item).

2. Possessive adjectives — paradigm & behaviour

Position: possessive adjectives follow the noun.

Root forms (person paradigm):

Person Root English Example with mũndũ
1st sing. -akwa my mũndũ wakwa
2nd sing. -aku your mũndũ waku
3rd sing. -ake his / her mũndũ wake
1st pl. -itũ our mũndũ witũ
2nd pl. -anyu your (pl.) mũndũ wanyu
3rd pl. -ao their mũndũ wao

Key rule: the possessive adjective's prefix varies by the noun's class; the noun itself remains unchanged.

3. Possessive forms with plural nouns (attested)

Example pattern Possessive Singular example Plural example
My mũciĩ wakwa (my home) mĩciĩ yakwa (my homes)
Your (sg) mũciĩ waku mĩciĩ yaku
His/Her mũciĩ wake mĩciĩ yake
Our mũciĩ witũ mĩciĩ yitũ
Your (pl) mũciĩ wanyu mĩciĩ yanyu
Their mũciĩ wao mĩciĩ yao

4. Compact reference — possessive prefix mapping by class

Noun class Singular prefix Plural prefix Example (my)
Mũ- (People) wa- a- mũndũ wakwa
