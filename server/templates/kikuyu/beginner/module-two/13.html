2.5.1 Introduction

Definition (formal)

  • Pronouns are linguistic items that replace nouns to avoid repetition and to render sentences shorter, clearer, and more cohesive.
  • In Kikuyu (Gĩkũyũ), pronouns operate both syntactically (as subjects, objects) and morphologically (they are frequently incorporated into verb forms).

Principal types (categorical overview)

  • Personal Pronouns — refer to specific persons or groups (I, you, he, we, they).
  • Possessive Pronouns — indicate ownership (mine, yours, theirs).
  • Demonstrative Pronouns — point to entities in the deictic space (this, that).
  • Interrogative Pronouns — form questions (who, what, where, why, when).
  • Relative Pronouns — connect subordinate clauses to antecedents.

Canonical subject-form examples (subject pronouns)

  • Niĩ — I
  • Wĩ — You (singular)
  • Nĩwe / E — He / She / It (gender-neutral in Gĩkũyũ)
  • Ithue / We — We / us
  • Inyũe / ũyũ — You (plural) (variants present in the data)
  • Acio — They / them

Pronoun behaviour & usage notes (functional points)

  • Headedness in verbs: Many Kikuyu verbs encode the subject; the independent pronoun may be omitted because the verb already marks person.

    Example given: Ũgũcokia = "You will return" (analysis: Wĩ + gũcokia).

  • Emphatic usage: A pronoun placed before the verb adds emphasis or contrast.

    Example given: Niĩ nĩgũgothomera — "I will read for you." (Niĩ emphasises the subject "I".)

  • Gender neutrality: The third-person singular can appear as Nĩwe or E; both function for "he" and "she" (the language does not grammatically mark gender here).

Interrogative pronouns (core items provided)

  • Ũũ — Who
  • Kĩĩ — What
  • Kũũ — Where
  • Rĩĩ — Why
  • Rĩrĩa — When
