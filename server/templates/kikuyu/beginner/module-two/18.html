2.6.1 Summary of Pronouns (Part 01)

A. Demonstrative Pronouns in Kikuyu

Definition

Pronouns used to point out or refer to specific nouns.

Their function is to locate a noun in space, time, or discourse, distinguishing what is near, far, or conceptually emphasized.

They work relationally: relative to the speaker, listener, or both.

1. Singular Demonstratives (pointing to a single item/person)

ũyũ → This (near the speaker)

Immediate, tactile, personal proximity.

Example: Mũndũ ũyũ nĩ mũthoni. (This person is Muthoni.)

ũcio → That (near the listener)

Within the listener's reach or field of awareness.

Example: Mwana ũcio nĩ mũciĩ. (That child is at home.)

ũrĩa → That over there (far from both speaker and listener)

Remote, possibly visible but detached.

Example: Mũndũ ũrĩa nĩ mũhunjĩ. (That person over there is a preacher.)

2. Plural Demonstratives (pointing to multiple persons/items)

aya → These (near the speaker)

Example: Andũ aya nĩ athomi. (These people are students.)

acio → Those (near the listener)

Example: Acio nĩ arata akwa. (Those are my friends.)

arĩa → Those (far from both speaker and listener)

Example: Arĩa me ndũnyũ. (Those are at the market over there.)

3. Demonstratives in Interrogatives (Question Form)

  • Ũyũ nũũ? → Who is this?
  • Ũcio nũũ? → Who is that?
  • Ũrĩa nũũ? → Who is that (far over there)?

These forms reinforce the role of demonstratives in identification and reference-tracking in discourse.

4. Demonstratives with Nouns

Demonstratives may precede or follow the noun:

  • ũyũ mũndũ → This person
  • mũndũ ũyũ → This person (with emphasis; highlights contrast or specification).

5. Demonstratives Indicating Place (Spatial Reference)

  • haha → Here (proximal, speaker's space)
  • haria → There (proximal to listener)
  • kũrĩa → Over there (remote, distal to both speaker and listener)

6. Demonstratives Indicating Time (Temporal Reference)

  • ũmũthĩ → Today (present reference)
  • rũciũ → Tomorrow (future, immediate)
  • ira → Yesterday (past, immediate)
  • igũrũ → Day after tomorrow (future, extended)

Pedagogical Note:

Kikuyu demonstratives are multidimensional:

They do not merely "point"; they encode proximity, direction, temporal scope, and discourse emphasis.

This makes them key in building shared reference, storytelling, and argumentation.

B. Distributive Pronouns in Kikuyu

Definition

Pronouns that divide attention among individuals or items in a group.

They distribute reference across entities, emphasizing individual treatment, total inclusiveness, or unspecified openness.

1. "Each One" (Person)

Mũndũ ũmwe na ũmwe → Each person individually

Function: Assigns agency/action to each individual within a group.

Example: Mũndũ ũmwe na ũmwe nĩ aĩrĩĩĩre. (Each person spoke.)

2. "Each One" (Thing)

Kĩndũ kĩmwe na kĩmwe → Each thing individually

Function: Marks independent identity of each item.

Example: Kĩndũ kĩmwe na kĩmwe nĩ kĩhũthagĩra. (Each thing has a purpose.)

3. "Any One" (Person)

Mũndũ o wothe → Any one person (unspecified)

Function: General openness; inclusion of any possible individual.

Example: Mũndũ o wothe no aũke. (Anyone can come.)

4. "Any One" (Thing)

Kĩndũ o kĩothe → Any one thing (random)

Function: Non-specific reference to an item; encourages choice.

Example: Hoya kĩndũ o kĩothe. (Ask for anything.)

5. "All" (Total Inclusiveness)

-othe (prefix varies with noun class) → All, absolutely all.

Function: Absolute, collective reference.

Example: Andũ othe nĩmegũka. (All the people will come.)

6. "Anything" (Unlimited Openness)

Kĩndũ kĩothe → Anything (open-ended)

Function: Emphasizes total openness in choice or possibility.

Example: Tũhikanire kĩndũ kĩothe. (We own anything.)

7. "Something" (Indefinite Specificity)

Kĩndũ → Something (non-specific)

Function: Signals existence of an unspecified but real item/event.

Example: Nĩngũhoroirie kĩndũ. (I heard something.)
