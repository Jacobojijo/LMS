2.3.5 Noun Construct

1. What is a noun construct?

  • A noun construct in Kikuyu is a morphological form of a noun specially prepared to receive suffixes (e.g., demonstratives, possessives).
  • It is used to modify or describe nouns more precisely by providing a stable stem to which descriptive elements attach.
  • Noun constructs exist for both singular and plural nouns; formation rules differ for the two.

2. How to form the noun construct

A. Regular noun-construct formation (singular)

Rule (succinct):

  1. Start from the plural noun.
  2. Remove the final vowel of the plural to produce the singular noun construct.

Procedure (stepwise):

  • Step 1 — identify the plural form of the noun.
  • Step 2 — drop the final vowel of that plural form.
  • Step 3 — the resulting string is the singular noun construct (usually shown with a trailing hyphen to indicate a stem ready for suffixation).

Illustrative table (regular cases):

English Singular Plural Singular Noun Construct
tree mũtĩ miti mit-
goat mbũri mbũri mbũr-
farm mũgũnda mĩgũnda mĩgũnd-
banana irigu marigu marig-

B. Exception cases

  • Some nouns deviate from the simple "drop-final-vowel" pattern; their construct-stems must be learned as exceptions.

Exception table:

English Singular Plural Singular Noun Construct
house nyũmba nyũmba nyũmb-
woman mũtumia atumia atumi-
person mũndũ andũ and-
friend mũrata arata arat-

Note: exception stems may look like truncated plural forms or like truncated singular forms — the important point is the construct-stem printed above (with hyphen) is what suffixes attach to.

C. Plural noun construct

  • General rule: the plural construct is usually identical to the plural noun itself (i.e., the plural noun can function as its own construct).
  • Exception: some plural nouns remain unchanged when becoming constructs (they already end in a form ready for suffixation).

Regular examples:

English Plural Noun Plural Construct
farms mĩgũnda mĩgũnda-
bananas marigu marigu-

Irregular examples (no visible change):

English Plural Noun Plural Construct
people andũ andũ-
women atumia atumia-

3. Uses of the noun construct

A. Demonstrative descriptions

  • Demonstratives are attached to noun constructs via demonstrative suffixes to express distinctions such as this, that (near), that (far), these, those (near), those (far).
Demonstrative suffixes Meaning Suffix
This -ĩno
That (near) -ĩyo
That (far) -ĩrĩa
These -ĩcĩĩ
Those (near) -ĩcĩo
Those (far) -ĩrĩa

Examples with "mũtĩ" (tree)

Noun Construct Suffix Word Translation
mit- -ĩno mĩtĩno these trees
mit- -ĩyo mĩtĩyo those trees (near)
mit- -ĩrĩa mĩtĩrĩa those trees (far)
mũtĩ- -ũyũ mũtĩyũ this tree
mũtĩ- -ũciũ mũtĩũciũ that tree (near)
mũtĩ- -ũrĩa mũtĩũrĩa that tree (far)

Observe: both the singular construct (e.g., mit-) and the full singular noun with hyphen (e.g., mũtĩ-) appear in the example set; the given forms show how demonstrative suffixes attach in real-word patterns.

B. Possessive constructions

  • The noun construct serves as the base for attaching possessive suffixes.
  • Full details on possessive suffixation are to be covered in the possessives section (coming later).

4. Summary — Construct formation rules (compact)

  • Singular construct (regular): Start with the plural noun → drop the final vowel.
    • Example: mĩgũnda → mĩgũnd-.
  • Plural construct: Usually identical to the plural noun (simply add a hyphen when using as a stem).
    • Example: marigu → marigu-.
  • Exceptions exist and must be learned individually (e.g., nyũmba → nyũmb- ; mũndũ → and-).
