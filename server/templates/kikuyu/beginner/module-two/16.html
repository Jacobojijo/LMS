2.5.4 Demonstrative pronouns

1. Definition — what demonstratives do

  • Demonstrative pronouns point out specific people, objects, places or times.
  • Functionally equivalent to English this / that / these / those.
  • They encode deictic relation — relative proximity to speaker, listener, or both.
  • Used in identification, description, reference and question formation.

2. Singular demonstratives — forms, use and examples (table + notes)

Kikuyu English equivalent Usage context Example
ũyũ this (near speaker) Refers to something close to the speaker Mwana ũyũ nĩ mũthoni → This child is Muthoni
ũcio that (near listener) Refers to something near the listener / not the speaker Nyũmba icio nĩ ya Kamau → Those houses are Kamau's
ũrĩa that over there (far) Refers to something far from both speaker and listener Mũndũ ũrĩa nĩ mũhunjĩ → That person over there is a teacher

Key point: choice among ũyũ / ũcio / ũrĩa is a choice of proximity (near speaker → near listener → far from both).

Usage note (from examples): singular demonstratives may appear immediately after the noun (Mwana ũyũ) or before it (ũyũ mũndũ), depending on focus and emphasis (see 5).

3. Plural demonstratives — forms, use and examples (table + notes)

Kikuyu English equivalent Usage context Example
aya these (near speaker) Near the person speaking Aya me nyũmba → These are in the house.
acio those (near listener) Near the person listening Acio nĩ arata aku → Those are your friends
arĩa those (far away) Far from both speaker & listener Arĩa me andũ a mũciĩ → Those are homemates over there

Parallel system: plural forms mirror the singular deictic distinctions (speaker / listener / far).

Practical effect: choose aya / acio / arĩa to index a group rather than a single entity.

4. Demonstratives in questions (forms and samples)

Demonstratives appear in interrogative contexts to ask identity or description. Examples from the source:

  • ũyũ no? → "Who is this?"
  • Nĩkĩ kĩ? → "What is that?"
  • Kĩu nĩ kĩ? → "What is that over there?"

Function: combine demonstrative deictic reference with interrogative force to seek identification.

5. Positioning with nouns — before vs after the noun (rules illustrated by examples)

Before the noun — neutral identification:

  • ũyũ mũndũ → "this person"

After the noun — added emphasis or specification:

  • mũndũ ũyũ → "this person" (emphatic)

Other placements (illustrative):

  • gĩthaka kĩu → "that garden"
  • nyũmba ĩrĩa → "that house over there"

Teaching point: ordering signals focus — placing the demonstrative after the noun highlights or narrows the referent.

6. Demonstratives combined with possessives

Demonstrative + possessive = fine-grained identification

  • Mwana ũyũ wakwa → this child of mine
  • gĩthaka k kĩu gĩaku → that garden of yours (as given)
  • andũ arĩa ao → those people of theirs

Practical effect: the demonstrative localises the referent; the possessive assigns ownership or association.

7. Representative specific demonstrative examples (compact list)

  • mũndũ ũyũ → this person
  • mwana ũcio → that child
  • mwana ũrĩa → that child over there
  • andũ aya → these people
  • mĩtĩ irĩa → those trees (far)

8. Demonstratives for place — deictic locatives (table + examples)

Kikuyu (place) English Example
haha here (very near speaker) ũyũnĩ mũciĩ wakwa → here is my home
haria there (near listener) ĩrĩa nĩ nyũmba ya Kamau → there is Kamau's house
kũrĩa over there / far away kurĩa nĩ kĩwanja → over there is a field

Usage: place-demonstratives locate entities in physical space relative to speech participants.

9. Demonstratives for time — temporal deixis (table + example)

Kikuyu (time) English
ũmũthĩ today
rũciũ tomorrow
ira yesterday
igũrũ day after tomorrow
rũciinĩ in the morning

Example sentences from source:

  • Tũgũcooka ũmũthĩ → We will return today.
  • Nĩgakũona rũciũ → I will see you tomorrow.

10. Demonstratives in action — discourse effects and sample statements (analysis + examples)

Specify identity / evaluate: demonstratives can identify and comment on the referent.

  • ũyũ nĩ mũndũ mwega → this is a good person

Categorize social status or role:

  • arĩa nĩ athamaki → those are chiefs / kings

Express ownership with deictic precision:

  • gĩthaka kĩu gĩakwa → that garden is mine
