4.6.1 Introductions

A. Purpose of the Chapter

This section explains how to:

  • Affirm (say yes) and negate (say no) statements in Kikuyu.
  • Use grammatical forms to express agreement, denial, or refusal.
  • Form polite responses in daily speech and conversation.
  • Apply negation and affirmation across different tenses and commands.

B. Core Vocabulary and Meanings

Expression Meaning in English Usage Context
Ĩĩ / Nĩwega Yes Affirmation or agreement
Aa / Tĩ / Ũtĩ No Negation or denial
Tĩ Not Negative particle before verb
Tĩrĩ There is/are not Negative existential

C. Affirmation Structures (Gũthomithia)

1. Simple Affirmative Responses

Used to confirm or agree with a statement or question.

Examples:

Kikuyu English Translation
Nĩ ũũĩ Mũrĩmi? Do you know Mũrĩmi?
Ĩĩ, nĩ ndĩmũũĩ Yes, I know him.
Nĩwega. It is good / Yes (polite agreement).
Ĩĩ, ndĩ haha. Yes, I am here.

💡 "Ĩĩ" and "Nĩwega" are polite affirmations showing agreement or positive confirmation.

D. Negation Structures (Gũtiganĩria)

1. Standard Negation with "Tĩ" or "nd"

"Tĩ" or "nd" is placed before the verb to show denial or negation.

Structural Formula:

"Tĩ" or "nd"+ Verb (in correct tense)

Examples:

Kikuyu Meaning
Ndíũí I don't know.
Ndarĩ haha. He/She is not here.
Ndũrathii. You are not going.
Tĩ gĩkeno. It is not happiness.

💡 "Tĩ" = not / no (used to negate statements).

The common, polite, and standard way to express negation in a response is generally to use Aca (No) followed by the grammatically negative verb form. The negative form of the verb itself is usually marked by a prefix like nd- or t- (or other forms based on person/class and tense) and sometimes a change in the final vowel, often to -a.

2. Response-Based Negation (Polite Form) - Corrected Kikuyu

Kikuyu (Corrected) English Grammatical Notes
Aca, ndĩkũthiĩ. No, I will not go. The polite "No" is Aca. Ndĩkũthiĩ is the negative future tense (I will not go/I won't go). Nd- is the negative marker, -ĩ- is the subject prefix 'I', -kũ- is the future marker.
Aca, ndũũĩ. No, you don't know. Aca (No). Ndũũĩ is the negative present/habitual of the verb kũũĩ (to know). Nd- is the negative marker, -ũ- is the subject prefix 'you (singular)'. The verb root ũĩ changes from ũĩ in the affirmative.
Aca, ndũrathiĩ. No, you are not going. Aca (No). Ndũrathiĩ is the negative progressive/present continuous tense (You are not going). Nd- is the negative marker, -ũ- is 'you (singular)', -ra- is the progressive/present marker.

Explanation of the Original Examples' Issues

The original examples had the following issues:

"Aa": While similar in sound to 'yes', Aca is the correct, polite word for 'No' in Gĩkũyũ.

"Tĩ":

  • Tĩ is a negative marker for the copula/stative verbs (e.g., Nĩ mũndũ - He is a person; Tĩ mũndũ - He is not a person).
  • It is sometimes used with certain subject prefixes in specific negative tenses, but not generally as the main particle for all negative responses.
  • Aa, tĩ ũkũmenya (No, you don't know) is grammatically incorrect. The verb kũmenya (to know) in its negative present/habitual form is usually ndũũĩ.

Key Takeaway: For polite, response-based negation in Gĩkũyũ, always use Aca followed by the verb correctly inflected in its negative form (e.g., nd- or t- + subject concord + tense/aspect marker + verb root).

3. Past Negation

For past actions, Kikuyu uses Ndathii or Ndathii tondũ tĩ…

No contraction is used like in Dholuo.

Examples:

Kikuyu Meaning
Ndinathii cukuru. I did not go to school.
Tũtiarĩ na mũgũnda. We did not have a farm.

4. Present & Future Negation

"Tĩ" remains before the verb regardless of tense.

Examples:

Kikuyu Meaning
Ndĩrathii. I am not going.
Ndĩkũthiĩ. I will not go.
Tũtirathoma. We are not studying.

5. Negative with "Tĩrĩ" (Existential Negation)

"Tĩrĩ" expresses there is no / there are no.

Examples:

Kikuyu Meaning
Gutĩrĩ mũndũ haha. There is no one here.
Gutĩrĩ mbeca. There is no money.
Gutĩrĩ gĩkeno. There is no joy.

6. Negative Imperative (Command Form)

Use "tiga" to say "Don't…"

Examples:

Kikuyu Meaning
Tiga gũũka! Don't come!
Tiga kũrĩa! Don't ask!
Tiga kũũhũũra! Don't wash!
Tiga kkũhoya! Don't pray!

💡 "Tiga" softens commands while maintaining firmness — commonly used by elders or teachers.
