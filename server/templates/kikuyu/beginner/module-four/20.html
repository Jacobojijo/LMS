4.14.1 Summary of Nomenclature, Preposition & Conjunctions

Expressions and Proverbs

Introductions

I. Cultural Role of Expressions

  • Kikuyu expressions reflect daily life, community values, and wisdom passed through generations.

II. Proverbs

  • Short sayings used to teach lessons or give advice.
  • Example: "Wendo wakwa ni wendo wa maitha" – Love for me is love for the ancestors.

III. Idioms

  • Figurative phrases that convey deeper meaning.
  • Example: "Githomo kia mũthenya ni kĩrĩa kĩa wendo" – Knowledge of the wise is like love itself.

IV. Conclusion

  • Expressions and proverbs are central in passing wisdom and etiquette in Kikuyu culture.

Kikuyu Nomenclature

Naming System

I. Introduction

  • Names reflect birth circumstances, events, and family lineage.

II. Temporal Names

  • Names based on the time of birth.
  • Example: Mwangi – born in the morning.

III. Event Names

  • Names reflecting events at birth.
  • Example: Gathoni – born during planting season.

IV. Place Names

  • Names indicating the place of birth.
  • Example: Mũkũyũ – born near a hill.

V. Phenomenon Names

  • Names inspired by natural occurrences.
  • Example: Njogu – born during a harvest of nuts.

VI. Twin Names

  • Special names given to twins.
  • Example: Wanjiku and Wambui.

VII. Inheritance Names

  • Names passed down through ancestors.
  • Example: Njoroge – common family lineage name.

VIII. Summary

  • Kikuyu names preserve culture, history, and social connections.

Price Asking

Introductions

I. Concept

  • Asking prices is part of trading and social interaction.

II. Asking Price

  • Politely ask: "Ni gĩkoho kĩrĩa?" – How much is this?

III. Negotiation

  • Use phrases like "Hinya mũno?" – Can it be cheaper?
  • Discuss discounts respectfully.

IV. Cultural Insight

  • Price discussions are common in markets; politeness maintains good relationships.

V. Summary

  • Asking prices requires language skills and understanding of social etiquette.

Preposition

Introductions

I. Definition

  • Prepositions show relationships between nouns, pronouns, and other words.

II. Core Vocabulary

  • Na – with
  • Kũrĩ – in/at
  • Kuuma – from

III. Semantic Distinctions

  • Prepositions can indicate location, direction, time, or manner.

IV. Expressing Companionship

  • Example: Ndĩna wendo na mũthoni – I am with a friend.

V. Possessive Expression

  • Example: Gĩkũyũ kĩa mũndũ – The thing of a person.

VI. Cultural Insight

  • Prepositions help explain kinship, location, and movement in stories and daily life.

VII. Classroom Activities

  • Identify prepositions in sentences.
  • Create sentences using core prepositions.

Relative Pronouns and Causal Connectives

I. Introduction

  • Relative pronouns link clauses; causal connectives show reason.

II. Relative Pronouns Examples

  • Mũndũ ayo – The person who…
  • Mũno wacio – The thing that…

III. Causal Connectives Examples

  • Ngwĩ – because
  • Na ũgĩkũyũ – therefore

IV. Usage in Sentences

  • Ndĩna githomo kĩrĩa mũndũ ayo agĩtũmĩte – I have the book that the person sent.
  • Na ũgĩkũyũ, tũrĩa twakũrĩa kũgĩa – Therefore, we decided to act.
