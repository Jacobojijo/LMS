4.17.1 Summary of Moods, Writing and Reading.

Moods

Introductions

In Kikuyu grammar, mood shows a verb's function in a sentence, indicating the speaker's attitude or intention toward the action or state. There are three main moods: indicative, imperative, and subjunctive.

Indicative Mood

Expresses a fact or statement, either as a declaration or question.

Most commonly used in everyday speech.

Example: "Njau ĩrarĩra." – The calf is crying.

Example: "Wenda kũnyua kahua?" – Do you like/want coffee?

Imperative Mood

Expresses a command or request, usually implying "you" as the subject.

Example: "Hingũra mũrango." – Open the door.

Example: "Teithia mũndũ ũyũ." – Please help this person.

Subjunctive Mood

Expresses a wish, hypothetical situation, suggestion, or condition contrary to fact.

Less common in daily speech, more frequent in formal writing.

Example: "Ndĩyũrĩtie nĩnjũragĩre kuo." – I wish I could run there.

Example: "Wangĩkorirwo nĩ we, ndũngĩĩkire ũguo." – If I were you, I would not do that.

Example: "Nĩndĩrahe ũhoro mũndũ ũcio aume." – I suggest that the man leave.

Indicative Mood

I. Definition

The Indicative Mood expresses facts, real events, and certainties across three time frames:

  • Past – what has already happened
  • Present – what is happening now
  • Future – what will happen

II. Structural Overview

Verbs are regular in form, allowing predictable conjugation.

Temporal distinction is marked by auxiliary particles:

Past {-a-} / {-ire} nĩ-na-, nĩ-nĩr-

IV. Morphological Notes

  • Past tense uses {-a-} or suffix {-ire}
  • Present Continuous uses {-ra-}
  • Future uses {-gũ-}, reflecting approaching events

Conditional Mood

I. Definition

Expresses cause-and-effect, dependency, or hypothetical situations.

Functions like English "If… then…"

Core Pattern:

Ningĩ + Present Tense Verb + (No nginya) + Future Tense Verb

  • Ningĩ = if
  • No nginya = then (optional)

Example:
Ningĩ wũka, no nginya ngũone. → If you come, then I will see you.

Negative Conditional:

Ningĩ waga kũcooka, no nginya ndingĩkuona → If you do not return, then I will not see you.

Past / Remote Past Conditional:

Ningĩ ndĩmenyete atĩ mũciĩ mũgũthoma, ningĩ ndathire hamwe → If I had known the meeting was starting, I would have gone.

Future Conditional:

Ningĩ ũka rũciũ, no nginya ningũkuhe → If he will come tomorrow, I will give him.

Subjunctive Mood

I. Definition & Core Function

Expresses non-factual, unrealized, or wished-for events.

Covers hope, probability, obligation, polite requests, and hypothetical reasoning.

II. Common Contexts

  • Possibility/Probability: oyũ, ũka, ũũgũ, ndingĩ…
  • Polite Requests: ndakũhoya, ndakũingĩra, ũka…
  • Prayers/Blessings: Ngai akũhe wega → May God give you goodness.

III. Tone-Based Meaning

  • Firm tone = command
  • Soft tone = polite request

IV. Imperative / Future-Imperative Constructions

  • Softened forms: Ũka ũthiĩ → You will go
  • Compound commands: Tũka tũthome → We shall study

Writing & Reading in Kikuyu

Introductions

  • Phonemic Awareness: Written forms match pronunciation (e.g., mũrũthi).
  • Tone Awareness: High vs low tone distinguishes meaning (mũthoni).
  • Orthography: Roman alphabet, use of Ĩ and Ũ, vowel length (a vs aa).
  • Grammar & Sentence Structure: SVO order, verbs encode TAM and negation.
