4.3.2 Indirect Complement

1. Definition and Function

An indirect complement in Kikuyu shows the person who receives or benefits from an action, instead of the direct object. It represents the recipient or beneficiary of what is being given, said, or done.

It helps show transfer or communication — where something is given to someone or done for someone.

2. Sentence Structure: Subject + Verb + Indirect Complement + Direct Object

Pattern:

  • Subject → Verb → Recipient (Indirect Complement) → Thing Given (Direct Object)

The indirect complement answers "To whom?" or "For whom?" The direct object answers "What?"

3. Key Characteristics

  • These sentences usually have two objects: one receives (indirect) and one thing is given (direct).
  • The verb often shows giving, sending, or telling.
  • The order helps identify who receives what.

4. Examples and Explanations

Example 1: Kamau aheire Wanjiku kĩheo. Translation: Kamau gave Wanjiku a gift.

  • Subject: Kamau
  • Verb: aheire (gave)
  • Indirect Complement: Wanjiku (recipient)
  • Direct Object: kĩheo (gift)

✅ The gift is given to Wanjiku by Kamau.

Example 2: Nĩndaheire Mũriũ ibuku. Translation: I gave Mũriũ a book.

  • Subject: ndá (I)
  • Verb: heire (gave)
  • Indirect Complement: Mũriũ (recipient)
  • Direct Object: ibuku (book)

✅ The book is what was given; Mũriũ is who received it.

Example 3: Mwarĩ amũheire mami kĩgwa. Translation: The daughter gave her mother sugarcane.

  • Subject: Mwarĩ (daughter)
  • Verb: amũheire (gave)
  • Indirect Complement: mami (her mother)
  • Direct Object: kĩgwa (sugarcane)

✅ The action of giving is done to the mother.

Example 4: Kamau aheirwo kĩheo kuuma Wanjiku. Translation: Kamau received the gift from Wanjiku.

  • Subject: Kamau
  • Verb: aheirwo (received)
  • Direct Object: kĩheo (gift)
  • Prepositional Indirect Complement: kuuma Wanjiku (from Wanjiku)

✅ The gift comes from Wanjiku to Kamau.

5. Semantic Roles of Indirect Complements

  • Recipient Role: shows who receives the object (e.g., Wanjiku in Kamau aheire Wanjiku kĩheo).
  • Beneficiary Role: shows who benefits from the action (e.g., amũheire mami gĩkũyũ).
  • Source Role: shows where something comes from (e.g., kuuma Wanjiku = from Wanjiku).

6. Structural Notes

  • When the action is receptive (like "receive" or "take"), the indirect complement is introduced by "kuuma" (from).
  • The indirect complement usually comes before the direct object when it's inside the same verb clause.
  • The subject marker (like a-, nĩ-, mũ-) appears inside the verb to match the doer of the action.
