4.7.1 Introductions

A. Purpose of the Section

This section introduces how questions are formed and answered in Kikuyu. It covers:

  • How to ask different types of questions (who, what, where, when, why, how).
  • How to use question tags to seek confirmation or politely challenge a statement.
  • How to form both affirmative and negative questions across tenses.
  • The social and polite tone of Kikuyu questioning, reflecting cultural respect.

B. General Characteristics of Kikuyu Questions

Tone-Based:

Kikuyu relies heavily on intonation (raising tone) at the end of a question to show inquiry.

Example:

  • Niũrathii? → Are you going?
  • Ndũrathii? → Aren't you going?

Question Words (Mawatho ma Kũũria):

Kikuyu has specific interrogative words for different kinds of questions — identity, reason, time, quantity, and place.

C. Essential Interrogative Vocabulary

Function Kikuyu Term Meaning in English Example

Where Kũrĩ? / Kũũ? / Haha? Where Ũrathii kũũ? (Where are you going?)

💡 Note: Most Kikuyu questions begin with a question word or simply raise the tone of a normal statement to make it interrogative.

E. Question Tags in Kikuyu (Mũhuro wa Kũũria)

1. Purpose

Used to:

  • Seek confirmation of a statement.
  • Politely invite agreement or verify information.
  • Maintain social harmony in conversation.

2. Common Kikuyu Question Tag Forms

Kikuyu Tag Meaning / Use Example Sentence (Kikuyu) English Translation
Ti hĩndĩ? Isn't it so? / Right? (Most common, neutral tag) Ũra thii, ti hĩndĩ? You are going, aren't you?
Ti ũguo? Isn't that so? / Is that not it? (Slightly more formal/literal) Gũkũ nĩ kũmũthĩ, ti ũguo? It's cold here, isn't that so?
Ũ-tĩ? Isn't it (for 2nd person singular)? (Dialectal/informal) Ũra thii, ũtĩ? You are going, aren't you?
Ti we? Isn't it you? (Identity/Subject focus) Nĩwe ũgũthii, ti we? It's you who is going, isn't it?
Nĩ atĩa? What is it? (Used after a simple affirmative) Nĩ mũthĩ, nĩ atĩa? It's good, is it what? (Meaning: It's good, right?)

💡 Cultural Note: Tag questions in Kikuyu often serve to soften statements, invite agreement, or express courtesy. They are common in polite, respectful, or friendly conversation.

F. Negative Question Forms (Kũũria na Kũtiganĩria)

Kikuyu negative questions are formed by adding nd- / t- before the verb:

Structure Example (Kikuyu) English Translation
Ndũ + Verb Ndũrathii? Aren't you going?
Tũtĩ + Verb Tũtĩrathoma? Aren't we reading?
Tĩ + Copula Tĩ gĩkeno, ũtĩ? It's not happiness, is it?

These forms are not accusatory — they are used to check politely, often with a tone of concern or curiosity.

G. Question–Response Examples

Question (Kikuyu) Response (Kikuyu) Meaning in English
Nĩũrathii? Ĩĩ, nĩndĩrathii. Are you going? – Yes, I am going.
Ũrathii kũũ? Nĩndĩrathii cũkũrũ. Where are you going? – I'm going to school.
Nũũrathiĩ? Nĩ Mũrĩmi. Who is going? – It's Mũrĩmi.
Ũrathiĩ rĩ? kũthiĩ ndarĩkia kũthoma. When will you go? – When I finish reading.
Ũthiĩ nĩkĩ? Tondũ ndĩna wendo. Why are you going? – Because I want to.
Nĩwega, ũtĩ? Ĩĩ, nĩwega. It's good, isn't it? – Yes, it is good.

I. Cultural Reflection

In Kikuyu culture:

  • Questions are not just grammatical tools — they show respect, curiosity, and courtesy.
  • The tone and form determine politeness.
  • Direct questions are softened using "ũtĩ" (isn't it?) or "nĩwega" (it's good, right?).
  • Among elders, polite phrasing like "Nĩwega ũtĩ?" is preferred over blunt questioning.
