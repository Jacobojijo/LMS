3.1.2 Adjective of Quality and Quantity

1. Adjectives of Quality in Kikuyu

Adjectives of quality describe the character, state, morality, or appearance of nouns. In Kikuyu, these adjectives are semantically loaded—they do not merely state a condition, but they embed social, moral, and evaluative judgments.

1.1 Mũega / Mũega muno

Gloss: good, beautiful, nice, attractive.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Moral uprightness: describes virtue, good character.
  • Aesthetic beauty: physical attractiveness, pleasing appearance.
  • General pleasantness: something admirable or agreeable.

Examples:

  • Mwarĩ mũthaka → "A beautiful/good girl."
  • Njĩra njega → "A good road."
  • Mũndũ mũega muno → "A very good person (morally upright and admirable)."

1.2 Mũũru / Mũũru muno

Gloss: bad, ugly, undesirable, evil.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Describes immorality, wickedness, or evil character.
  • Can also denote physical unattractiveness or unpleasantness.

Examples:

  • Mũndũ mũũru → "A bad/evil person."
  • Wendo mũũru muno → "A very bad relationship (toxic, undesirable)."

1.3 Mũrũrũ

Gloss: bitter, harsh, unpleasant.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Literal taste (bitter fruit or food).
  • Figurative meaning (harsh life experiences, sorrowful days).

Examples:

  • Mũtũnda mũrũrũ → "Bitter fruit tree."
  • Mũthenya mũrũrũ → "A bitter day/experience."

1.4 Mũhũũru

Gloss: dead, lifeless.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Refers to death in both human and non-human entities.
  • Symbolizes loss, lack of vitality, or the end of usefulness.

Examples:

  • Mũtĩ mũkuo→ "A dead tree."
  • Mũndũ mũkuo → "A dead person."

2. Adjectives of Quantity in Kikuyu

Adjectives of quantity describe amount, size, weight, or sufficiency. They measure "how much" or "how many", but also carry metaphorical extensions—for instance, mũnene can imply "importance" beyond "bigness."

2.1 Mũnene

Gloss: big, large.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Literal: physical size.
  • Figurative: importance, status, maturity.

Examples:

  • Nyũmba nene → "A big house."
  • Mũndũ mũnene → "An important/grown person."

2.2 Nyingi

Gloss: many, a lot.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Applies to countable nouns (children, trees, objects).
  • Denotes abundance without exaggeration.

Examples:

  • Ciana nyingi → "Many children."
  • Mĩtĩ mĩingi → "Many trees."

2.3 Nini

Gloss: few, little, small.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Denotes scarcity or small quantity.
  • Can describe size (small objects) or number.

Examples:

  • Ciana nini → "Few children."
  • Mũtĩ mũnini → "A small tree."

2.4 -ritũ

Gloss: heavy, weighty.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Physical heaviness.
  • Figurative: difficult or burdensome.

Examples:

  • Wĩra mũritũ → "A heavy work (difficult task)."
  • Gĩtĩ kĩritũ → "Heavy chair."

2.5 Mũhuthu

Gloss: easy, simple, not difficult.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Literal: not hard, not complicated.
  • Figurative: soft, gentle, effortless.

Examples:

  • Kiama kihuthu → "An easy task."
  • Wira mũhuthu → "Easy work."

2.6 Nyingi muno

Gloss: abundant, overflowing, very many.

Expanded Meaning:

  • Extreme abundance, often implying excess or plenty.
  • Used for emphatic expression of largeness in number or amount.

Examples:

  • Ciana nyingi muno → "Very many children."
  • Mbembe nyingi muno → "Plenty of maize (overflowing harvest)."

3. Interrogative Quantifiers in Kikuyu

Kikuyu also uses special question-quantifiers to ask about number, size, or extent.

  • Igana? = How many?
    • Ciana cigana? → "How many children?"
    • Mĩtĩ igana? → "How many trees?"
  • Nene atĩa? = How big?
    • Nyũmba nene atĩa? → "How big is the house?"
    • Gĩti kinene atĩa? → "How big is the chair?"
