3.8.5 Past Perfect Tense

1. Definition & Grammatical Function

Core function: expresses an action/state that had already occurred before another past event — i.e., pastness + completion prior to a past reference point.

Pragmatic role: crucial in narratives and sequences — sets background events or the earlier step in a chain of past events.

Analytic summary: combines temporal anchoring (past) with a completion operator (already done) relative to some other past time.

2. Morphological Structure

2.1 Composite construction (formula)

Canonical formula:

Nĩ- + subject-prefix + verb-root + -irĩte / -etĩte
(optionally accompanied by a temporal adverbial or subordinate "before" clause to mark the earlier event)

2.2 Key structural notes

  • The prefix Nĩ- continues to mark past/affirmative in the composite form.
  • The -irĩte / -etĩte ending specifically emphasizes completion prior to another past event (i.e., true Past Perfect semantics).
  • Temporal markers such as mbere ya ("before …") frequently co-occur to make the sequence explicit.

2.3 Illustrative (user-provided) constructions

  • Nĩndarĩte mbere ya gũcooka. — "I had eaten before returning."
  • Nĩarutirĩte mbere ya mũthenya ũcio. — "He/She had dug before that day."

3. Subject-concord paradigms

English Kikuyu (past perfect)
I had eaten Nĩndarĩte
You had eaten Nĩwarĩte
He/She had eaten Nĩarĩte
We had eaten Nĩtwarĩte
You (pl.) had … Nĩmwarĩte
They had eaten Nĩmarĩte

Important note: the variant ending -irĩte / -etĩte is the explicit Past-Perfect marker used to show an event was completed before another past event.

4. Semantic characteristics

  • Temporal ordering: event A (past perfect) ⟹ completed before event B (reference past).
  • Narrative function: used to position background situations or antecedent events prior to a focal past action.
  • Result-state interpretation: often implicates that the earlier event has consequences or relevance at the later past reference point (e.g., "They had eaten (so) …").

Example from the data:

Nĩmarĩte irio mbere ya gũkoma. — "They had eaten before they fell asleep."

here eating (past perfect) precedes and conditions the later falling-asleep event.

5. Syntactic features & patterns (how it appears in clauses)

Clause template:

Nĩ- + subject-prefix + verb-root + -irĩte/-etĩte + (subordinate clause / temporal adverbial)

Temporal subordinators commonly used with Past Perfect: mbere ya ("before …"), sometimes tene (in other contexts).

Sequential examples (user data):

  • Nĩndarĩte mbere ya gũthii soko. — "I had eaten before going to the market."
  • Nĩarĩmĩte mbere ya rũĩ kũihũra. — "He had dug before the river flooded."

7. Linguistic observations

  • Affirmative past marker: Nĩ- persists as the anchor for past/affirmative forms.
  • Distinctive ending: -irĩte / -etĩte functions to differentiate Past Perfect from other past forms such as Simple Past (-ire) and Extended Past (-ete).
  • Contextual anchoring: subordinate temporal expressions (mbere ya, etc.) frequently co-occur and are pragmatically important to signal the earlier-than-past relation.
  • Narrative sequencing: Past Perfect is the preferred device to set a background prior to another past event.
