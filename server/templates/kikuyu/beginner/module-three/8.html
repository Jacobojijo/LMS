3.4.2 Infinitive verbs

1. The Indicative Mood in Kikuyu

Function:

  • States facts, describes reality, and makes declarative statements.

Form:

  • Combines personal pronouns with verbs.
  • The verb to be (nĩ/kũrĩ) plays a special role.

2. Infinitive Verbs in Kikuyu

Most infinitives begin with kũ-.

Core examples:

  • kũmenya → to know
  • kũhoya → to pray
  • kũhanda → to plant
  • kũigua → to hear
  • kũrĩa → to eat

3. Exception: The Verb "To Be"

Irregular Form: kũrĩ (to be)

Functions in two main ways:

  1. Existential sense → there is / there are
    • Kũrĩ mũndũ haha. → There is a person here.
  2. Identity sense → X is Y
    • Nĩ mũratai wakwa. → She is my friend.

Important Note:

In identity statements, the particle nĩ is often used as a copula.

6. Structure of Simple Indicative Sentences

General Pattern:

[Subject] + nĩ + [Predicate/Object]

Illustrative Examples:

  • Nĩĩ ndĩ mũrutwo. → I am a student.
  • We wĩ mwarimũ. → You are a teacher.
  • Ũyũ nĩ mũndũ mwega. → He/She is a good person.
  • Ithũe twĩ Agĩkũyũ. → We are Kikuyus.
  • Inyuĩ mwĩ arutani. → You (pl.) are teachers.
  • Ũcio nĩ mũndũ wa Nairobi. → They are people of Nairobi.

7. Cultural-Linguistic Insight

The verb nĩ (to be) has a central cultural role in Kikuyu:

  • Used in introductions and self-identification.
  • Links an individual to their profession, ethnicity, or social role.

Examples of Usage in Social Identity:

  • Professions:
    • We wĩ mwarimũ. → You are a teacher.
  • Ethnic Identity:
    • Ithũe twĩ Agĩkũyũ. → We are Kikuyus.
  • Social Belonging:
    • Acio nĩ andũ a Nairobi. → They are people of Nairobi.

Cultural Note:

  • Kikuyu communication emphasizes community, belonging, and role.
  • Identity is less about isolated individuals and more about integration into society.
