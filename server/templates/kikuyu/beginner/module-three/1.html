3.1 Adjectives

3.1.1 Adjectives & Adjective Clauses

Core definition (concise): Adjectives in Kikuyu denote qualities, states or attributes of nouns.

Agreement: Adjectives do not carry a single fixed prefix across the board; instead they agree with the noun class of the modified noun. Agreement is realised by class prefixes such as mũ-, kĩ-, i-, ũ-, etc.

Canonical word order / syntax:

Noun + Adjective (with class agreement) — e.g., Gĩti kĩu kĩeru = "The white chair"; Mũndũ mũũru = "A bad person".

Adjective clauses; the material lists adjective behaviour and examples in NP contexts (N + Adj with agreement); no independent clause-level patterns were supplied beyond this NP pattern — treat adjective clauses for annotation and elicitation as NPs where the adjective must show class agreement.

Morphological markers of adjectives

General statement: Adjectives typically occur with a class prefix that corresponds to the noun they modify.

Semantic categories

A. Physical appearance & size

  • Big: mũnene
  • Small: nini
  • Tall: mũraihu
  • New: njerũ (recently acquired/made)

B. Colour

  • White: kĩeru
  • Black: kĩrũ
  • Brown / dark: gĩtĩĩri
  • Red: mũtune

C. Temperature & taste

  • Hot / strong (liquids etc.): hiũ
  • Cold (air, objects): hehu
  • Sweet: sama / attested surface as thaka in contexts
  • Bitter: ndũrũ

D. Weight & quantity

  • Heavy: nditũ
  • Light (not heavy; weak / easy): hũthũ
  • Many: nyingĩ
  • Few: nini

E. Quality & character

  • Good: mũega
  • Bad: mũũru
  • Strong / able: mũhoti
  • New / strange: mũgeni
  • Friendly / good-hearted: mũrata

Formation of adjectives from verbs — rules and exemplars

General rule (as provided): many Kikuyu adjectives derive from verbs via a participial/nominalising pattern analogous to English "-ing" / "-ed" → verb root + participial formation = descriptive adjective (person/state).

Illustrative table (exact items from your material):

Verb Gloss Derived adjective Meaning
kũhoya to pray mũhoi one who prays (praying person)
gũkua to die mũkuo one who is dead (dead)
kũhota to be able mũhoti strong, able
kũruga to cook mũrugi cooked

Analytic note; derived adjectival forms frequently bear the class prefix that matches the noun they modify (compare mũhoi, mũhũũru, mũhotu, mũrugi).

Linguistic & cultural insights

  • Agreement is obligatory: adjectives must agree with the noun class of their head noun; agreement is realised by class prefixes (e.g., mũ-, kĩ-).
  • Cultural loading of descriptive terms: lexical items for moral/ aesthetic value are socially salient — e.g., mũega ("good") frequently indexes moral uprightness as well as aesthetic approval.
  • Social reading in adjectival phrases: adjectives referring to beauty, strength or morality carry pragmatic/social implicatures: mwarĩ mũega can imply good manners as well as physical beauty.
  • Prosody and emphasis: adjectives combine with tone and emphatic particles to alter force — example exemplar: nene muno = "very big".
