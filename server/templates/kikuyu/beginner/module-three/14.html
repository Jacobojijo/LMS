3.7.1 Summary of Verbs

1. Transitive Verbs in Kikuyu

1.1 Nature of Transitive Verbs

Definition: Verbs that require a direct object to complete their semantic meaning.

Directionality of Action:
The action is projected outward from the subject (the doer/agent) to an external entity (the patient/object).

Canonical Word Order:
Kikuyu typically follows the SVO structure:

Subject → Verb → Object
Example: Nĩndũhe gitabu – I give a book.

1.2 Syntactic and Morphological Properties

Feature Description Illustration
Subject Prefixes Bound morphemes prefixed to the verb to encode the grammatical subject (person/number). N-gũ-he → "I give you" (N = I).
Object Infixes Inserted between the subject prefix and verb root to encode the recipient/object of the action. N-gũ-he → "I give you" (gũ = you).
Agglutinative Structure Kikuyu verbs are agglutinative, meaning multiple grammatical functions (subject, object, tense, aspect, etc.) are compacted into a single word form. Ngũhe = N (I) + gũ (you) + he (give).

1.3 Subjective Pronouns (Subject Prefixes)

Function: Mark the agent/doer of the action.
Position: Appear as prefixes attached to the verb root.

Forms:

Person Prefix Example (with he = give) Translation
1st sg. (I) N-/Nd- Ngũhe I give you
2nd sg. (you) Ũ-/U- Ũmũhe You give him/her
3rd sg. (he/she) A-/Ø A-tũ-he He gives us
1st pl. (we) Tũ- Tũmahe We give them
2nd pl. (you pl.) Mũ- Mũmũhe You (pl.) give him/her
3rd pl. (they) Ma-/M- Matũhe They give us

1.4 Objective Pronouns (Object Infixes)

Function: Indicate the recipient of the action.
Position: Appear as infixes between the subject prefix and the verb root.
Formula: Subject Prefix + Object Infix + Verb Stem

Forms:

Person Object Infix Example (with he = give) Translation
1st sg. (me) -ĩ- / -a- Ũndĩhe You give me
2nd sg. (you) -gũ- Ngũhe I give you
3rd sg. (him/her) -mũ- Ũmũhe You give him/her
1st pl. (us) -tũ- Atũhe He gives us
2nd pl. (you pl.) -mũ- Mũmũhe You (pl.) give him/her
3rd pl. (them) -ma- Tũmahe We give them

1.5 Conjugation Case Studies

Verb Form Gloss Meaning

Ũmũhe Ũ-mũ-he You give him/her

2. Intransitive Verbs in Kikuyu

2.1 Nature of Intransitives

Definition: Verbs that do not require a direct object; the action is self-contained within the subject.

Examples:

  • Gũthimaga – to run
  • Gũcooka – to return
  • Gũkoma – to sleep
  • Gũthaka – to dance
  • Gũtuura – to dwell/live
  • Gũikara – to sit

2.2 Illustrative Sentences

Sentence Gloss Meaning
Nĩndakoma gitanda. I-have-slept bed I have slept on the bed
Nĩtwathimaga mbere. We-run forward We run forward

2.3 The Verb "To Have" in Kikuyu

Possession with "na": Expressed through gũkorũo na = "to be with."
Structure: Personal Pronoun + rĩ na

Examples:

  • Ndĩ na gitanda – I have a bed.
  • Arĩ na guoko – He has a hand.

3. Reflexive Verbs in Kikuyu

3.1 Formation

Created by adding reflexive suffixes to the verb stem:

  • -ĩka → self-directed action.
  • -ana → reciprocal/mutual action.
  • -ĩra → reflexive/benefactive action.

3.2 Examples

Root Verb Reflexive Form Meaning
Kũhitha (to hide) Kũhitha-ĩka To hide oneself
Kũona (to see) Kũonana To see each other
Kũthamba (to wash) Kũthambia To wash oneself

3.3 Reflexive Verbs as Nouns

Reflexive forms can be nominalized using the prefix Gũ-.

Examples:

  • Gũthambia nĩwega. – Washing oneself is good.
  • Gũthakara nĩ mbi. – Fighting each other is bad.

4. Pedagogical and Linguistic Implications

Morphological Mastery

  • Competence in subject prefixes and object infixes is fundamental for correct conjugation.
  • Misplacement or omission leads to ambiguity or ungrammaticality.

Agglutinative Compactness

  • Kikuyu's verb morphology allows for dense encoding of subject, object, and action in one word.
  • Demonstrates efficiency of the Bantu verbal system.

Balanced Understanding of Verb Classes

  • Intransitives build basic action vocabulary.
  • Reflexives enrich expression of self-directed and mutual actions.
  • Transitives develop interactional syntax between subject and object.

Possessive Structures

  • The use of gũkorũo na for "to have" emphasizes existential belonging rather than ownership.
  • Reflects Kikuyu worldview: possession = being with.
