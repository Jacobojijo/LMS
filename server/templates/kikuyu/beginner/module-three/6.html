3.3.1 Introduction to Comparatives

Core definition & scope

Comparatives: constructions used to compare qualities (height, size, taste, moral quality, etc.) between two or more entities.

Two principal comparative types in Kikuyu (from the data):

  • Relative comparison — "more / -er" (e.g., taller, sweeter).
  • Comparison of equality — "as … as" (e.g., as tall as, as sweet as).

Form-building devices:

  • the particle kūrĩ = "more than" (marks relative comparatives)
  • the particle ta = "as … as" (marks equality)
  • Occasional reduplication of adjectives for emphasis (used sometimes to intensify).

Canonical comparative morphology

General morphological strategy (explicit): Kikuyu comparatives are analytic: the comparative is formed by combining an adjective (or adjective root) with the particle kūrĩ rather than by internal morphological change.

Formal pattern (schematic):

  • Relative comparative: [Adjective root] + kūrĩ → "more-/-er"
    e.g., mũnene → nene kūrĩ = bigger
    e.g., thaka → thaka kūrĩ = sweeter
  • Equality: use ta in a clause expressing equivalence: … nĩ [Adj] ta … → "is as [Adj] as …"

Syntactic template for relative comparison (from examples):

NP₁ nĩ [Adj] kūrĩ NP₂

Example: Mũndũ ũyũ nĩ mũnene kūrĩ ũcio. → "This person is bigger than that one."

Syntactic template for equality:

NP₁ nĩ [Adj] ta NP₂

Example: Mũndũ ũyũ nĩ mũnene ta ũcio. → "This person is as big as that one."

Lexical comparative pairs

Example sentences

  • Mũndũ ũyũ nĩ mũnene kūrĩ ũcio. — "This person is bigger than that one."
  • Gĩti gĩkĩ nĩ gĩkũrũ kūrĩ kĩrĩa. — "This chair is older than that one."
  • Mũtĩ ũyũ nĩ mũraihu kūrĩ ũcio. — "This tree is taller than that one."
  • Njĩra ĩno nĩ njega kūrĩ ĩrĩa. — "This road is better than that one."
  • Njaũ ĩno nĩ thaka kūrĩ ĩrĩa. — "This calf is more beautiful than that one."

Comparison of equality (the particle ta)

Function: ta corresponds to English "as … as" and appears between the adjective and the compared target in the NP clause.

Standard clause form (practical description): NP₁ nĩ [Adj] ta NP₂

Examples:

  • Mũndũ ũyũ nĩ mũnene ta ũcio. → "This person is as big as that one."
  • Mũtĩ ũyũ nĩ mũraihu ta ũcio. → "This tree is as tall as that one."
  • Njaũ ĩno nĩ thaka ta ĩrĩa. → "This calf is as more beautiful as that one."
  • Mwarĩ ũyũ nĩ mũega ta ũrĩa. → "This girl is as good/beautiful as that one."
  • Njĩra ĩno nĩ njega ta ĩrĩa. → "This road is as good as that one."

Reduplication

Usage (data): sometimes adjectives are reduplicated for emphasis (intensification).

Interpretation: reduplication functions pragmatically to increase intensity (akin to English "very, very").

Caveat: the dataset notes use of reduplication but does not supply token examples; treat reduplication as an attested strategy to test in elicitation.

Morphosyntactic diagnostics & tests (how to identify comparative constructions)

Diagnostic 1 — presence of kūrĩ:

If an adjective is followed by kūrĩ and a second NP, the clause expresses a relative comparative (more/-er).

Example test sentence: X nĩ Adj kūrĩ Y → relative comparison.

Diagnostic 2 — presence of ta:

If ta connects the adjective and the second NP, the clause expresses equality (as … as).

Example test sentence: X nĩ Adj ta Y → equality comparison.

Diagnostic 3 — reduplication (emphasis):

If the adjective shows reduplication, expect an intensifying reading (verify with informants).

Morphological patterns

Analytic strategy (explicit): comparatives are formed externally (particle + adjective) rather than via internal stem alternation.

Pattern notation:

  • Positive → [Adj root]
  • Comparative (relative) → [Adj root] + kūrĩ
  • Equality → [Adj] ta [NP₂]

Implication: morphology remains stable across positive/comparative; syntax supplies the comparative meaning.

Cultural & pragmatic functions

Moral/character comparisons: Kikuyu comparative constructions are routinely used to compare moral or character attributes (e.g., mũega 'good' → wega kūrĩ 'better'), not only physical properties.

Social evaluation: comparative statements often function as social evaluation (ranking persons by virtue, reputation, or capacity).

Semantic breadth: comparative constructions therefore have descriptive and evaluative force — they describe and judge (e.g., Njĩra ĩno nĩ njega kūrĩ ĩrĩa = this road is better than that one, evaluative).

Pedagogic note: comparisons with mũega/mũũru encode culturally salient moral distinctions; treat them as sociolinguistic data points as well as grammatical phenomena.
