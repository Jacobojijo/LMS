3.6.2 Intransitive & Reflexive Verbs

1. Intransitive Verbs in Kikuyu

Definition

Intransitive verbs are verbs that:

  • Do not take a direct object.
  • Describe complete actions that focus solely on the subject.
  • Express states or actions that are self-contained.

Key Features

  • The action stops at the subject; it does not extend to an external object.
  • The subject is both the initiator and the completer of the action.
  • Often describe movement, posture, states of being, or habitual actions.

Common Intransitive Verbs in Kikuyu

Kikuyu Verb English Meaning
Gũthimaga to run
Gũcooka to return
Gũkoma to sleep
Gũthaka to dance
Gũtuura to live / dwell
Gũikara to sit

Illustrative Sentences

  • Nĩndakoma gitanda → "I have slept on the bed."
  • Nĩtwathimaga mbere → "We run forward."
  • Nĩacookire nyũmba → "He/She returned home."

Pedagogical Note

Mnemonic Device: Think of intransitive verbs as "solo performers" on stage: the subject acts, but there is no "receiver" of the action.

2. The Verb "To Have" in Kikuyu

Expression of Possession

  • Kikuyu does not have a simple verb meaning "to have."
  • Instead, possession is expressed using:
    • gũkorũo na → "to be with"
    • Formula: Personal Pronoun + rĩ na = "To have"

Conjugation Table: Possessive with "na"

Pronoun Kikuyu Form English Meaning
I Ndĩ na I have
You (sg.) Ũrĩ na You have
He/She Arĩ na He/She has
We Tũrĩ na We have
You (pl.) Mũrĩ na You (pl.) have
They Marĩ na They have

Examples

  • Ndĩ na gitanda → "I have a bed."
  • Arĩ na guoko → "He has a hand."
  • Mũrĩ na nyũmba → "You (pl.) have a house."
  • Tũrĩ na gĩkeno → "We have joy."

Pedagogical Note

Metaphor: In Kikuyu, possession is not ownership but companionship. You "are with" an item, showing relational possession rather than absolute ownership.

3. Reflexive Verbs in Kikuyu

A. Formation of Reflexive Verbs

  • Reflexivity is expressed by adding suffixes to the verb stem.
  • These suffixes signal that the subject:
    • Acts upon themselves (individual reflexive).
    • Acts upon each other (reciprocal reflexive).

Common Reflexive Suffixes

  • -ĩka → "oneself"
  • -ana → "each other"
  • -ĩra → reciprocal or extended reflexive nuance

B. Infinitive vs Reflexive Comparison

Verb (Infinitive) Meaning Reflexive Form Reflexive Meaning
Kũhitha to hide Kũĩhitha to hide oneself
Kũona to see Kũonana to see each other
Kũruta to teach Kũrutana to teach each other / to learn
gũthambia to wash gũthambia to wash oneself

Observation

Reflexives broaden the semantic scope of verbs: they can shift from one-directional action to self-directed or mutual interaction.

C. Reflexive Verb Conjugation (Kũthambia – to wash oneself)

Subject Kikuyu Example English Meaning
I Nĩndethambia I am washing myself
You (sg.) Nĩurethambia You are washing yourself
He/She Nĩethambia He/She is washing himself/herself
We Nĩtũrethambia We are washing ourselves
You (pl.) Nĩmũrethambia You (pl.) are washing yourselves
They Nĩmarethambia They are washing themselves

Pedagogical Note

Mnemonic Device: Reflexive verbs are like a mirror: the action "bounces back" to the subject.

D. Reflexive Verbs Used as Nouns

  • Kikuyu reflexive verbs can be nominalized by adding the prefix gũ- before the verb stem.
  • This creates abstract nouns denoting the act/state of doing something reflexively.

Examples

  • Gũthambia nĩwega → "Washing is good."
  • Gũthakara nĩ mbi → "Fighting each other is bad."
  • Gũthoma nĩ ũũgĩ → "Learning is wisdom."

Observation

This demonstrates the flexibility of Kikuyu verbs to shift between action and concept/state seamlessly.
