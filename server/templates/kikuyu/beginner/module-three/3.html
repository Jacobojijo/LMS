3.1.3 Adjective of Color

Color adjectives in Kikuyu are both descriptive and cultural. They function syntactically as post-nominal modifiers (they follow the noun), and semantically they express literal, symbolic, and cultural meanings. The Kikuyu lexicon of colors reflects a blend of indigenous imagery (soil, yolk, purity) and modern borrowings (blue, purple, green).

1. Structural and Morphological Features

Kikuyu color adjectives:

  • Follow the noun (Noun + Color Adjective).
  • Are not marked by a fixed prefix, unlike quality and quantity adjectives.
  • The color root changes its prefix depending on the noun class it modifies.

Sources of Kikuyu color terms:

  • Native Kikuyu roots (e.g., -tune, -iru, -eru, gĩtĩiri).
  • Cultural metaphors (e.g., gakarakara = yolk = yellow).
  • Borrowings from Swahili/English (buluu, ngirini, ndigo, thamba, brun).

2. Kikuyu Color Adjectives

2.1 Ndune / Mũtune (Red)

Root: -tune.

Prefix: agrees with the noun class.

Semantic Domain:

  • Literal: red objects, skin, fabric.
  • Symbolic: vitality, blood, energy.

Examples:

Gĩtĩ gĩtune → "A red chair."

Mũndũ mũtune → "A red-skinned person."

2.2 -iru → Njiru (Black)

Root: -iru.

Morphology: Prefix adapts to noun class (e.g., njirũ, mũirũ).

Semantic Note: Darkness, misfortune, or mystery.

Examples:

Ngũo njirũ → "Black clothes."

Mũndũ mũirũ → "Black person."

2.3 -ĩeru → Kĩeru (White)

Root: -eru.

Semantic Associations: Purity, peace, cleanness, innocence.

Examples:

Mbũri njeru → "White goat."

Mũndũ mweru → "A white person."

2.4 Buluu (Blue)

Origin: Borrowed from English/Swahili.

Usage: Fabrics, modern objects, sky.

Examples:

Ngũo buluu → "Blue clothes."

Gĩti buluu → "Blue chair."

2.5 Gakarakara (Yellow)

Origin: Metaphor—"egg yolk color."

Cultural Insight: Shared with Dholuo (karakara). Symbol of freshness and fertility.

Examples:

Gĩti gakarakara → "Yellow chair."

Ngũo gakarakara → "Yellow garment."

2.6 Ngirini (Green)

Origin: Borrowed from Swahili (kijani → ngirini).

Usage: Vegetation, freshness, natural scenery.

Examples:

Mũgũnda ngirini → "Green farm/vegetation."

Ngũo ngirini → "Green clothes."

2.7 Ndigo (Indigo / Dark Blue-Violet)

Origin: Swahili loanword (ndigo).

Usage: Deep-dye colors, especially in clothing and traditional fabrics.

Examples:

Ngũo ndigo → "Indigo clothing."

Gĩti ndigo → "Indigo-painted chair."

2.8 Thamba (Purple)

Origin: Borrowed modern term (thamba).

Cultural Significance: Associated with elegance, royalty, ceremonial clothing.

Examples:

Ngũo thamba → "Purple dress."

Bũkũ thamba → "Purple books."

2.9 Kĩeru na Kĩtũ (Black and White)

Root Phrase: literally "white and black."

Usage: For patterned coloration (checkered, spotted, mixed tones).

Examples:

Mbũri njerũ na njirũ → "Black and white goat."

Ngũo njerũ na njirũ → "Checkered clothes."

2.10 Gĩtĩiri (Brown)

Root: Indigenous Kikuyu term.

Semantic Range: Earth color, soil, natural tone.

Modern Variant: Brun (borrowed).

Examples:

Gĩtĩ gĩtĩiri → "Brown chair."

Mwarĩ gĩtĩiri → "Brown-skinned girl."

3. General Observations

  • Syntactic Behavior: Color adjectives follow nouns (N + Color).
  • Lexical Origins:
    • Native: mũtune, kĩeru, njirũ, gĩtĩiri.
    • Borrowed: buluu, ngirini, ndigo, thamba, brun.
  • Cultural References: Natural imagery (egg yolk = yellow, soil = brown) enriches Kikuyu color vocabulary.
  • Symbolism: Colors in Kikuyu carry cultural meaning:
    • White = purity.
    • Black = misfortune or depth.
    • Red = vitality.
    • Purple = prestige.
