3.9.1 Summary of Tenses

1. Tense overview (compact system schema)

  • Kikuyu verbs are organised temporally into three primary tenses: Past, Present, Future.
  • Each primary tense admits four aspectual/mode subtypes: Simple, Continuous (Progressive), Perfect, Habitual.
  • Practical consequence: a speaker combines tense + aspect + subject concord + (where needed) auxiliaries / suffixes to encode time, aspect and participant all in the verb complex.

2. Present — Rĩa Rũciaro (core properties & forms)

Functions

  • Current / ongoing actions (e.g., Wanjikũ nĩa rarĩa — Wanjiku is eating).
  • Habitual / regular actions (e.g., Kamau arĩagĩra nyũmba — Kamau usually eats at home).

Infinitive behaviour

Most verbs keep the infinitive prefix kũ- in non-finite contexts; in finite present forms the root is combined with subject markers.

Subject prefixes & sample conjugation (retain user examples)

Person Pronoun Prefix Example (present)
1 sg Ndĩ Nd-/N- Nĩndĩrarĩa
2 sg Ũ Ũ-/U- Nĩurarĩa
3 sg Nĩ A-/Ø Niararĩa

2 pl Mũ Mũ- Nĩmũrarĩa
3 pl Nĩ M-/Ma- Nĩmararĩa

Overt subject rule

Proper nouns / lexical subjects may surface; the pronoun prefix is often retained on the verb but the overt noun overrides the pronominal reference:

  • Wanjikũ nĩararĩa. → Wanjiku is eating.
  • Kamau nĩathii nyũmba. → Kamau has gone home.

3. Present continuous vs simple present

Formal observation

Kikuyu often uses the same verb frame for simple and continuous present; context and aspect markers decide interpretation.

Contextual cues

Time words and discourse: rũciaro, rũgendo, ũndũ wa thutha determine ongoing vs habitual reading.

Habitual marker

Suffix -aga marks habitual/repetitive actions: Kamau arĩagĩra nyũmba (Kamau usually eats at home).

Quick comparison

Feature Simple Present Present Continuous
Primary reading Habitual / general Ongoing / in progress
Marker (often none) or -aga for habitual contextual progressive markers (see § present continuous)
Cues habitual adverbials immediate time adverbials (rũciaro, rũgendo)

4. Morphological & syntactic remarks

  • Agglutinative: subject + tense/aspect frequently fuse into a single verb form (e.g., Ndirĩa = n- + -rĩa).
  • Infinitives (kũ-) appear in future and subordinate constructions.
  • Subjunctive formation: verb stem without final -o (noted in later pedagogical notes).

5. Pedagogical insights (present)

Focus points for learners

  • Master subject prefixes and their fusion with tense markers.
  • Recognize -aga as the habitual flag.
  • Use discourse/time cues to decide progressive vs habitual interpretation.

Challenge

No single distinct progressive morpheme visible in every present form — learners must attend to context and to the explicitly marked progressive forms taught elsewhere.

6. Simple Past — Rĩa Rĩaathire (core facts)

  • Function: completed past actions (bounded, finished events).
  • Canonical structure (surface): Subject prefix + Past marker (nĩ) + Verb (examples follow).

Examples (user data):

  • Wanjikũ nĩathii kũndũ koũ. — Wanjiku went there.
  • Nĩndĩire irio. — I ate food.
  • Maitũ nĩacokire nyũmba. — Mother returned home.

7. Past — contraction, varieties, and temporal nuance

Contracted past

nĩ often contracts with subject prefix: nĩ ndirathii → ndirathii; nĩ arathii → arathii.

Immediate past

Recent/completed action akin to English "I have just …": Nĩndagũra → I have just bought.

Yesterday past

Specific marker iyo / ũnyo indicates 'yesterday': Ndĩrookire ira → I came yesterday.

8. Recent perfective & past continuous/habitual

Recent past perfective

Morphology: Subject Prefix + Verb + -ire / -ete → e.g., Ndirĩire (I have eaten), Ametheire (He has cooked).

Past continuous / habitual

Formed with nĩ + subject + verb + -aga → e.g., Ndirĩaga (I was eating), Twathiiaga nyũmba (We used to go home).

Morphological insight

nĩ = auxiliary past marker; subject agreement applies across past forms.

9. Past perfect (had done / anterior past)

  • Function: action completed before another past event.
  • Structure: subject + verb + -ire / -ete / -ĩte / -etĩte depending on class and nuance (see previous Past Perfect section).
  • Examples: Ndirĩire mbere wa kũmena — I had eaten before breaking; Matũragire mbere ya gũcokio — They had settled before returning.

10. Future — auxiliary gũka / gũ

Core idea

  • Future is expressed by an auxiliary derived from 'to come/to go' (gũka, gũ) preceding the main infinitive.
  • The main verb remains infinitive (kũ-); the auxiliary bears subject agreement.

Immediate future construction

Subject prefix + gũka + Infinitive → Ndĩgũka rĩa (I will eat); Ũgũka thii (You will go).

Contracted future

In spoken forms gũka often contracts: Ndĩka rĩa (I'll eat), Mũka thii (You'll go).

Conjugation sample

Person Form (gũka) Example Gloss
I ndĩgũka Ndĩgũka rĩa I will eat
You sg ũgũka Ũgũka thii You will go
He/She agũka Agũka rĩa He/She will eat
We tũgũka Tũgũka thii We will go
You pl mũgũka Mũgũka rĩa You (pl.) will eat
They magũka Magũka thii They will go

Contracted paradigms: Ndĩgũkũrĩa → Ndĩkũrĩa; Nĩmũgũkũrĩa → Nĩmũgũrĩa etc.

11. Future continuous & "future of to be"

Future continuous

Construction: gũka + ga + verb → e.g., Ndĩgũka ga thii (I will be going), Magũka ga rĩa (They will be eating).

Future of 'to be / to have'

Use gũ + kũgũcokia (be with / have): Nĩngũgũcokia ana arĩa igĩrĩ → I will have two children (lit. "I will be with two children").

12. Obligation, modals & other moods

  • Obligation (should / must) — nomũhaka + verb: Nomũhaka ũthiĩ (You should go).
  • Possibility / permission (may) — kũhoteka + infinitive: Kwahoteka ndigothii (I may not go).
  • Conditional / would — verb + -aga in conditional context: Nĩngũthiiaga kũu (I would go there).
  • Polite imperative / subjunctive (let me/us) — reke / reeke + subjunctive verb form: Reke tũthiĩ! (Let us go!).
  • Past obligation (should have) — nĩ + kũgũĩrĩra + infinitive: Nĩangũĩrĩire gũthiĩ (He should have gone).
  • Had done / Past perfect — verb + -ĩte / -ete endings (anterior): Nĩndarĩire mbere ya gũkoma (I had eaten before sleeping).
  • Causative — verb + -ithia / -ithĩrĩria: Nĩndarugithirie Wanjikũ irio (I made Wanjikũ cook food).

13. Morphosyntactic summary

  • Word order: SVO (Subject–Verb–Object).
  • Auxiliaries (gũka, nomũhaka, kũhoteka, kũgũĩrĩra) carry tense/modality and subject-agreement; main verb in future/modal contexts usually stays infinitive.
  • Agglutination: stacked affixes encode person + tense/aspect + modality in one verbal word.
  • Subjunctive cue: subjunctive stems often formed by removing the verb's final -o (per user note).
  • Contractions frequent in speech: e.g., ndarĩa, ndĩka rĩa (contr. future).
