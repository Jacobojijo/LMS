3.8.7 Other Tenses

1. The "Should" construction (nomũhaka)

Function

Expresses moral / expected obligation or necessity in the present or near future.

Structure

nomũhaka (impersonal auxiliary) + conjugated verb (subject marked on the verb).

Canonical example set

Nomũhaka ũthiĩ. → "You should go."

Nomũhaka mũke. → "You (pl.) should come."

Nomũhaka tũrute wĩra. → "We should do the work."

Morphological notes

nomũhaka functions as a fixed auxiliary phrase; the main verb carries subject concord and verbal morphology as required.

Pedagogical focus

Drill: substitute different subject pronouns on the verb after nomũhaka.

Elicit real-life obligations (use provided sentence frames).

2. The "May" construction (possibility / permission) — kũhoteka

Function

Expresses possibility, permission, or potentiality.

Structure

kũhoteka used impersonally + infinitive (infinitive follows as potential action).

Examples

Kwahoteka ndigothiĩ. → "I may not go."

Kwahoteka no mamenye. → "They may know."

Nĩgũkũhoteka mũhũre. → "You (pl.) may hit."

Morphological notes

kũhoteka behaves as a modal predicate introducing possibility; subject realization may be expressed on the following infinitive or implicitly understood.

Pedagogical focus

Controlled practice converting declarative sentences into permissive/possible forms using kũhoteka.

3. The "Would" construction (conditional / past-habitual)

Function

Expresses hypothetical, conditional, or past habitual actions — "would" in English.

Structure

Verb marked with past-habitual suffix -aga plus an implied or explicit conditional context/clause (often left to context or a conditional clause).

Examples

Nĩngũthiiaga kũũ (if you had come). → "I would go there."

Nĩtũkũrĩaga mũgati (if we had money). → "We would buy bread."

Morphological notes

-aga serves as past/habitual marker in conditional contexts to yield the English sense of "would."

Pedagogical focus

Pair practice: give conditional cue (if…); students produce the -aga form and complete clause.

4. "Let me / Let us" — subjunctive / polite imperative

Function

Polite imperative: requests or permissions that include the speaker or speaker+group.

Structure

Subjunctive verb form, indicated by appropriate subject prefix + specific verb stem alteration (subjunctive morphology).

Examples

Reeke thiĩ! → "Let me go!"

Reke tũthiĩ! → "Let us go!"

Nĩmaruge! → "Let them cook!"

Pedagogical focus

Role-play polite requests; contrast direct imperative vs. subjunctive "let me/us."

5. "Should have" (past obligation) — kũgũĩrĩra

Function

Expresses obligation in the past — something required but not done.

Structure

Past marker nĩ + kũgũĩrĩra (to be fitting) + infinitive of main verb.

Examples

Nĩangũĩrĩire gũthiĩ. → "He should have gone."

Nĩmũgũĩrĩire gũtũcokerera. → "You (pl.) should have returned to us."

Morphological notes

The combination of past marker and kũgũĩrĩra signals retrospective obligation.

Pedagogical focus

Error identification: present vs. past obligation; transform present obligation sentences into past obligation using nĩ + kũgũĩrĩra.

6. "Had done" (Past Perfect / anterior past)

Function

Expresses an event completed before another past event (anteriority).

Structure

Past perfect morphology: -ĩte or -ete ending on the verb (the form carries the "had done" meaning).

Examples (user data)

Nĩndarĩire mbere ya mũndũ ũcio gũkoma. → "I had eaten before that person slept."

Nĩmarugire kũũ mbere ya nduka gũkinya. → "They had cooked here before the shop arrived."

Pedagogical focus

Sequence exercises: reorder two past events so students choose the past perfect form for the earlier action.

7. Causative construction ("to make someone do something")

Function

Expresses causation (coercion, instruction, influence); the subject causes the object to carry out an action.

Structure

Verb stem + causative suffix (commonly -ithia or -ithĩrĩria).

Examples

Nĩndarugithirie Wanjikũ irio. → "I made Wanjikũ cook food."

Nĩarũgamithirie ciana. → "He made the children stand."

Nĩndĩrĩũkithĩrĩria andũ gũthoma. → "I am making the people read."

Syntactic effect

Adds a causer role (subject) and a caused agent (object) who performs the base action.

Pedagogical focus

Morphological drills: form causatives from base verbs using -ithia/-ithĩrĩria and test agent/patient roles.

8. Morphological features

Subjunctive mood

Used for polite commands/requests; often marked by specific verb stem forms and auxiliaries (reke, reeke).

Modal auxiliaries

nomũhaka, kũhoteka, kũgũĩrĩra are primary markers for obligation, possibility, and past obligation respectively.

Past marking

Prefix nĩ- combined with endings (-ire, -ete, -ĩte) or suffix -aga mark different past aspectual meanings.

Causative morphology

-ithia, -ithĩrĩria convert intransitive/neutral verbs into causatives.

9. Syntactic properties

Basic word order: Subject – Verb – Object (SVO).

Causative clauses: retain SVO; object is the person/thing induced to act.

Agglutination: affixation (prefixes, infixes, suffixes) stacks meaning onto a single verbal word — tense, mood, modality, causation.
