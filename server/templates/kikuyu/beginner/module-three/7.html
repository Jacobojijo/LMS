3.3.2 Comparison of two things & Superlatives

1. Comparing Two Things (Relative Comparison)

a. Standard Comparative Structure

Marker Used: kūrĩ

Literally means "than / more than."

Grammatical Role:

Introduces the second entity in the comparison. Functions equivalently to English "than."

Position:

Occurs after the adjective.

Sentence pattern:

[Subject A] + [Adjective] + kūrĩ + [Subject B]

b. Illustrative Examples

Kikuyu Sentence Literal Translation Idiomatic English Equivalent
Mũndũ ũyũ nĩ mũnene kūrĩ ũcio. This person is big than that one. This person is bigger than that one.
Mũtĩ ũyũ nĩ mũraihu kūrĩ ũcio. This tree is tall than that one. This tree is taller than that one.
Njĩra ĩno nĩ njega kūrĩ ĩrĩa. This road is good than that one. This road is better than that one.
Irigũ rĩrĩ nĩ rĩthaka kūrĩ rĩrĩa. This banana is sweet than that one. This banana is sweeter than that one.
Nyũmba ĩno nĩ rĩki kūrĩ ĩrĩa. This house is new than that one. This house is newer than that one.
Mũgũnda wakwa nĩ mũnene kūrĩ waku. My farm is big than yours. My farm is bigger than yours.

c. Special Note on "mũraihu" (tall/long)

Kikuyu adjectives do not reduce internally to form comparative forms. The particle kūrĩ is consistently used for all comparisons.

Example:

Njĩra ĩno nĩ ndaihu kūrĩ ĩrĩa.

This way is longer than that one.

2. Comparisons Using "ta" (Equality)

a. Equality Structure

Marker Used: ta

Expresses "as…as" equivalence.

Pattern:

[Subject A] + [Adjective] + ta + [Subject B]

b. Illustrative Examples

Kikuyu Sentence Literal Translation Idiomatic English Equivalent
Mũndũ ũyũ nĩ mũnene ta ũcio. This person is big as that one. This person is as big as that one.
Mũtĩ ũyũ nĩ mũraihu ta ũcio. This tree is tall as that one. This tree is as tall as that one.
Irigũ rĩrĩ nĩ rĩthaka ta rĩrĩa. This banana is sweet as that one. This banana is as sweet as that one.
Mwarĩ ũyũ nĩ mũega ta ũrĩa. This girl is good as that one. This girl is as beautiful/good as that one.
Njĩra ĩno nĩ njega ta ĩrĩa. This road is good as that one. This road is as good as that one.

3. Superlative Forms in Kikuyu

a. Structural Principle

The superlative is formed by attaching:

[Adjective] + kūrĩ othe

= "the most [adjective] / more than all."

b. Sentence Pattern

[Subject] + [Adjective] + kūrĩ othe

X is the most [adjective].

c. Examples of Superlatives

Adjective (Base Form) Superlative Form English Equivalent
mũega (good) njega kūrĩ othe the best
mũraihu (tall/long) raihu kūrĩ othe the tallest / longest
mũũru (bad) ũũru kūrĩ othe the worst
mũnene (big) nene kūrĩ othe the biggest

Illustrative Sentence:

Mwarĩ ũyũ nĩ mwega kūrĩ othe.

This girl is the most beautiful.

4. Intensifiers: Expressing Extreme Qualities

a. High-Degree Intensifiers

Kikuyu employs intensifying adverbs such as mũno (very). Reduplication or emphatic repetition also heightens intensity. Function: Indicates extreme or emphatic degree of a quality.

b. Examples of Intensified Adjectives

Kikuyu Form Literal Gloss English Equivalent
mweru mũno very white extremely white
mũtune mũno very red extremely red
mũiru mũno very black pitch black
Hiũ mũno very hot extremely hot
Hehu mũno very cold extremely cold
njerũ mũno very new brand new

c. Example Sentences

Kikuyu Sentence English Translation
Rũcinĩ rũrũ rũrĩ na ũritũ mũnene. This morning is very important.
Riũa nĩ rĩhiũ mũno! The sun is very hot!

5. Summary of Key Grammatical Markers

Function Marker / Phrase Translation / Role
Equality Comparison ta as…as
Comparative Degree kūrĩ than, more than
Superlative Degree kūrĩ othe the most / more than all
Extreme Quality (intensity) mũno / repetition very [quality]

6. Cultural-Linguistic Insight

Comparisons in Kikuyu are not restricted to physical description (size, height, sweetness). They extend deeply into moral, social, and evaluative domains.

Key Areas of Comparison in Kikuyu Culture:

Moral Values:

mũega (good) vs mũũru (bad). Expresses approval/disapproval of behavior.

Character and Personality:

mũrũgũ (rude/foolish) vs mũthĩnjĩ (wise). Socially evaluative; crucial in defining interpersonal respect.

Social Status and Evaluation:

Superlatives used to judge leadership, wealth, or social prestige. Example: "mũnene kūrĩ othe" may describe the most influential elder.

Interpretive Note:

Comparison functions as both linguistic and cultural evaluation. It provides a means of ranking individuals, behaviors, and values. In Kikuyu society, language thus becomes a mirror of morality and social order.
