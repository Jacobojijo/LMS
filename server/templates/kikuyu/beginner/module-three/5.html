3.2.1 Summary of Adjectives

1. Morphosyntactic structure

Function: adjectives modify nouns to express qualities, states, or attributes.

Agreement: adjectives must agree with the noun class of the head noun; agreement is realised by class prefixes (e.g., mũ-, kĩ-, i-, ũ-, etc.).

Canonical syntax: Noun + Adjective (with noun-class prefix).

Example tokens from your data:

  • Gĩti kĩeru → "the white chair."
  • Mũndũ mũũru → "a bad person."

2. Morphological markers — root ↔ surface examples (compact table)

Generalization: most adjective roots appear in surface form with a class prefix matching the noun. The root may be glossed; surface token shows the prefix.

Adjective root Surface example Gloss
-nene Mũndũ mũnene big person
-eru Gĩti kĩeru white chair
-ũru Mũndũ mũũru bad person
-ega Njĩra njega good road
-raihu Mũtĩ mũraihu tall tree
-ingi Ciana nyingi many children
-nini Nyũmba nini small house

3. Semantic classification (category → items)

A. Physical appearance & size

  • mũnene — big
  • nini — small / few
  • mũraihu — tall
  • njerũ — new / recently acquired

B. Colour

  • kĩeru — white
  • kĩrũ — black
  • gĩtĩiri — brown / soil colour
  • mũtune — red

C. Temperature & taste

  • mũhiũ — hot / strong (liquids etc.)
  • mũhehu— cold (air, objects)
  • mũthaka / thaka — sweet
  • mũrũrũ / ndũrũ — bitter

D. Weight & quantity

  • nditũ — heavy
  • hũthũ — light (not heavy / weak / easy)
  • nyingĩ— many
  • nini — few

E. Quality & character

  • mũega — good, morally upright
  • mũũru — bad, immoral
  • mũhoti — strong, able
  • mũgeni — new / strange
  • mũrata — friendly / kind

4. Derivation: adjectives from verbs (rule + examples)

Rule (summary): verb root + participial/nominalising morphology → adjectival/participial form (used attributively).

Examples provided:

Verb Gloss Derived adjective Meaning
kũhoya to pray mũhoi one who prays / praying person
gũkua to die mũkuũ dead
kũhota to be able mũhotu strong, able
kũruga to cook mũrugi cooked

5. Examples in natural syntax

  • Njĩra nĩnene — big road
  • Gĩti kĩeru — white chair

6. Adjectives of quality

6.1. Mũega / Mũega muno

Gloss: good, beautiful, morally upright.

Pragmatic range: aesthetic + moral evaluation.

Examples: Mwarĩ mũega; Mũndũ mũega muno ("a very good person").

6.2. Mũũru / Mũũru muno

Gloss: bad, ugly, immoral.

Range: ethical judgement, negative description.

Example: Mũndũ mũũru; Wendo mũũru muno ("a very bad relationship").

6.3. Mũrũrũ

Gloss: bitter, unpleasant.

Range: taste and figurative unpleasantness.

Examples: Mũtũnda mũrũrũ;

6.4. Mũkuũ

Gloss: dead, lifeless.

Range: literal death; also state/condition.

Examples: Mũtĩ mũkuũ; Mũndũ mũkuũ.

7. Adjectives of quantity

7.1. Mũnene — big / important (literal + metaphorical). Example: Mũndũ mũnene.

7.2. Nyingi — many. Example: Ciana nyingi.

7.3. Nini — few / small. Example: Ciana nini.

7.4. Nditũ / -ritũ — heavy / weighty. Example: Wĩra mũritũ (heavy work).

7.5. Mũhuthu — easy / simple. Example: Kiama kihuthu (an easy task).

7.6. Nyingi muno — abundant / very many. Example: Mbembe nyingi muno (plenty of maize).

8. Adjectives of colour

Syntactic position: color adjectives follow the noun (N + color).

Morphology: color terms are often independent lexical items and may appear without overt class inflection in many tokens.

Representative items and example tokens:

Color Term(s) Example(s)

black njirũ / mũirũ Ngũo njirũ — "black cloth"
white njeru / mweru Mbũri njeru — "white goat"
blue buluu (borrowed) Ngũo buluu — "blue clothes"
yellow gakarakara (egg-yolk) Gĩti gakarakara — "yellow chair"
green ngirini (borrowed) Mũgũnda ngirini — "green farm"

9. Pluralization of adjectives

9.1 General rule

When a noun is pluralised, its adjective must change to agree: adjective prefix alternates according to the noun's plural class (singular mũ-, plural a-, i-, ma-, mi-, etc. as shown in examples).

9.2 Morphological examples (singular → plural)

  • Mũndũ mũnene → Andũ anene (big person → big people)
  • Mwarĩ mũũru → Awarĩ aũru (bad girl → bad girls)
  • Mũtĩ mũkuhĩ → Mĩti mĩkuhĩ (short tree → short trees)
  • Mũndũ mũthĩĩni → Andũ athĩĩni (poor person → poor people)
  • Gĩti gĩkũrũ → Mĩti mĩkũrũ (old chair → old chairs)

9.3 Adjectives that do not change (invariant)

Main classes that often remain invariable: colours and taste/feeling adjectives.

Examples:

  • Thani ya ngirini → Mathani ma ngirini (a green plate → green plates).
  • Njaũ thaka → Njaũ thaka (a sweet banana → sweet bananas).

9.4 Semantic categories re: inflection

Inflecting adjectives: mũnene → anene; mũũru → aũru;

Non-inflecting adjectives: colours (e.g., ngirini), tastes/feelings (e.g., thaka, ndũrũ, mũgathĩ).

10. Linguistic & cultural insights

Agreement is central: adjective-noun concord is an obligatory morphosyntactic property of Kikuyu; both elements change in parallel when number changes.

Semantic loading: descriptive adjectives often carry cultural and moral weight — e.g., mũega goes beyond "good" to imply moral uprightness and social approbation.

Lexical stability vs. morphological variability: some adjective classes (notably colour and taste terms) are lexicalised and resist plural inflection; others participate fully in the noun-class system.

Derivational productivity: verbs supply a productive source of adjectival roots via participial/nominalising operations (see mũhotu, mũhũũru, mũrugi).

Syntactic economy: the canonical N + Adj order and prefixal agreement produce compact noun phrases well suited for rapid descriptive speech and for marking pragmatic evaluation (e.g., mũndũ mũega muno = very good person).

Educational note: teaching adjective agreement is best done with paradigms that show the noun across singular/plural and the adjective in matching forms.
