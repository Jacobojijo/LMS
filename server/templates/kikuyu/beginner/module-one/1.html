1.1 Introduction to Kikuyu and Agikuyu

1.1.1 Introduction

The Agikuyu, Kenya's largest ethnic group, are a Bantu-speaking community traditionally occupying the central highlands of Kenya. Their culture is rich with oral traditions, social systems, and a deep connection to land, spirituality, and family. The Kikuyu language (Gikuyu) belongs to the Bantu branch of the Niger-Congo language family.

Origins and Historical Background

The Agikuyu trace their origins to Mũkũrwe wa Gathanga, a sacred grove in present-day Murang'a County. According to oral traditions, the community descended from Gikũyũ and Mũmbi, a mythical couple blessed by Ngai (God) and given nine daughters, the ancestors of the nine main Kikuyu clans (mathematically ten, due to the later inclusion of an unofficial tenth clan).

Settlement and Migration

Though traditionally considered autochthonous to Central Kenya, historical and linguistic evidence suggests the Kikuyu migrated southwards from regions near Mount Elgon alongside other Eastern Bantu groups such as the Embu, Meru, and Kamba. Their expansion was influenced by the search for fertile land and water sources for agriculture, which is central to Kikuyu life.

Key Settlement Regions

By the 18th century, the Agikuyu had firmly established themselves in present-day:

  • Murang'a
  • Nyeri
  • Kiambu
  • Kirinyaga
  • Nyandarua
  • Parts of Laikipia

Language and Linguistic Classification

Gikuyu (Kikuyu) is a Bantu language belonging to the Niger-Congo language family, specifically under the Benue-Congo > Bantoid > Bantu > Northeast Bantu > Thagiicu group. It is mutually intelligible with languages such as Embu, Meru, and Kamba.

Distinct Features of Kikuyu Language

  • Tonal (meaning changes with tone)
  • Vowel harmony
  • Noun class system (similar to grammatical gender)
  • Verb morphology that encodes subject, tense, aspect, mood

Cultural Practices and Identity

The Kikuyu culture is built around:

  • Family (mbari) and clan (muhiriga) structures
  • Strong agrarian traditions—main crops: maize, beans, bananas, potatoes, and coffee
  • Rites of passage: Birth, initiation (circumcision), marriage, and death
  • Deep spiritual connection to Mount Kenya (Kirinyaga), believed to be the dwelling place of Ngai, the supreme deity
  • A long tradition of resistance and leadership, especially evident in the Mau Mau rebellion against colonialism

Distribution Today

The Agikuyu are primarily found in:

  • Central Kenya
  • Nairobi and other urban centers across the country

They form approximately 17-22% of Kenya's population.

Linguistic Relatives of Gikuyu

  • Kamba (Kikamba)
  • Embu
  • Mbeere
  • Meru
  • Chuka
  • Igembe
  • Tharaka

These communities share linguistic roots and some cultural practices due to common Bantu ancestry and migration routes.

1.1.2 History & Culture of the Kikuyu of Kenya

Etymology and Historical Background

Etymology of "Kikuyu"

The term Kikuyu originates from the word Gĩkũyũ, meaning "a large sycamore tree." According to oral tradition, the first man, Gĩkũyũ, was shown the land to settle by Ngai (God) at the sacred mountain, Kĩrĩnyaga (Mount Kenya), under a large mugumo tree. The name thus reflects both a spiritual and ecological relationship with the environment.

Origin of "Kabete" and Early References

The name Kabete, commonly used in early colonial descriptions of the Kikuyu territory, came from the Kikuyu word kubetia, meaning "to cross or pass." European explorers used this to describe the region where early encounters occurred. Similarly, some locations like Nyeri and Murang'a have Bantu linguistic roots, showing the continuity of indigenous naming systems.

Migration into Central Kenya

The Kikuyu's migration into their current homeland in Central Kenya is traced through oral history rather than written records, occurring gradually over centuries. These movements were guided by ecological suitability, spiritual signs, and clan agreements.

First Group: Agacera a Kĩmathi

These early settlers came through the Tana River basin and settled near the slopes of Mount Kenya. They include clans such as Anjiru, Ambui, and Agacera.

Second Group: Ahiti a Mwangi

Migrating from the eastern side of Mount Kenya, they expanded into parts of Kirinyaga and Embu, integrating with groups like the Aembu and Ambeere.

Third Group: Atumia a Gathigia

This wave moved westward, settling in present-day Nyeri and Murang'a, displacing smaller Bantu-speaking groups and absorbing others through intermarriage.

Fourth Group: Aigana a Gikuyu na Mumbi

These were the legendary descendants of Gikuyu and Mumbi who organized into the nine clans (mbari) and spread across Central Kenya.

Fifth Group: Aigana a Thagana

A later migration that settled near river valleys like Thagana (Tana), integrating with bordering communities and extending the Kikuyu frontier.

Interactions with Other Ethnic Groups

When the Kikuyu expanded into Central Kenya, they encountered other Bantu communities such as the Aembu, Akamba, and Abagusii. Some of these groups were pushed to new areas, while others were absorbed culturally and linguistically through marriage and trade. In regions bordering the Maasai, like Naivasha and Laikipia, cultural exchanges also occurred, especially in naming, livestock practices, and spiritual beliefs.

Socio-Cultural Organization and Clans

The Kikuyu are traditionally organized into nine clans (ĩtũra) descending from the daughters of Mumbi. Each clan consists of various lineages (mbari) and family units. The prefix "A-" denotes "people of" in Kikuyu.

Major Kikuyu Clans (Itura):

  • Angũi
  • Agachikũ
  • Ambui
  • Anjiru
  • Angarĩ
  • Aacera
  • Ambũra
  • Angeci
  • Airimũ

Each clan maintains its unique oral traditions, spiritual roles, and social customs. Some clans are traditionally associated with leadership, others with prophecy, and others with healing or farming expertise.
