1.6.5: Titles of the People

Understanding Respect, Roles, and Social Status in Kikuyu Society

In the Kikuyu community, titles are used to show wĩhĩtĩ (respect), thirikari (authority), and ũhoro wa ũthoni (relationships). These names and terms show how people are valued depending on their age, family role, or work.

a) Titles of Authority and Leadership

  • Mũthamaki – Chief or King. Traditional leader in the community.
  • Mũndũ wa wīra wa burũri – Political leader (e.g., MCA, MP).
  • Mũno wa athuri – Head of elders, helps solve family or village issues.
  • Mutongoria mũnene – President/head of government.

b) Maitho ma ũhonokio (Respectful Titles by Age or Status)

  • Mũkũrũ – Elder. Used to show respect to older men or women.
  • Mũtumia wa mbere – First wife, respected in traditional households.
  • Mũtumia mũrata – Mrs. or married woman.
  • Mũthoni – Miss; used to refer to unmarried young women.
  • Mũhindi – Mister/Sir.

c) Family and Youth Titles

  • Mwana – Child. A general term for any young one.
  • Mwarĩ mũtogo – Youth/young lady.
  • Kĩhii – Youth/young man.
  • Mũrata – Friend (used casually).
  • E.g. "Mũrata wakwa, wĩ nene?" (My friend, how are you?)

d) Professional and Job Titles

  • Mwarimũ / Mũrutani – Teacher. Respected for knowledge.
  • Mũrutwo – Student/learner.
  • Mũcunjĩ wa mbeca – Treasurer/accountant.
  • Mũthĩnjĩ wa wĩra – CEO/boss.
  • Mũthomi wa wĩra – Secretary.
  • Mũthamaki wa gũikaranĩria – Organizing leader.
  • Injĩnia – Engineer.
  • Mũtharitũ wa wendo – Counselor.
  • Mũrĩmũ wa ũhoro – News reader or news anchor.
  • Mũhoti – Soldier.
  • Mũrũmĩ wa ũthĩnji – Watchman/security guard.
  • Mũcĩakamũrũ – Lawyer.

e) Medical and Government Titles

  • Dagitari – Doctor or medical healer.
  • Mũgo – Traditional herbalist/healer (not necessarily negative).
  • Mũthigari – Police officer.
  • Wira mũciĩ – Staff/worker.
  • Mũcemanĩ – Messenger or courier.

f) Casual and Modern Usage

  • Kikuyu titles are often used with gentleness and humility.
  • You may hear people use:
    • "Wamũkũrũ" for elders
    • "Waĩrĩmũ" or "WaKĩmani" (child of...) for identifying people
  • "Gũtirĩ ũhoro?" as a greeting meaning "Is there peace?"
