2.1 Verbs

2.1.1 Introduction to Verbs

Verbs presuppose the presence of participants (noun phrases) which function as subjects and objects of the verb. This criterion works well for languages such as English, in which there can occur no sentence without a verb. For example, while (a) is a well-formed English sentence (b) and (c) not because they are verb-less.

a) Peter works

b) *Peter a teacher

c) *Peter good

In Dholuo, however, the presence of a verb is not a prerequisite for the well-formedness of a sentence. Thus parallel constructions to (a), (b) and (c) are well-formed in Dholuo, as the following sentences confirm:

a) Peter tiyo
Peter works.

b) Peter japuonj
'Peter is a teacher.'

c) Peter ber
'Peter is good.'

In this study, two criteria have been applied in a complementary manner to tell whether or not a given word is a verb in Dholuo. I.e

  1. In their cognitive function, verbs are symbols for events (creations, changes of state, processes, actions) for of affairs, and for speaker intention and attitude (promises, commands, hopes, expectations).
  2. The second criterion, which is based specifically on observations concerning Dholuo morphology, is that verbs are characterized by their ability formally to show aspectual distinctions tonally and/or segmentally.

For example, by the cognitive criterion above one cannot tell in a conclusive manner whether or not the words [fuwo] and [ber] are verbs in sentences (a) and (b), respectively.

a) Juma ofuwo
Juma is-foolish

b) Juma ber
Juma is-good

In both cases some kind of state of affairs is expressed, and from the English equivalents one even senses some degree of adjectiveness in the predicative expressions.

But we notice that in (a) the predicate [fuwo] takes a perfective morpheme, marked by [o-] while [ber] in (b) does not. We note further that [fuwo] can be tonally marked for imperfective aspect whereas [ber] remains aspectually neutral, as the following sentences illustrate:

a) Juma tinde f√∫wo
Juma nowadays becoming-foolish

b) *Juma tinde ber

This kind of evidence shows that the two words belong to different classes, one (fuwo) being a verb and the other (ber) an adjective.

The grammatical operators which normally satellite around the verb, although their real scope may be a larger construction (e.g. a clause) include tense, aspect, modality, and negation.
