2.7 Review & Practice

2.7.1 Summary of Adverbials & Sentence Types

Adverbials in Dholuo Grammar

Adverbials in Dholuo modify verbs, adjectives, or entire sentences by providing details about time, place, manner, frequency, degree, reason, and purpose. They enrich communication by specifying the circumstances of an action.

1. Predictions Over Events and Actions

These adverbials describe aspects of actions or states:

  • Locative: Specifies location (e.g., Ng'ato nindo e tiend yath – "Someone is sleeping under the tree").
  • Directional: Indicates movement or orientation (e.g., Nyithindo dhi e sikul – "Children are going to school").
  • Durative: Expresses duration (e.g., Ne gitiyo kuom higni apar – "They worked for ten years").
  • Extent: Denotes spatial equivalent of duration (e.g., Asewuotho mael abich – "I have walked five miles").
  • Point Time: Marks a specific time (e.g., Kenyatta notho higa mokalo – "Kenyatta died last year").
  • Manner: Describes how an action is performed (e.g., Gari ringo matek – "The train moves fast").
  • Instrument: Identifies tools used (e.g., Andiko gi kalam – "I am writing with a pen").
  • Accompaniment: Indicates an accompanying participant (e.g., Aloo dhi nam gi wuon – "Aloo is going to the lake with her father").
  • Equipment: Denotes objects used for an intended purpose (e.g., Aloo dhi nam gi ndoo – "Aloo is going to the lake with a pail").
  • Means: Shows how an action is achieved (e.g., Adhi sikul gi tienda – "I go to school on foot").
2. Predictions Over Propositions

These adverbials modify entire propositions rather than just actions:

  • Factive: Marks a proposition as truth (e.g., Kiwacho adieri to Jo-Kenya ohero pesa – "Truly speaking, Kenyans love money").
  • Modality: Expresses the speaker's attitude or possibility (e.g., Ka nyalore to Onyango biro duogo – "If possible, Onyango will return").
  • Concession: Introduces unexpected outcomes (e.g., Kata aonge pesa to nyaka abed gi ot – "Even if I have no money, I must have a house").
3. Predictions of Motivations, Conditions, and Transition in Dholuo

This category of adverbials explains causality, conditions, and transitions in discourse:

  • Purpose: Expresses intent or motive (Ng'atno odere mondo oa e chandruok – "That man committed suicide to escape problems").
  • Cause: Identifies reasons for events (Kech nitie nikech piny notho – "There is famine because the crops failed").
  • Conditional: Specifies prerequisite conditions (Ka oyanya to abiro goye – "If he insults me, I'll beat him").
  • Transitional: Signals shifts in thought or state (Kata kamano to adwa neno jatelo – "However, I'd like to see the boss"), often overlapping with concessional functions.

Quantifiers, Comparatives, and Interrogative Adverbials in Dholuo

This category of adverbials conveys frequency, intensity, comparison, and inquiry:

  • Frequency: Indicates recurrence (Okadho ka pile – "He passes here every day").
  • Intensifier: Amplifies magnitude (Japuonj no ofuwo ahinya – "That teacher is very foolish").
  • Comparative: Establishes relative difference (Otieno riek moloyo owadgi – "Otieno is cleverer than his brother").

Interrogative Adverbs, often predicating over propositions, include:

  • Locative/Directional: John nindo kanye? – "Where is John sleeping?"
  • Temporal: Ibiro nena karang'o? – "When are you coming to see me?"
  • Purpose/Cause: Ukelo bunde ka nang'o? – "Why are you bringing a gun here?"
  • Means: Ere kaka anyalo yudo pesa kawuono? – "How can I get money today?"

Sentence Types in Dholuo

Sentence structures in Dholuo encode distinct communicative functions through syntactic patterns. The primary sentence types include:

Declarative Sentences

Used for assertions and descriptions, occurring in various clause structures:

  • SV (Subject + Verb): Omolo biro ("Omolo is coming").
  • S Cs (Subject + Subject Complement): Omolo ber ("Omolo is good").
  • SVO (Subject + Verb + Object): Ng'ato onego jajwok ("Someone has killed a wizard").
  • S CLA (Subject + Locative Adverbial Complement): Thuol ni e ot ("A snake is in the house").
  • S VOA (Subject + Verb + Object + Locative Adverbial): Apiyo keto pala e tiende ("Apiyo is putting the knife on her foot").
  • S V ID DO (Subject + Verb + Indirect Object + Direct Object): Mama miyo nyathi chiemo ("Mother is giving the baby food").
  • SVO Co (Subject + Verb + Object + Object Complement): Ji oketo Opiyo ruoth ("People have made Opiyo their chief").
Interrogatives Sentences

Interrogative sentences in Dholuo serve two primary functions: seeking information and seeking confirmation.

1. Information-Seeking Questions

These questions incorporate interrogative words within declarative sentence structures, filling an informational gap. Key question words include:

Ng'a (who), ang'o (what), nade (how), nang'o (why), dak (why, negative), kanye (where), ere (where is), mane (which one), adi (how many).

Compound forms such as mar ang'o (why, for what) and nikech ang'o (why, because of what) provide additional nuance.

Structurally, information-seeking questions follow predictable patterns:

  • Q V DO: Ng'a ma kelo koko? ("Who is causing trouble?")
  • S V Q: Juma otimo ang'o? ("What has Juma done?")
  • S V IO Q: Otieno omiyo nyathi ang'o? ("What has Otieno given the baby?")
  • S V O Q: Giyiero Omolo ang'o? ("What have they made Omolo?")
  • S COP Q: Owadu ni kanye? ("Where is your brother?")
2. Confirmation-Seeking Questions

These are formed by:

  • Tonal variation in declaratives.
  • Interrogative markers:
    • Bende (seeks neutral confirmation, e.g., Bende ng'ato pidho nyathi? "Is anyone feeding the baby?")
    • Donge (expects confirmation, e.g., Donge ubiro? "Aren't you coming?")
    • Koso (expects negation, e.g., Koso ubiro? "Might you be coming?"
Imperatives Sentences

Imperative constructions in Dholuo are structurally defined by the absence of an overt subject and the presence of a main verb in the active voice. Their primary functions are to issue commands and make requests, with verb modifications distinguishing these uses.

1. Command Formation

Commands are generally formed using the verb root, with modifications based on phonological structure:

  • Vowel, Nasal, Glide, and Liquid-final roots: The verb root remains unchanged.
    • kel ('bring' from kelo)
    • tem ('try' from temo)
    • ne ('see' from neno, with phonological reduction)
  • Glide-final roots: Dholuo prohibits final palatal glides, so such roots become vowel-final.
    • go ('beat' from goyo)
    • we ('leave' from weyo)
  • Obstruent-final roots: Require the suffix -i when sentence-final.
    • losi ('talk' from loso)
    • duoki ('return' from duoko)
  • Reflexive verbs: Require -i universally.
    • tamri ('refuse' from tamruok)
    • gori ('fight' from goruok)

For plural commands, non-reflexive verbs take -uru, while reflexive verbs insert -e before -uru.

  • kawuru ('take!')
  • tamreuru ('refuse!')
2. Request Formation

Requests are softer imperatives and employ distinct suffixation:

  • Without pronominal objects:
    • lupie ('please fish')
    • chamie ('please eat')
  • With pronominal objects:
    • make ('catch him') → makee ('please, catch him')
    • kelgi ('bring them') → kelgie ('please, bring them')

Alternative request marker: The addition of ane/anena to command or request forms reinforces politeness.

  • bedie piny ('please sit down') → bed ane piny ('please, sit down!')
3. Plural Requests

Plural request forms append -uru to the relevant singular request forms.

  • kelieuru pala ('please bring a knife!')
  • tang' ane uru lwetu ('please show me your hands!')
