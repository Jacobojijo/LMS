2.6.3 Interrogatives

The most typical function of the interrogative sentence is to ask questions. It is this function that shall be our concern here. The main question-types in Dholuo are (i) those that seek information and (ii) those that seek confirmation. They include the following:

Non-Emphatic

ng'a [Pl. ng'a gini] 'who'
ang'o 'what'
Nade 'how'
nang'o 'why'
Dak 'why (negative)'
Kanye 'where'
Ere 'where is'
Mane 'which one'
Adi 'how many'

In addition to these, speakers (depending on personal taste) can form such compound forms as [mar ang'o] 'why (for what)', [nikech ang'o] 'why (because of what)', [mondo ang'o] 'so that what (may happen)', etc.

Generally, the clause patterns for information-seeking questions are the same as the patterns for their corresponding declaratives, the significant difference being that in the former there is a question word filling some structural position.

Placing a question word in preverbal position necessitates the relativization of what comes after it if the information sought by the question word can be given in the form of a nominal (phrase). For example, in the following pairs of semantically equivalent sentences:

Ng'at no dwaro ang'o?
What does that person want?

Ang'o ma ngat no dwaro?
What does that person want?

Ere pala?
Where is the knife?

Pala ere?
Where is the knife?

The information required by (a,b) would normally be given in the form of a nominal, e.g. [odwaro) pesa] '(He wants) money.', while the information required by (c,d) would normally take the form of a prepositional phrase, e.g. (Pala) ni e wi mesa '(The knife is) on the table. This explains why we have a relative clause after ang'o in (b) but not after [ere] in (d).

Representing the Question word with [a] Q, we can, following our observations above, identify some of the relevant clause patterns as follows:

Q V DO, e.g.

Ng'a ma kelo koko
Who is causing trouble?

Ang'o ma yiengo ot
What is shaking the house?

S V Q, e.g.

Juma otimo ang'o
What has Juma done?

Kwach onego ng'a
Whom has the leopard killed?

S V IO Q, e.g.

Otieno omiyo nyathi ang'o
What has Otieno given the baby?

Ong'iewo ni ang'o
What has he bought you?

S V Q DO, e.g.

Ikelo ne ng'a gigo
To whom are you bringing those things?

S V O Q, e.g.

Giyiero Omolo ang'o?
What have they made Omolo?

S V Q C, e.g.

Onyango oloko ang'o mesa
What has Onyango converted into a table?

S P Q (P = Predicate), e.g.

Jodongo chiemo kanye
Where the elders eating?

S COP. Q (COP = locative copula) e.g.

Owadu ni kanye
Where is your brother?

Q S V, e.g.

Ang'o ma ng'at no dwaro
What does that person want?

There are many clause patterns for information-seeking questions which we can in Dholuo. The important principle is that in every such construction there is an informational gap which is indicated by the presence of a question word in some position.

Confirmation-seeking questions are formed either (i) by merely expanding the tonal register of a declarative construction, or (ii) by using an appropriate question word.

The key words used in the second strategy are [bende, donge, and koso], all of which ask for the confirmation of an accompanying statement. The defining characteristic of a confirmation-seeking question is that it can be adequately responded to by merely saying either [ee] 'yes' or [ooyo] 'no'. Bende usually occurs either sentence-initially or after the subject, e.g.

Bende ng'ato pidho nyathi?
Is anyone feeding the baby?

With subjects of more specific reference than [ng'ato], the occurrence of [bende] after the subject (or any other referring expression) may suggest a restriction of the scope of [bende] to the expression in question with the sense of 'as well' added to its general role as confirmation-seeking question word. Thus, for instance, the sentence

Atieno bende biro
May mean either 'Is Atieno coming? or 'Is Atieno also coming?' (that is, we know someone else who is).

The important semantic point about [bende] is that it shows only the speaker's desire to know whether the propositional content of the X-element in any sentence of the form bende X (where X is a declarative construction) is or is not the case. For example, the interest of the person who asks the question (as far as we can tell from the wording alone)

Bende joUlaya puro?
Do Europeans cultivate (their land)?

is only to know whether it is or is not the case that

Jo Ulaya puro
Europeans cultivate (their land).

On the other hand, using [donge], as in

Donge ubiro
Aren't you coming?

suggests not only that the speaker expects the X-element of the sentence [donge] X to be true, but also that he has an interest in its confirmation.

If the speaker does not expect the X-element in any sentence QX? (where Q is a confirmation-seeking question word) to be true, then he uses the word [koso], as in

Koso ubiro
Might you be coming?
Do you eat fish? (I assume that you do not)

Thus [koso] has an inherent negative meaning, rather like (though perhaps not to the same degree as) the information-seeking question word dak 'why not', as in the sentence

Dak iwuo
Why don't you talk?

Some of the question words, e.g. [donge], are fairly free in their occurrence relative to the other constituents in a sentence. Thus, we can say

Donge yamo kudho.
Isn't (the) wind blowing?

Yamo donge kudho?

Yamo kudho.
