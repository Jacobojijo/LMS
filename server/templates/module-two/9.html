2.6 Sentence Types

2.6.1 Introduction

The sentence types in a language depend on which of these communicative functions are regularly coded in the language by special syntactic patterns. In general, where they occur, declarative sentences are used to make assertions, presentative sentences are used to introduce new information, interrogative sentences are used to ask questions and make requests, and imperative sentences are used to give commands (and also make requests).

Three of these, namely declarative, interrogative, and imperative sentence types are overtly marked in Dholuo, where they are characterized as follows:

2.6.2 Declaratives

Taking only obligatory constituents into consideration, Dholuo declaratives occur in the following clause patterns (the English equivalent given does not necessarily fall under the same pattern as its Dholuo counterpart):

Subject + Verb (SV), e.g.

1. Omolo biro

'Omolo is coming.'

2. Nyako cha tedo.

'That girl is cooking.'

In general, this clause pattern consists of a Subject Noun Phrase (of any cornplexity) followed by an intransitive verb.

Subject + Subject Complement (S Cs), e.g.

1. Omolo ber

'Omolo is good.'

2. Jatichna jakuo

'My servant is a thief.'

Here the predicate consists of a nominal or adjectival complement of the subject in question.

Subject + Verb + Object (S V O), e.g.

1. Ng'ato onego jajwok

'Someone has killed a wizard.'

2. Juma ochamo ring'o manumu

'Juma has eaten raw meat.'

Subject + Locative Adverbial Complement (S CLA), e.g.

1. Thuol ni e ot

'A snake is in the house.'

2. Nyasaye ni e polo

'God is in heaven.'

Note that since [ni] functions as a locative copula, we regard it here as part of the complement phrase.

Subject + Verb + Object + Locative Adverb (S VOA), e.g.

1. Apiyo keto pala e tiende

'Apiyo is putting the knife on her foot.'

2. Ng'ato oolo pi e(i) puga

'Someone has poured water in a gourd.'

Subj + Verb + Indirect Object + Direct Object (S V ID DO), e.g.

1. Mama miyo nyathi chiemo

'Mother is giving the baby food.'

2. Baba okelo ne nyathi nanga

'Father has brought the baby a dress.'

Subj + Verb + Object + Obj Complement (SV O Co) e.g.

1. Ji oketo Opiyo ruoth

'(The) people have made Opiyo (their) chief.'

2. Uloko Otieno raura.

'You have turned Otieno into a fool.'

Since the clause patterns given above have only obligatory constituents, it needs to be noted that any one of them can be extended by adding some appropriate (usually adverbial) constituent to give some more information.
