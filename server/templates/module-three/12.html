3.8 Cleft Constructions

3.8.1 Introduction

The sentences in (a, b) below represent what are referred to as cleft constructions.

Rech ema nyathi dwaro
'It is fish that the child wants.'
Gi ma nyathi dwaro en rech
Gi ma japuonj ni timo en (mana) chwado nyithindo
'What this teacher does is beat children.'
Japuonj no ema jodongo ohero
'It is that teacher whom the elders like.'

The notion of a cleft-construction is based on the apparent relationship between constructions such as the ones given above and those in (57) below.

Nyathi dwaro rech
'(The) child want(s) fish.'
Japuonj ni chwado nyithindo
'This teacher beats(s) children.'
Jodongo obero japuonj no
'(The) elders like that teacher.'

Relating (a-d) to (e-g), we can say that (e) is the non-cleft form of (a) and (b); (f) is the non-cleft form of (c); and (g) is the non-cleft form of (d).

The cleft-constructions themselves fall into two categories, namely, cleft-constructions proper (a) and (d)) and pseudo-cleft constructions (b) and (c)). To the extent that both of them are believed to 'split up' what is a unified phrase in a corresponding non-cleft constructions they are both cleft-constructions.

The differenceis that in the pseudo-cleft ones the verb still has what, in traditional terms, can be called its object. In either case, the non-cleft variety is considered to be the marked form. That is why transformational accounts of pseudo-cleft constructions work with deep structures corresponding more closely to the non-cleft constructions.

From their surface forms, there is no doubt that cleft-constructions are Focus constructions. The pseudo-cleft ones involve only the insertion of some lexical forms in certain positions; and this can easily be handled by expression rules and the procedure for building predications from predicate frames.

The same can be said of cleft-constructions in which an alpha term has a Focus function and is, therefore, placed in its right position according to the fimctional patterns of a language and a Focus marker (e (ma)) postposed to it by expression rules.

This will take care of those cleftconstructions which would not require a movement rule even in a transformational account. Here, such cases include (b) and (c).

Thus, the only kind of cleft-construction that would require a movement rule in a TG account is the cleft-construction proper, in our case, (a) and (d), in which non-alpha terms [rech] and [japuonjno], respectively, stand in the preverbal position.

In the approach adopted here, (a) and (d) do not present any problem so long as we think of them as normal Focus constructions in which the Focus constituents are non-alpha terms.
