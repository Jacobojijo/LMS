3.9.1 Summary of Words Ordering, Word Fronting & Cleft Constructions

Expression Rules & Constituent Order.

Introduction

Functional Grammar (FG) assumes that predications are unordered constructions.

Expression rules determine linear order by prioritizing different preferential tendencies in constituent arrangement.

Preferential Tendencies in Ordering

Functional Consistency: Constituents with the same function appear in fixed positions.

Special Positions for Pragmatic Functions: Certain constituents (e.g., Focus, Topic) take designated positions.

Categorial Complexity: Constituents are ordered from simple to complex (left to right).

Dholuo Functional Pattern

Semantic Functions (SFs) govern syntactic positions.

Alpha term (Agent) → Preverbal Position (A).

One of the first three non-alpha terms (Goal, Recipient, Beneficiary) → Immediately postverbal position (Ø).

Basic Functional Order:

Theme, P1 V Ø

Theme and Tail are external to the predication.

Postverbal Constituent Combinations (Verb Placement - V)

V Go (Most common, transitive action verbs)

Ng'ato tedo rech → "Someone is cooking fish."

V Rec Go (Rare, Recipient without preposition)

Ng'ato omiyo Atieno pesa mathoth → "Someone has given Atieno a lot of money."

V Ben Go (Rare, Beneficiary without preposition)

Jatedo otuono japidi chiemo → "The cook has denied the nurse food."

V Go Loc (Locative occurs post-Goal within nuclear predication)

Mama oketo kuon e mesa → "Mother has put ugali on the table."

VØ (Zero semantic function, typical in state predications)

Ng'at cha paro chiege → "That man is thinking of his wife."

Flexibility of Non-Alpha Terms

X-constituents (e.g., Beneficiary) can be postposed after the binding constituent without altering grammaticality.

Ng'ato tedo rech newelo → "Someone is cooking fish for the visitors."

Word Fronting in Dholuo: A Functional Analysis

Introduction

Word fronting in Dholuo involves placing question words in preverbal positions to identify non-alpha terms. This results in paraphrase variations where question words may appear before or after the verb, as seen in:

Nyathi dwaro ang'o? ('What does the child want?')
Ang'o ma nyathi dwaro? ('What is it that the child wants?')

While non-alpha terms can shift positions, interrogatives targeting alpha terms lack similar flexibility:

Ng'a ma dwaro ruoth? ('Who wants the chief?')

Typology of Interrogative Constructions in Dholuo

Dholuo exhibits multiple strategies for forming interrogative constructions:

Straight Interrogatives: These employ question words in their standard positions.

Bende oyud jakuo? ('Has the thief been found?')
Ere wuon ot? ('Where is the owner of the house?')

Questioned Focus Constructions: These highlight an alpha term using a focus marker (ma).

Ng'a ma chamo punda? ('Who eats a donkey?')
Punda ng'a ma chamo? (= above)

Interrogative Variations in Free or Complementary Distribution:

Jakuo okawo ang'o? ('What has the thief taken?')
Ang'o ma jakuo okawo? ('What is it that the thief has taken?')
Sibuor ochamo ng'a? ('Whom has the lion eaten?')
Ng'a ma sibuor ochamo? ('Who is it that the lion has eaten?')

Focus in Interrogative and Declarative Constructions

Focus-marked constructions emphasize the subject or object for clarity. For example:

Juma kelo kong'o. ('Juma is bringing beer.') → Answers: Juma timo ang'o? ('What is Juma doing?')
Juma ema kelo kong'o. ('It is Juma who is bringing beer.') → Answers: Ng'a ma kelo kong'o? ('Who is bringing beer?')

Positioning of Time Adverbs (TAs)

Permutation in Transformational Grammar: The placement of time adverbs like kawuono ('today') varies across sentence structures while maintaining meaning.

Illustration of Flexibility:

Otieno nyalo loso kom ne min kawuono.
Otieno kawuono nyalo loso kom ne min.
Kawuono Otieno nyalo loso kom ne min.

General Rule:

TA placement is flexible but cannot occur between the predicate and binding constituent (V O Ob).

Topicalization & Pragmatic Use:

TA in PI (pre-initial) position marks Topic (e.g., Kawuono Otieno loso kom…).

Pragmatic testing: Nyoro Otieno notimo ang'o? To kawuono? → Kawuono Otieno loso kom… is the best response.

Cleft Constructions

Definition: A syntactic structure that splits a unified phrase into distinct elements for Focus.

Types:

Cleft proper: Focused noun phrase precedes the verb.

Rech ema nyathi dwaro ('It is fish that the child wants').
Japuonj no ema jodongo ohero ('It is that teacher whom the elders like').

Pseudo-cleft: Uses relative clauses while retaining object-verb order.

Gi ma nyathi dwaro en rech ('What the child wants is fish').
Gi ma japuonj ni timo en chwado nyithindo ('What the teacher does is beat children').

Transformation & Focus:

Clefting emphasizes Focus, distinguishing Topic from Comment.

Cleft-proper structures involve syntactic movement, while pseudo-clefts rely on lexical insertion.

Transformational analysis treats pseudo-clefts as derived from simpler (non-cleft) structures.

Functional Interpretation:

Both types operate as Focus markers, structuring information according to discourse needs.

Positioning of the Word "Ere"

The lexical item ere functions as a locative copula, equivalent to ni kanye ('is where?'), but with distinct syntactic constraints:

Ere only occurs in the present tense:

❌ Pala ne ere (Incorrect)
✅ Palane ni kanye? (Correct)

Ere precedes pronominal subjects:

✅ Ere-i? ('Where are you?')
❌ I-ere? (Incorrect)

For full noun phrases, ere cannot replace ni kanye in focus constructions:

✅ Kanye ma pala nitie. ('Where the knife is.')
❌ Kanye pala. (Incorrect)

Positioning of the Negative Element "Ok"

The negative morpheme ok can appear in two main syntactic positions but must immediately follow an alpha term (typically a referring expression):

Sentence-initial negative placement:

✅ Ok ng'at ni nyal ketho gira kamano. ('This man cannot spoil my property like that.')
✅ Ng'at ni ok nyal ketho gira kamano. (Same meaning, different structure)
❌ Ng'at ni nyal ok ketho gira kamano. (Incorrect)

When a topicalized phrase precedes the alpha term (gira is topicalized here):

✅ Gira ok ng'at ni nyal ketho kamano.
✅ Gira ng'at ni ok nyal ketho kamano.
❌ Ok gira ng'at ni nyal ketho kamano. (Incorrect)

In the absence of an alpha term, ok must appear immediately before the verb:

✅ Ok okel ne japidi nanga. ('The nurse was not brought a dress.')
❌ Okel ok ne japidi nanga. (Incorrect)

With auxiliary verbs, ok precedes the auxiliary:

✅ Ok bi neg diel ne welo. ('A goat will not be slaughtered for the guests.')
❌ Bi ok neg diel ne welo. (Incorrect)

Interaction with Time Adverbs (TA):

✅ Otieno ok bi neno sinema kawuono. ('Otieno won't watch the film today.')
✅ Kawuono Otieno ok bi neno sinema.
❌ Ok kawuono Otieno bi neno sinema. (Incorrect)

Conclusion

Ere is a present-tense locative copula with strict word order constraints.

Ok follows the alpha term and must precede auxiliaries and verbs.

Time adverbs can shift positions, but ok retains its placement constraints.
