3.9.3 Positioning of the Negative Element "Ok"

The negative morpheme [ok] can occur in two structural positions, namely, either immediately before or immediately after the sentence-initial constituent (referring expression). We can see this kind of distribution in the following constructions, of which only two are well-formed.

1. *Ok ng'at ni nyal ketho gira kamano*
'This man cannot spoil my property like that.'
1. *Ng'at ni Ok nyal ketho gira kamano (=a) *
2. **Ng'at ni nyal ok ketho gira kamano*
3. **Ng'at ni nyal ketho ok gira kamano*
4. **Ng'at ni nyal ketho gira kamano ok*

The general statement made above concerning the positioning of ok needs some modification in the light of what we see in (a, b) below.

1. *Gira ok ng'at ni nyal ketho kamano *
Go Top
1. Gira ng'at ni ok nyal ketho kamano
Go Top

In (a, b- above) a referring expression [gira], comes into sentence-initial position by Top assignment. However, the term that [ok] can occur immediately after or before is still [ng'at ni]. An attempt to apply our general statement, which refers to the sentence-initial term, to (a, b above) will produce (a- above) and (a-below).

1. **Ok gira ng'at ni nyal ketho kamano*

But (a- above) is ill-formed, or at least, highly marked (according to some Dholuo speakers).

We can explain these observations by saying that the crucial constituent for the positioning of [ok] in the above examples is [ ng'at ni] or, generally, the alpha term in the construction. Thus, our statement of the distribution of [ok] is more accurate if we it to say that the negative morpheme [ok] is placed immediately after the alpha term.

What happens in the case of predications without an alpha term? E.g.

1. *Okel ne japidi nanga *
'The nurse has been brought a dress.'

There are five positions in which [ok] can stand as shown below.

1. *Ok okel ne japidi nanga*
2. **Okel ok ne japidi nanga*
3. **Okel ne ok japidi nanga*
4. **Okel ne japidi nanga ok*

Of these five possibilities, only (a) is well-formed. This suggests that in the absence of an alpha term [ok] must stay in the preverbal position anyway. Moreover, since pragmatic function assignment can place some non-alpha term before the verb, as in (a- and b-below), we must insist on the immediate preverbal position.

1. *Japidi ok okel-ne nanga*
2. *Japidi e ma ok okel-ne nanga *

But an element which is regarded as being co-functional with the verb, e.g. [bi(ro)] 'to come' as a FUT marker, must also be preceded by [ok]. Consider the possibilities revealed below.

1. *Ok bineg dielne wendo kill. *
'A goat will not be slaughtered for the guests.'
1. *Diel ok bi neg ne welo*
2. *Welo ok bi neg ne-gi diel*
3. **Bi ok neg diel ne welo*
4. **Diel bi ok neg ne welo*

The facts of (a-e) are consistent with our observations above so long as we maintain the view expressed earlier in this chapter that auxiliaries and their main verbs are, in any given predication, co-fillers of the V position.

However, there is one more constituent whose placement with respect to [ok] still requires some investigation: The Time Adverb (TA). The ordering problems concerning the TA. The real concern here is how it relates to the positioning of [ok]. For some insight into the problem, let us consider the constructions in (83) below.

1. *Otieno ok bi oeno sinema kawuono*
'Otieno won't watch the film today.'
1. *Otieno kawuono ok bi neno sinema *
2. *Kawuono Otieno ok bi neno sinema *
3. *Otieno ok kawuono bi neno sinema*
4. *Ok kawuono Otieno bi neno sinema*
