3.4.1 Summary of Predicate Frames & Expression Rules

Predicate Frames

Introduction

Predicate frames in Dholuo define the structural patterns that govern the relationship between verbs and their arguments (subjects, objects, complements). They determine:

  • The number of arguments a verb requires (one, two, or three).
  • The syntactic and semantic roles of arguments (e.g., agent, patient).
  • Possible modifications to the verb (tense, aspect, negation).

Classification of Predicate Frames

A. Intransitive Predicate Frames (One-argument structures)

  • These verbs require only a subject and do not take an object.
  • Structure: (NP) + V (e.g., Omondi owuok – "Omondi has left.")
  • Types:
    • Unaccusative verbs (experiencer subject, e.g., tho "to die").
    • Unergative verbs (agent subject, e.g., wuok "to leave").

B. Monotransitive Predicate Frames (Two-argument structures)

  • Require a subject and a direct object.
  • Structure: (NP) + V + (NP) (e.g., Omondi neno ot – "Omondi is seeing a house.").
  • Typically describe actions affecting an object (neno "to see," kelo "to bring").

C. Ditransitive Predicate Frames (Three-argument structures)

  • Require a subject, an indirect object, and a direct object.
  • Structure: (NP) + V + (NP) + (NP) (e.g., Omondi miyo nyar gi pesa – "Omondi is giving their girl money.").
  • Common in verbs of transfer (miyo "to give," tang'o "to show").

Satellites in Predicate Frames

Satellites provide additional information about a sentence but do not define its core structure. They modify a predication by specifying:

  • Time (e.g., Achola kelo pi sani – "Achola is bringing water now.").
  • Manner (e.g., Jathum miel mamit – "The musician is singing beautifully.").
  • Location (e.g., Otieno dhi nam – "Otieno is going to the lake.").

Syntax Functions

Functional Grammar (FG) traditionally recognizes only two syntactic functions: Subject (Subj) and Object (Obj). The Subj function refers to the entity from which the predication's perspective is determined, while the Obj serves as the secondary vantage point. This distinction is particularly relevant in active-passive transformations in languages like English.

However, in Dholuo, Subj-Obj assignment proves inadequate for describing constituent order. Instead, Dholuo syntax is governed by semantic and pragmatic functions without reliance on syntactic roles.

Dholuo follows an unmarked constituent order: 1 V 2 ... n, where V represents the verb, and 1, 2, … n denote term positions dictated by the verb's valency. Observations on Dholuo syntax reveal:

  • The alpha term (e.g., Otieno, Jatedo) always precedes the verb.
  • The post-verbal position (2) is occupied by the highest-ranked non-alpha function (e.g., Goal, Recipient, Beneficiary).
  • Lower-ranked terms follow hierarchical positioning unless pragmatic functions alter their placement.

Given that Subj-Obj assignment lacks descriptive relevance in Dholuo, syntactic functions (as used in FG) are deemed unnecessary. Instead, constituent order is best explained through semantic role hierarchies (e.g., Agent, Goal, Beneficiary) and pragmatic considerations.

Pragmatic Functions

Pragmatic functions (PFs) in FG serve to mark the informational structure of utterances. They are classified as:

  • External PFs: Theme (frames discourse relevance) and Tail (provides afterthoughts).
  • Internal PFs: Topic (sets referential focus) and Focus (marks the most salient information).

Themes & Tails Functions

Theme: Introduces the discourse framework, limiting relevance to a specific referent:

Nyako ma biro no en osiepni? → That coming girl, is she your friend?
Joluo, gin gikia ohala. → The Luo, they do not know business matters.

Tail: Appends additional information post-predication, often as an afterthought:

Ok adware, wiye duong' no. → I don't want him, that big-headed one.
Ohero koko, en. → He likes noise, him.

Topic Function

The Topic function in Functional Grammar assigns prominence to an entity about which a predication makes a statement. In Dholuo:

  • The Topic is preverbal.
  • Alpha terms always appear before the verb, so they do not gain prominence solely from being Topics.
  • Any term (nuclear or satellite) can be assigned the Topic function under appropriate pragmatic conditions.

Examples:

Inego kwach → 'A leopard is being killed.' (General information)
Kwach inego → 'The leopard is being killed.' (Focus on 'kwach')

Contextual Differences:

Q: Ango ma timore? ('What is happening?') → A: Inego kwach.
Q: Itimo kwach nade? ('What is happening to the leopard?') → A: Kwach inego.
Nyithindo nego kwach → 'Children are killing a leopard.'
Kwach nyithindo nego → 'The leopard is being killed by children.'

Focus Function

Focus identifies the most salient information in a sentence. There are six types:

  • Completive Focus – Fills missing information.
  • Selective Focus – Highlights one option among several.
  • Replacing Focus – Corrects prior information.
  • Expanding Focus – Adds to given information.
  • Restricting Focus – Narrows a presupposed set.
  • Parallel Focus – Contrasts two pieces of information.

Dholuo Focus Markers:

  • e (primary marker)
  • ma (used in relative clauses)
  • e ma (combined for emphasis)

Examples:

Otieno e wuoyi. → 'Otieno is the boy.' (Contrastive Focus)
Otieno wuoyi. → 'Otieno is a boy.' (General statement)
Otieno e ma nyalo konyowa. → 'Otieno is the one who can help us.'
Chunya e ma rach. → 'I am just in low spirits (nothing else is wrong).'
Ang'o ma nyathino dwaro kanyo? → 'What does the child want there?'

Expression Rules in Dholuo

These rules govern how grammatical structures are realized.

Linguistic Features Covered:

  • Case and adpositions.
  • Predicate realization (voice, auxiliaries, agreement).
  • Word order.
  • Stress and intonation.

Verbal Predicate Expression:

  • Passive Form if no agent is mentioned:
    Oneg diel. → 'A goat has been killed.'
  • Active Form if an alpha term (agent) is present:
    Ng'ato nego diel. → 'Someone is killing a goat.'
  • Preverbal Positioning affects verb morphology:
    Wendo wanego-ne diel. → 'The guest we are slaughtering a goat for.'

Semantic Functions & Prepositional Marking

Systemic Functional Grammar (SFG) relates semantic functions (SFs) to prepositional markers in surface structures. The rules governing this relationship are:

Zero Prepositional Marking

If the SF is Ø, PO, Fo, Proc, or Dir (with no content-word preposition), there is no prepositional marking.

Example: Nyithindo dhi nam (Direction) → "The children are going to the lake."

Possession Marking

  • Singular possession: mar precedes the term.
    Example: Pesa ni mar japidi → "This money belongs to the nurse."
  • Plural possession: mag precedes the term.
    Example: Pesa gi mag japidi → "This money belongs to the nurses."

Companion/Instruction Marking

gi is used before the term.

Example: Nyathi wuotho gi min (Companion) → "The child is walking with its mother."

Beneficiary & Recipient Marking

ne precedes the term.

Example: Ang'iewo buk ne nyathina (Beneficiary) → "I have bought a book for my child."

Location, Source, or Direction Marking

e precedes the term.

Example: Aketo chiemo e wi mesa (Location) → "I've placed food on the table."

Special Directional Marker (yo)

Functions as a content-word preposition, disallowing other general prepositions.

Example: Atieno odhi yo sikul → "Atieno has gone in the direction of the school."
Yo sikul ema Atieno (Focus) → "It is in the direction of the school that Atieno has gone."

Pragmatic Functions & Morphological Effects

Theme & Tail

Positioned outside the predication proper. No special marking needed.

Topic Assignment

No morphological changes unless a prepositionally marked term moves to the preverbal position.

Focus Assignment

Morphologically marked by e (ma) immediately after the term.

Example: Juma e ma ji dwaro (Focus) → "It is Juma that the people want."
Juma e ma ji oriedo-ne (Focus + verb modification)

Semantic roles dictate prepositional markers: (e.g., mar for singular possession, gi for companionship).

Focus function introduces e (ma): e.g., Juma e ma ji dwaro → "It is Juma that the people want."

Directional preposition (yo) behaves uniquely: necessitates preverbal positioning with an added e suffix.
