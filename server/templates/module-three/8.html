3.2.3 Expression of Semantic & Pragmatic Functions

Expression of Semantic Functions

The following expression rules are required to relate SFS (Systemic Functional Grammar) to their corresponding prepositional markers in the surface structure.

If the SF of a term is Ã˜, PO, Fo, Proc, or Dir (where there is no content-word preposition), then there is zero prepositional marking for the term, e.g.:

Nyithindo dhi nam (Direction)
'The children are gomg to the lake.'

If the SF of a term is Poss, and the possessed element is singular, then the term is immediately preceded by mar, e.g.:

Pesa ni mar japidi(Possesion)
'This money (sg.) belongs to the nurse.'

If the SF of a term is Poss, and the possessed element is plural, then the term is immediately preceded by mag, e.g.:

Pesa gi mag japidi (Possesion)
This money (pl.) belongs to the nurse.'

If the SF of a term is Companion or Instruction, then the term is immediately preceded by (gi), e.g.

Nyathi wuotho gi min (Companion)
The child is walking with its mother.'

If the SF of a term is Ben or Rec, then the term is immediately preceded by ne, e.g.

Ang'iewo buk ne nyathina. (Ben)
I have bought a book for my child.'

If the SF of a term is Loc, So, or Dirirection (Where there is a content-word preposition), then the term is immediately preceded by [e], e.g.

Aketo chiemo e wi mesa (Loc)
'I've placed food on the table.'

One preposition whose realization has not been dealt with here is the non-target Direction marker [yo]. This preposition is better treated as a content-word preposition though it has the properties of both a specific and a general preposition. Like the latter, it does not allow any other general preposition to accompany it. Thus, only predications like (a) are well formed, and not those like (b).

Adeno odhi yo sikul
'Atieno has gone in the direction of the school.'
*Afieno odhi e yo sikul

But, like content-word prepositions, it goes to the preverbal position with its term, normally necessitating the suffixing of the general preposition e to the verbal predicate. Hence the realization of predications such as (a).

Yo sikul ema Atieno. (Foc)
'It is in the direction of the school that Atieno has gone.'

It, therefore, seems proper to regard yo as a content-word preposition whose general prepositional marker is realized only when the term in which it occurs has a PF function.

Expression of Pragmagtic Functions

Since the Theme and Tail of a predication are, in principle, outside the predication proper, their positioning is self-evident from a schema such as the one given in (sections discussing Theme & Tail).

Given that the assignment of these two functions does not lead to any special marking on the surface realizations of a predication, we shall say no more about them here.

The main concern of this section is with the PFs which are within the predication proper, namely, Topic and Focus.

As we have seen in (3.2.1.4), Topic assignment as such does not have any morphological consequences. However, if a term whose SF is prepositionally marked is assigned the Topic function and, consequently, has to stand in the preverbal position, there will be a morphological effect on the verb form following the specifications of expression rule (I (BC).

As we have already explained, the assignment of the PF of Focus has, as its morphological consequence, the introduction of [e (ma)], realized immediately after the term in question. This fact may be stated by expression rule (i).

If a term Xl is assigned Focus function, then the term Xl is realized as ...Xl e(ma)..., e.g.:

Juma e ma ji dwaro. (Focus)
'It is Juma that the people want.'

As in the case of Topic assignment, the morphological consequences of Focus assignment on the verb will be handled by our expression rule (iii on verbal predicate), e.g.

Juma e ma ji oriedo-ne (Foc)
'It is for Juma that the people have lined up

Directional preposition (yo) behaves uniquely: necessitates preverbal positioning with an added e suffix.
