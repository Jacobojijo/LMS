3.2.1.4 Topic

According to the standard Functional Grammar theory, the Topic function has the following three characteristics:

  1. it is assigned to the constituent designating 'the about which the predication predicates something in the given setting'.
  2. such an entity is usually 'coreferential to a constituent in a preceding question (or in a question that could have preceded)'
  3. 'any term of a predication, regardless of its semantic and syntactic function may for Topic function, given the appropriate pragmatic conditions'.

The special position for Topic constituents in Dholuo is the preverbal position. Since alpha terms must stand before the verb if they occur in a predication, they do not gain any prominence by having the Topic function. Let us take the following predication:

1. Inego kwach 'A leopard is being killed,'

In such a predication, the non-alpha term [kwach] can, if assigned the Topic function, stand before the verb as in:

1. Kwach inego

Although (a) and (b) have the same meaning to th√© extent that they bear the same truth conditions, they perform different communicative functions. The former provides information as to what is taking place (in general, at a given time and place), but the latter gives information on what is happening to [kwach] in particular. Thus, for instance, to the question:

1. Ango ma timore 'What is happening here?

The predication expressed by (a) constitutes an appropriate answer, while its counterpart (b) does not. The latter would be an appropriate response to the question:

1. Itimo kwach nade? 'What is being done to the leopard?'

where it may, if communicatively found appropriate, be replaced by the non-alpha pronoun [-e], as in:

1. Ineg-e 'It is being killed.'

Again, it should be noted that (e) cannot replace (a) as a response to (c). Now take the predication:

1. Nyithindo nego kwach 'Children are killing a leopard.'

Given a situation in which [kwach] is the Topic element, we would have the predication:

1. Kwach nyithindo nego

in which kwach precedes the alpha term nyitbindo, which has to stand in preverbal position so long as it occurs in a given predication. This gives us the general pattern:

1. Topic Alpha Verb X

(where X represents any constituent whose positloning is not accounted for by the procedures under discussion in this section).

In general, given any underlying predication, we can assign the pragmatic function of Topic to one of its terms, nuclear or satellite.
