1.2.1.3 Quantification in Noun Phrases

Quantification

Like other languages, Dholuo has quantifiers in the form of cardinal numbers, ordinals, or adjectives of relative quantity. In a noun phrase, the head comes before the quantifier as in the following examples.

Adwaro diek ariyo

I want two goats.

Koth chwe e dwe mar abich

It rains in the fifth month (May).

Ruoth nigi rombe mathoth

The chief has many sheep.

Quantification by a cardinal is straightforward once we have a number such as [ariyo 'two'] as shown above. So is quantification by adjectives of relative quantity, e.g. [mathoth 'many'] as shown above, whose morphological constitution and other relevant properties will be discussed together with the other relative constructions (clauses).

Dholuo ordinals, however, require further explanation. In general, the number of ordinals a language has corresponds one-to-one to the number of its cardinals. Nevertheless, languages differ in how they form their ordinals morphologically.

In Dholuo, the ordinal corresponding to ['first'] in English is derived from the verb [kwongo] to come first' by prefixing the relativizing morpheme [ma] to a stem consisting of [kwongo] and the perfective marker [o.] Thus [ma + o + kwongo] gives us the word [mokwongo]['first]' after a vowel deletion or assimilation process.

The word for ['last'] is similarly derived from the verb [gik] 'to be last, to end'. Thus [ma + o + gik] gives us the word [mogik 'last']. All the other ordinals are phrasal words consisting of the word [mar] 'of' and a cardinal,

e.g. [mar ariyo] (of two) 'second', [mar ochiko] (of nine) 'ninth' [mar mia achie] l (of a hundred) 'one hundredth', etc.
