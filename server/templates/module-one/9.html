1.5 Adjectives

1.5.1 Introduction

In Dholuo grammar, adjectives are words that describe or modify nouns by providing additional information about their qualities, characteristics, or states. Adjectives help to specify the size, color, shape, quantity, or condition of a noun. They enrich communication by adding detail and precision to sentences.

1.5.2 Adjectives and Adjective Clauses

The distinction between verbs and adjectives is not clearly defined in linguistics. Outside language-specific considerations of morphological possibilities for word-classification and differentiation, there is only the semantic idea that verbs generally predicate events whereas adjectives generally predicate. But even here there are problems of overlap, for while adjectives have the function, by definition, of predicating states, a number of verbs or words which otherwise behave like verbs are often capable of predicating for example, in the sentences

Dienda olal goat-mine
'My goat is lost.'

Dienda echo lal.
There is my goat getting lost.'

The word [Ial] may be regarded either as a verb or an adjective depending on whether we understand it (in a given context) to mean 'to get lost' or 'to be lose. It does not help to regard the second interpretation as that of a stative verb because, by so doing, we exclude the possibility of having a sentence such as (b) which expresses a non-stative situation. Of course, this is a case of something that belongs to the grey area between two grammatical categories and is likely to remain controversial for a long time

Accordingly, we shall regard as adjectives those words which predicate only states. Unmodified, Dholuo adjectives can have only a predicative function, e.g.

Dhiang' ber
'A cow is good'

Punda rach
'A donkey is bad

Kwach ger
'A leopard is fierce'

Dala bor
'Home is far away'

The predicative idea is only semantic (and not syntactic) in Dholuo because adjectives, whether predicative or attributive, occur postpositionally. The attributive function is performed by adjectives in the form of minimal or one-word relative clauses in which, as usual, the clitic ma functions as a relativizer, e.g.

Dhiang' ma - ber
'a cow which (is) good' (a good cow)

Punda ma-rach
'a donkey which (is) bad' (a bad donkey)

Kwach ma-ger
'a leopard which (is) fierce' (a fierce leopard)

Piny ma-bor
'a country which (is) far (away)' (a far (away) country)

Although elementary grammars refer to forms like [maber] as adjectives, I find it more accurate to regard them as relative clauses, albeit (structurally) minimal ones. By so doting we capture the generalization contained in the observation that the clitic [ma] is used in all relative clauses in the language. The following sentences exemplify this idea:

Yien ma wiye owang'
'a tree whose top is burnt'

Jaduong' ma ohero pesa
'an elder who likes money'

Phonologically, [ma + ohero] is often realized as] mohero]

Typologically, this is not an unusual phenomenon, given that even in cases like that of English in which no relativizer appears in attributive constructions (e.g. 'a good man'), such phrases are derived from underlying relative clauses (in this case, 'a man who is good').

A characteristic feature of predication in Dholuo is that it is direct, making use of no copula. For example, in a sentence such as

Opiyo riek
'Opiyo is clever.'

only word-order establishes the relationship between the two words used. Where a nominal and an adjective are involved, the reverse word-order to that of (a) would produce a non-sentence. Thus

* riek Opiyo

is syntactically ill-formed and semantically anomalous. The ability of nouns, especially common ones, to function both as and as predicates allows us to have word-order-based minimal pairs such as

Japuonj jajuok
'The teacher is a wizard.'

Jajuok japuonj
'The wizard is a teacher'

A slight complication arises from the fact that personal pronouns seem to be capable of functioning as a copula, giving us sentences like

Japuonj en jajuok
The teacher is a wizard.'

Dhok gin mwandu
'Cattle are wealth.'

Kuo en richo
'Theft is sin.'

Nyithindo gin gweth
'Children are a blessing.'
