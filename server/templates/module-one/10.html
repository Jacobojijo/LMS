1.6 Review & Practice

1.6.1 Summary of Pronouns & Adjectives

Personal Pronouns

Dholuo personal pronouns exist in two forms: emphatic (free morphemes) and non-emphatic (bound morphemes, either prefixes or suffixes). The non-emphatic forms distinguish between subject prefixes and object suffixes, with the exception of the third-person singular, which has two allomorphs (-e, -go).

Person Emphatic Non-Emphatic (Subject Prefix) Non-Emphatic (Object Suffix)
1st sg an a- -a
1st pl wan wa- -wa
2nd sg in i- -i
2nd pl un u- -u
3rd sg en o- -e, -go
3rd pl gin gi- -gi

In sentences, emphatic forms stand alone, while non-emphatic forms attach to verbs. Example:

Emphatic: An japuonj → "I am a teacher."

Non-emphatic: A-biro neni → "I am coming to see you."

Possessive Pronouns

Possession in Dholuo is marked by mar (singular) and mag/mek (plural), suffixed with a non-emphatic pronoun.

Singular Plural
mar-a ("mine") mag-a/mek-a ("ours")
mar-i ("yours") mag-i/mek-i ("yours (pl.)")
mar-e ("his/hers/its") mag-e/mek-e ("theirs")

Possession is expressed in three ways:

  1. Nominal possession (bug japuonj → "the teacher's book")
  2. Pronominal possession (bug-e → "his book")
  3. Possessive pronouns (mar-e → "his/hers")

Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstratives in Dholuo function as adjectives (modifying a noun) or pronouns (replacing a noun). Their primary role is to indicate the relative position of an entity concerning the speaker and listener.

Demonstrative Adjectives

Singular Plural Proximity
ni gi Near the speaker (e.g., Wuoyi ni dwari → "This boy wants you.")
no Go Closer to the listener (e.g., Nyako no osiepna → "That girl is my friend.")
cha Ka Distant from both (e.g., Dhako cha binyore → "That woman is getting hurt.")

Demonstrative Pronouns

Constructed using the bound morpheme ma- + a demonstrative adjective:

Singular Plural
ma-e ("this") ma-gi ("these")
ma-no ("that") ma-go ("those")
ma-cha ("that") ma-ka ("those")

Example: Mae wach mokela iri → "This is the matter that brought me to you."

Interrogative Pronouns

Dholuo has three interrogative pronouns:

Singular Plural Meaning
ng'a/ng'awa/ng'ano -gini "Who"
ang'o/ang'owa/ang'ono -gini "What"
Mane mage "Which"

Example usage:

Ng'a ma dwaro rech? → "Who wants fish?"

Ukelo ang'o? → "What have you brought?"

Relative Pronouns

A relative pronoun replaces a nominal term in a subordinate clause while referring to an antecedent in a higher clause. Dholuo has only one relative pronoun: [ma], which introduces relative clauses.

Example:

Mano dhiang' ma onge tunge → "That is a cow which has no horns."

Morphophonemic Variations:

  • ma remains independent unless followed by a vowel, where it merges as a prefix (e.g., Ng'at moneno jakuo → "The person who has seen the thief").
  • Certain constructions resist this fusion (e.g., ma onge → "which is absent" instead of monge).

Connectives

Dholuo employs coordinators and subordinators for syntactic linkage.

Coordinators

Connective Meaning Example
gi "and" Otieno gi wuon gero ot → "Otieno and his father are building a house."
to "but" Akelo ndiko to nyamin somo → "Akelo is writing, but her sister is reading."
kendo "and (same subject)" John dhi kendo duogo pile ka pile → "John goes and comes back every day."

Distinctions:

  • gi joins nouns/phrases but not clauses with the same subject.
  • kendo is used instead of gi when clauses share a subject.
  • to conjoins simultaneous actions (e.g., Opiyo chiemo to nindo → "Opiyo eats while sleeping").

Subordinators

Subordinators introduce dependent clauses expressing relationships such as:

Function Subordinator Example
Reason nikech/emomiyo Ok adhi sikul nikech wiya bara → "I am not going to school because I have a headache."
Concession kata Kata ahere to ok anyal kende → "Much as I love her, I cannot marry her."
Condition ka/kik Ka koth ochwe to kik ibi → "If it rains, do not come."
Time ka Ne gidonjo ka Ching' podho → "They arrived at sunset."
Comparison kaka Ode rieny kaka sulwe → "His house shines like a star."
Capacity kaka Obiro kaka jatendwa → "He is coming as our leader."

Adjectives and Adjective Clauses

The boundary between verbs and adjectives in linguistics is often blurred. While verbs typically denote events and adjectives describe states, Dholuo presents cases where words function as both. For instance, lal can mean to get lost (verb) or to be lost (adjective), showing the fluidity between categories.

In Dholuo, adjectives primarily function predicatively, as in:

Dhiang' ber ('A cow is good')

Punda rach ('A donkey is bad')

Unlike English, adjectives in attributive positions occur as minimal relative clauses, using the clitic ma as a relativizer:

Dhiang' ma-ber ('A cow which is good' → 'A good cow')

Piny ma-bor ('A country which is far' → 'A distant country')

This structure aligns with a broader typological pattern where adjectives derive from relative clauses. Additionally, Dholuo lacks a copula, with predication achieved through direct juxtaposition, e.g.,

Opiyo riek ('Opiyo is clever').

Reversing word order (riek Opiyo) results in syntactic ill-formedness, emphasizing the importance of word placement. However, personal pronouns (en, gin) occasionally function as copulas in identity statements:

Japuonj en jajuok ('The teacher is a wizard')
