1.1 Introduction to Abagusii and Ekegusii

1.1.1 Introduction

Identity and Geographic Setting

Ethnonym and Self-Designation:

  • The term "Abagusii" refers to the people themselves.
  • An individual member of this community is referred to as an "Omogusii."

Colonial and Popular Terminology:

  • The word "Kisii" originates from Swahili.
  • It was popularized and institutionalized by the British colonial administration.
  • Despite its foreign origin, "Kisii" remains widely used in contemporary Kenyan discourse.

Clan-Based Social Structure:

Abagusii society is composed of seven clan clusters:

  • Kitutu
  • North Mogirango
  • South Mogirango
  • Bomachoge
  • Bonchari
  • Bobasi
  • Nyaribari

Location and Ecology:

  • The Gusii inhabit a region in Western Kenya, approximately 50 kilometers east of Lake Victoria.
  • The area is renowned for:
    • Abundant rainfall
    • Rich volcanic soils
    • High agricultural productivity, making Gusiiland one of Kenya's most fertile zones.

Administrative Division:

Gusiiland is subdivided into two counties:

  • Kisii County
  • Nyamira County

Historical Migration of the Abagusii

Linguistic Affiliation:

  • The Abagusii speak Ekegusii, a Bantu language.
  • It is indigenous to the Kisii and Nyamira regions.

Demographic Data:

As per the 2019 Kenya National Census, Ekegusii boasts an estimated 2.7 million native speakers.

Migration Pathways:

Abagusii are historically believed to have:

  • Migrated from the Congo forest region.
  • Passed through Uganda.
  • Entered Kenya through its western corridor.

Oral Tradition and Mythical Origin:

Gusii folklore identifies their origin as 'Misiri'—an indigenous rendering of Egypt, highlighting symbolic links to antiquity and migration legends.

Key Migration Waves and Dialectal Differentiation

Dialect Classification:

Ekegusii has two primary dialects:

  • Rogoro Dialect (Upper-side)
  • Maate Dialect (Lower-side)

Geographic Distribution:

  • Rogoro is spoken predominantly in the northern zones of Gusiiland.
  • Maate is prevalent in the southern territories of Gusiiland.

Sociolinguistic Prestige:

The Rogoro dialect is considered the standard form, as it is:

  • Used in published materials.
  • Featured in Ekegusii news broadcasts.

Phonological Distinctions:

A notable difference lies in the articulation of the consonant /t/ between the two dialects.

Lexical Variations and Examples:

Although mutually intelligible, the dialects use different terms for the same referents:

Cat:
Rogoro: Ekemoni
Maate: Ekebusi
Sandals/Slippers:
Rogoro: Chisiripasi (loanword from "slippers")
Maate: Chitaratara

Settlements and Distribution Patterns

Pre-Colonial Social Organization:

The traditional extended polygamous Gusii family was organized into two spatial divisions:

Omochie (Homestead):

Residence of:

  • Married men and women
  • Unmarried daughters
  • Uncircumcised sons

Ebisarate (Cattle Camps):

  • Situated in grazing zones
  • Guarded by male warriors
  • Served to protect cattle, a critical economic asset

Colonial Disruption:

In 1913, the British colonial administration abolished cattle camps, altering the socio-spatial fabric.

19th Century Settlement Features:

  • Most Gusii households practiced dispersed settlement across farmsteads.
  • Exception: North Mogirango built fortified villages as a defensive measure against Kipsigis raids.

Homestead Structure and Architecture:

Traditional Gusii Homestead included:

  • Separate wives' houses.
  • Elevated granaries for storing finger millet.
  • Central courtyard for communal interaction.

Traditional Gusii House (Enyomba):

  • Circular, windowless design.
  • Constructed with dried mud walls.
  • Topped with a conical, thatched roof.

Modern Housing Evolution (1990s Onward):

  • Continued pattern of dispersed homesteads surrounded by farm holdings.
  • Transition to rectangular houses, typically with:
    • Thatch or corrugated-iron roofing.
    • Detached kitchen structures—a significant shift from indoor cooking traditions.

1.1.5 History & Culture of the Abagusii

A. Economic Subsistence Patterns

Pre-Colonial Agricultural Staples:

  • The primary staple crop was finger millet, central to the Gusii diet and rituals.
  • Finger millet was often intercropped with:
    • Sorghum
    • Beans
    • Sweet potatoes

Complementary Food Sources:

  • Livestock products such as meat and milk served as essential protein sources.
  • Wild vegetables were frequently foraged to enrich the diet and provide medicinal benefits.

Shift in Staple Crops Post-1920:

By the 1920s, maize had:

  • Replaced finger millet as the dominant staple food.
  • Evolved into a key cash crop, marking a significant agricultural transition.

Other Contemporary Cultivated Crops:

  • Cassava
  • Pigeon peas
  • Green grams
  • Onions
  • Bananas
  • Potatoes
  • Tomatoes

These crops reflect diversification in agronomy aligned with ecological adaptation and market needs.

B. Commercial Activities

Early Commercialization of Cash Crops:

  • Coffee cultivation began as early as 1913, albeit on a limited scale.
  • Over time, Gusiiland developed a reputation for producing:
    • High-quality coffee
    • Premium-grade tea

Tools and Implements:

  • Iron hoes and ox-drawn plows remain integral in contemporary cultivation techniques.
  • These reflect a continuity of agro-industrial heritage and low-mechanization reliance.

Livestock Husbandry:

Though livestock numbers have decreased over time, Gusii farmers continue to raise:

  • Cattle
  • Goats
  • Sheep
  • Chicken

C. Industrial Arts and Craftsmanship

Pre-Colonial Industrial Output:

Locally produced items included:

  • Iron tools and weapons
  • Wooden utensils and implements
  • Decorative items
  • Poison containers
  • Miniature baskets used in serving porridge or storing household items

Pottery and Basketry Trade:

While pottery and some basketries were not locally manufactured, they were obtained through barter trade with neighboring Luoland.

D. Pre-Colonial Trade Systems

Localized Economic Exchange:

Trade was centered within homesteads, rooted in kinship-based economies.

Trade Items and Media of Exchange:

Goods exchanged included:

  • Agricultural produce
  • Iron tools
  • Weapons
  • Crafts

Livestock (particularly goats and cows) served as transactional currency, especially in bride price and land deals.

E. Division of Labor and Gender Roles (Late 1900s)

Women's Responsibilities:

  • Food cultivation and processing
  • Cooking and brewing
  • Fetching water and firewood
  • Maintaining household cleanliness

Men's Responsibilities:

  • Warfare and defense activities
  • Construction of homes and livestock fences
  • Clearing new fields
  • Animal herding and protection of livestock assets

Key Factors in the Evolution of the Gusii Language

1. Linguistic Influences and Borrowing

Multilingual Interactions:

Prolonged contact with Swahili and English—especially during and after colonial administration—has led to:

  • Widespread loanwords in areas like technology, governance, religion, and education.
  • A blended lexicon reflecting linguistic hybridity.

Examples of Borrowing Contexts:

  • Swahili terms for food, social greetings, and common nouns.
  • English vocabulary in education, tools, and modern occupations.

2. Phonological Changes Over Time

Sound System Shifts:

Historical developments have seen pronunciation and articulation patterns evolve, including:

  • Simplification of consonant clusters.
  • Adaptation of foreign phonemes into Gusii's phonotactic structure.

Influencing Factors:

  • Language contact
  • Urbanization
  • Intergenerational transmission shifts

3. Lexical Changes and Vocabulary Expansion

Semantic Evolution:

The Ekegusii lexicon has experienced:

  • Coinage of neologisms to capture modern realities.
  • Borrowing of technical and academic vocabulary from global languages.
  • Cultural adaptations for previously foreign concepts.

Reflections of Societal Change:

Vocabulary shifts mirror transitions in:

  • Technology (e.g., electricity, internet)
  • Agricultural mechanization
  • Religious and political terminology

4. Grammatical Developments and Syntactic Shifts

Morphosyntactic Evolution:

Changes include:

  • Word order rearrangements
  • Verb conjugation transformations
  • Introduction of auxiliary constructions

Drivers of Grammatical Change:

  • Language internal dynamics (such as regularization of irregular forms)
  • External influence from bilingualism and code-switching
  • Shift from oral to written form creating standardization pressure
