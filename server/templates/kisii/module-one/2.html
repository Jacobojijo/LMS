1.1.2.1 Review & Practice

Identity and Geographic Setting

Ethnic Identity:

  • Abagusii is the autonym (self-designation).
  • A single member is called Omogusii.

Exonym Usage:

  • Kisii is a Swahili-derived term institutionalized by British colonial authorities.
  • Still widely used in Kenya's national discourse.

Clan Composition:

  • Society is organized into seven clan clusters:
  • Kitutu, North Mogirango, South Mogirango, Bomachoge, Bonchari, Bobasi, Nyaribari.

Geographic Location:

  • Gusiiland lies in Western Kenya, ~50 km east of Lake Victoria.
  • Known for:
    • Abundant rainfall
    • Rich volcanic soils
    • High agricultural productivity

Administrative Subdivision:

  • Gusiiland is distributed across:
  • Kisii County
  • Nyamira County

Historical Migration of the Abagusii

Language and People:

  • Ekegusii is a Bantu language spoken by the Abagusii.
  • Indigenous to Kisii and Nyamira.

Population:

  • 2.7 million native speakers (2019 National Census).

Migration Narrative:

  • Believed to have:
  • Originated in the Congo Forest.
  • Migrated through Uganda into Kenya's western regions.

Cultural Mythology:

  • Oral tradition claims origin from Misiri (Egypt), symbolizing ancestral antiquity.

Dialectal Variation and Migration Waves

Two Main Dialects:

  • Rogoro Dialect (Upper-side)
  • Maate Dialect (Lower-side)

Geographical Spread:

  • Rogoro: Northern Gusii
  • Maate: Southern Gusii

Sociolinguistic Status:

  • Rogoro considered the standard dialect:
  • Used in print
  • Employed in media broadcasts

Phonological Contrast:

  • Distinct articulation of /t/ between dialects.

Lexical Divergence:

  • Example:
  • Cat: Ekemoni (Rogoro), Ekebusi (Maate)
  • Sandals: Chisiripasi (Rogoro), Chitaratara (Maate)

Settlements and Distribution Patterns

Traditional Spatial Organization:

  • Omochie (Homestead): residence for married members, daughters, and uncircumcised sons.
  • Ebisarate (Cattle camps): male-guarded zones for livestock protection.

Colonial Transformation:

  • British abolished cattle camps in 1913.

19th Century Settlements:

  • Predominantly dispersed farmsteads.
  • North Mogirango practiced fortified settlements for security.

Homestead Architecture:

  • Round, windowless houses (Enyomba) with mud walls and thatched roofs.
  • Elevated granaries for finger millet storage.

Modern Housing Trends (1990s):

  • Continuation of dispersed homesteads.
  • Shift to rectangular houses with:
    • Corrugated iron or thatched roofs
    • Separate kitchen buildings

History and Culture of the Abagusii

A. Economic Subsistence

Pre-1920 Staples:

  • Finger millet (primary staple)
  • Intercropped with sorghum, beans, and sweet potatoes

Additional Food Sources:

  • Livestock for meat and milk
  • Wild vegetables for dietary and medicinal use

Post-1920 Transition:

  • Maize overtook finger millet as:
  • Staple food
  • Primary cash crop

Other Key Crops:

  • Cassava, pigeon peas, green grams, onions, bananas, potatoes, tomatoes

B. Commercial Agriculture

Cash Crop Evolution:

  • Coffee cultivation began around 1913
  • Gusiiland recognized for:
  • High-grade coffee
  • Premium tea

Agricultural Tools:

  • Iron hoes and ox-drawn plows still in use

Animal Husbandry:

  • Farmers continue to rear:
  • Cattle, goats, sheep, chickens

C. Industrial Arts

Pre-Colonial Craftsmanship:

  • Locally made tools and items:
  • Iron weapons, wooden utensils, baskets, poison containers

External Trade:

  • Pottery and basketry traded from neighboring Luoland

D. Trade Systems

Household-Based Economy:

  • Economic exchange occurred within homesteads.

Trade Goods:

  • Included iron tools, weapons, agricultural produce
  • Livestock (especially cows and goats) used as currency

E. Gendered Division of Labor

Women's Roles:

  • Food production, cooking, brewing, cleaning, water/firewood collection

Men's Roles:

  • Defense, house building, field clearing, livestock herding

Key Factors in the Evolution of the Gusii Language

1. Linguistic Influences

Language Contact:

  • Borrowing from Swahili and English
  • Domains of borrowing:
  • Technology, governance, religion, education

Lexical Blending:

  • Swahili terms for daily life
  • English vocabulary for technical and institutional terms

2. Phonological Changes

Sound Shifts:

  • Evolution in pronunciation patterns
  • Simplification of clusters and adaptation of foreign phonemes

Change Agents:

  • Urbanization
  • Intergenerational language shifts

3. Lexical Adaptation

Semantic Growth:

  • Introduction of new vocabulary for modern contexts
  • Borrowing and neologism

Drivers:

  • Societal modernization
  • Technological advancement
  • Religious and political discourse

4. Grammatical Development

Structural Evolution:

  • Shifts in word order, verb conjugation, auxiliary usage

Change Influences:

  • Bilingualism, code-switching, internal linguistic restructuring

Emergence of written Ekegusii promoting standardization
