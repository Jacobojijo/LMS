1.4.5

Telling Time

The Ekegusii language features a rich inventory of time-related expressions that reflect both linear and cyclical perceptions of time. This system incorporates general time markers, daily time segments, and precise syntactic constructions for expressing time in various tenses and social contexts.

1. General Temporal Expressions in Ekegusii

These adverbial markers express relative or chronological time and serve to situate actions or states in the past, present, or future.

Ekegusii Term English Translation Functional Usage
Bono Now Present tense anchor
Bono bono Right now Immediate present, urgency
Kare Later Deferred or postponed action
Igoro Yesterday Past event reference
Moisonde The day before yesterday Extended past reference
Rero Today Current-day reference
Mambia / Ankio Tomorrow Anticipated future action
Mambia yende The day after tomorrow Near future, advanced planning

Illustrative Examples:

Imbwate emeremo emenge bono.
"I have a lot of work now."
→ Uses present moment emphasis (bono).
Nigo atagete korora ise igoro.
"He wanted to see their father yesterday."
→ Anchors the sentence in recent past (igoro).
Kare, ntoché tokonye emeremo.
"Later, we will help with the work."
→ Deferred action expression (kare).
Mambia yende toché togende inka.
"The day after tomorrow, we will go home."
→ Near-future planning (mambia yende)

2. Time Segments of the Day in Ekegusii

The Abagusii people categorize the 24-hour day into four primary phases, each with its unique cultural and linguistic marker.

Ekegusii Term Translation Meaning / Usage
Maambi Morning Dawn to midday
Mogaso Afternoon Midday to early evening
Marogoba Evening Dusk; transitional light
Botuko Night Nighttime (rest/darkness)

Additional Temporal Descriptors:

Ekegusii Phrase Translation Description
Maambi mambi Very early in the morning Pre-dawn hours; anticipation
Botuko gati Midnight Middle of the night
Saa tisa kamili Exactly 3 PM Precision time reference

Illustrative Examples:

Maambi abanto nigi bagokwanere oborabu.
"In the morning, people talk about light."
→ Highlights symbolic use of light and hope.
Nigo yaigete botuko gati ekero omonto ararete.
"It reached midnight when someone was sleeping."
→ Night as a time of rest (botuko gati).
Chinsa kianda chia'marogoba echire ekemanyererio egekong'u kio omwaka.
"Exactly 3 PM marks the hardest part of the year."
→ Precise time symbolizing difficulty or climax.

3. Sentence Structure in Ekegusii Time Expressions

Ekegusii integrates time expressions flexibly within syntactic structures. Temporal markers can precede or follow the verb phrase depending on focus or emphasis.

Structural Variants:

Structure Example Explanation
Time Marker + Subject + Verb + Object Igoro nigo aroche ise.
“Yesterday, he saw his father.”		 Time emphasis precedes action
Subject + Verb + Time Marker Nkogeka'inde kare.
“I will go later.”		 Action placed first; time deferred
Temporal Adverb + Clause Botuko gati, nigo naende aiguete amariogo.
“At midnight, she still heard voices.”		 Expressive temporal framing

4. Cultural and Linguistic Notes

  • Ekegusii temporal terms do not merely denote clock time, but also evoke mood, rhythm, and context.
  • Time expressions like "maambi mambi" and "mambia yende" reflect deep awareness of natural cycles and preparation for events.
  • The placement of time markers in sentences contributes to pragmatic emphasis and rhetorical effect.
  • Use of compound numerals (e.g., saa tisa kamili) illustrates modern integration with standardized clock systems.
