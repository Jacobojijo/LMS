1.5 Review & Practice
1.5.1 Summary of Numbers & Numbering
I. Numerical System Overview

Ekegusii employs a decimal-based numeral system.

Numerical expressions are additive, precise, and culturally embedded.

Numbers serve critical functions in trade, timekeeping, genealogy, rituals, and social organization.

II. Cardinal Numbers
Category Formation Rule Examples
Basic Numbers Single root terms from 1 to 10 eyemo (1), ibere (2), ..., ikomi (10)
Compound Numbers Ikomi (10) + unit digit using "na" (and) or elided "ne" Ikomi neyemo (11), ikomi na ibere (12)
Multiples of Ten Emerongo + base numeral Emerongo ebere (20), emerongo kianda (90)
Hundreds Emia/Chimia + numeral Chimia ibere (200), chimia kianda (900)
Thousands Eribu/Chiribu + numeral Chiribu isato (3000), chiribu kianda (9000)
III. Applications in Contextual Usage

Trade & Economy

"Abagaka nigo basimegete emete emerongo etano n'ebere"

→ (Elders planted seventy-two trees)

Social Counting

"Omokungu oyomon'abasacha babere"

→ (One woman and two men)

Quantification Examples:

Item Quantity in Ekegusii

Chinka (homes) Ikomi n'aisano (15)
Abana (children) Ikomi n'oyomo (11)

IV. Ordinal Numbers

Ordinal expressions denote rank, sequence, and order.

Formed using the prefix "oyo" added to the cardinal root.

Exceptions:

Motang'ani – First

Moerio – Last

Ordinal Rank Ekegusii Form
First Motang'ani
Second Oyokabere
Third Oyogatato
Fourth Oyokane
Fifth Oyogatano
... (Progressive pattern)
V. Sociolinguistic & Cultural Relevance

Days of the Week
I. Weekly Temporal Framework: Days of the Week

The Abagusii recognize a seven-day week, integrating labor cycles, economic activity, and spiritual obligations into daily nomenclature.

Day Ekegusii Name Literal Meaning / Function Cultural Significance
Monday Tichi rimo First workday Marks resumption of labor (resets work rhythm)
Tuesday Tichi kabere Second workday Reinforces ongoing labor continuity
Wednesday Tichi gatato Third workday Midweek reference, often agricultural
Thursday Tichi kane Fourth workday Preparatory phase for market activity
Friday Tichi gatano Fifth workday Transition to economic exchange day
Saturday Enyogesa Market day Commerce and trading dominate social activity
Sunday Omokubio Day of rest Reserved for worship and spiritual reflection
II. Annual Temporal Framework: Months of the Year

The Abagusii calendar aligns structurally with the Gregorian twelve-month format, naming each month through an ordinal pattern prefixed by "Omotienyi" (month).

Month Ekegusii Name Literal Meaning
January Omotienyi omotang'ani First month
February Omotienyi o'kabere Second month
March Omotienyi ogatato Third month
April Omotienyi o'kane Fourth month
May Omotienyi o'gatano Fifth month
June Omotienyi o'gatano na rimo Sixth month
July Omotienyi o'gatano na kabere Seventh month
August Omotienyi o'gatano na gatato Eighth month
September Omotienyi o'kianda Ninth month
October Omotienyi o'ikomi Tenth month
November Omotienyi o'ikomi na rimo Eleventh month
December Omotienyi o'ikomi na kabere Twelfth month
III. Temporal Lexicon: Time Expressions in Ekegusii
A. General Temporal Markers
Time Concept Ekegusii Term Meaning
Present moment bono now
Immediate present bono bono right now,
Recent past igoro yesterday
Current day rero today
Imminent future ankio tomorrow
Distant future mambia yende day after tomorrow
B. Division of the Day

Ekegusii classifies the day into four structured segments, enriched by specific sub-phases:

Time Segment Ekegusii Term Description
Morning maambi Beginning of the day
Early morning mambi maambi Dawn; pre-sunrise
Afternoon mogaso Post-noon daylight hours
Evening marogoba Dusk; transition to night
Night botuko General night period
Midnight botuko gati Deep night / midpoint of the night
C. Syntax and Usage in Sentences

Time expressions are embedded in structured syntactic patterns, aligning verbs and time markers with clear semantic flow.

Past Example: Igoro naroche ise

→ "Yesterday, he saw his father."

Future Example: Nkogendande kare

→ "I will go later."

These structures reflect the temporal consciousness of the Abagusii, combining sequential logic with cultural coherence.
