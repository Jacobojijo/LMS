1.6.3

Family Members

The Abagusii kinship system is one of complex relational depth, extending beyond the nuclear family to recognize and institutionalize roles for extended, generational, and affinal (in-law) relations. This structure reflects an intergenerational worldview, where lineage, seniority, and communal responsibility define social identity and moral belonging.

1. The Structure of Family in Abagusii Society

  • Kinship is inclusive and hierarchical, comprising both nuclear and extended family members.
  • Emphasis is placed on generational continuity, ancestral reverence, and collective responsibility.
  • Unlike Western family systems, Abagusii kinship integrates distant relatives into the core of social life.
  • Terms are often gender-neutral or used interchangeably across both paternal and maternal lineages.

3. Gender and Kinship: Cultural and Linguistic Notes

Kinship terms in Ekegusii are largely gender-neutral, which simplifies communication and reflects equal relational value.

One term may serve both genders, relying on context or modifiers for specification.

Affinal Relations (In-Laws):

Relation Ekegusii Term Explanation
Daughter-in-law Moka momura Used when she returns to her paternal home
Son-in-law (my) Omwana one "My child" — relationally affectionate and inclusive
Son-in-law (your) Omwana ogo "Your child" — relational reference

4. Generational Depth and Lineage Terminology

Abagusii kinship recognizes generational succession with unique terms for great-grandchildren, affirming the importance of ancestral memory and legacy.

Table 2: Multigenerational Descendants

Generation Ekegusii Term Meaning
First great-grandchild Omochokoro omotang'ani "First great-grandchild"
Second great-grandchild Omochokoro o'kabere "Second great-grandchild"
Third great-grandchild Omochokoro o'gatato "Third great-grandchild"
These terms highlight lineage tracking, social memory, and the ritual significance of birth order in ancestry.

5. Polygamy and Kinship Differentiation

In traditional Abagusii society, polygamy was not only accepted but institutionally structured, influencing terminology used for wives and household roles.

Table 3: Classification of Wives in Polygamous Households

Wife Rank Ekegusii Term Cultural Role and Implication
First Wife Omokungu omotang'ani Highest status; authority over junior wives and family management
Second Wife Omokungu o'kabere Secondary status, still influential; subordinate to the first wife
Third Wife Omokungu o'gatato Youngest or last, often newer to the household; receives less privilege
Each wife has distinct domestic roles, inheritance rights, and child-rearing responsibilities.

6. Cultural Significance of Kinship in Abagusii Society

  • Kinship terms double as social roles—they dictate how one interacts, respects, and obligates themselves to others.
  • The extended family is not peripheral; it is core to social security, identity, and collective memory.
  • Elders (e.g., sokoro and magokoro) serve as custodians of wisdom, moral educators, and ritual leaders.
  • Kinship terms encode genealogical proximity, seniority, and responsibility, anchoring personal identity to communal lineage.
