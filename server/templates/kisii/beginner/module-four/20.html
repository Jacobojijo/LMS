4.14.1 Summary of Nomenclature, Preposition & Conjunctions

Gusii Expressions and Proverbs

I. Cultural Function and Linguistic Significance

Proverbs and idioms act as intellectual, moral, and aesthetic pillars in Gusii culture.

Serve as:

  • Linguistic ornaments in daily speech.
  • Ethical instruments and social correctives.
  • Tools of education, diplomacy, and wit.
  • Encoded repositories of worldview and ancestral memory.

Ekegusii Proverbs

Mominchori 'imi tang'ana mosera 'ibu

Mominchori refers to an early riser while mosera ibu refers to a person who keeps in-doors. Rimi is morning dew. It therefore means that a person who rises up early and goes out braving the morning dew is much better economically than one who remains at home, feels lazy to venture out there to fend for himself. Lesson: We should all strive to excel and avoid laziness.

'Keumbu kiaare nkegundi 'moba

Ekeumbu is the ant hill in which mushrooms flourish. In essence, ekeumbu which is distant tends to be forgotten hence the mushrooms end up rotting. It therefore implies that it is easier to tend to something within our reach more effectively than one that is miles away.

Nyama 'nke yaria bokima 'kee

Ekee is that traditional bowl woven out of chimboba chia obori. Until recently, this was the sole plate and bowl used by our fore-fathers in ugali and other stuff. It means literally that small piece of meat can consume large quantity of ugali. The real hidden meaning is that something you might refer as a small issue can cause untold damage.

'Mwana obande, 'mamiria makendu

It means that one first and foremost attends to issues affecting him as a person before he can think of neighbour.

Ebiabande nsongora igoti, ebiao nkunyunyu

When a situation arises that you are eating/feasting at a friend's place, you are very ready and willing to attend. But when it is your turn to give, you are unwilling. We should be generous and give with happiness just as we are willing to receive.

Abange mbaya, nekero bariete kiane nkaigwa bobe

When you have some problem, you feel good when many people come and assist in the spirit of unity. The problem is shared and happiness prevails. But, when the many people come to eat from you, you tend to be offended because you will spend a lot.

Enda n'esese

Esese is a dog and enda is stomach. Refers to a situation where you eat and forget that there is no tomorrow. Eat and keep some for a rainy day. However much you eat, you will certainly feel hungry.

Bwanchani mbwa 'maiso, n'emioyo etamanyaini

Obwanchani is love. People tend to have their inner feelings to themselves even if they appear friendly and helpful. Beware of two-faced friends who pretend to be your friends while they hate you inside.

Mosomba n'onguru

A good servant is one who works hard for he will reap some benefits out of his hard work and patience. Encourages those who are employed to work hard in order for them to survive lest they get fired at the slightest mistake.

Egiasireire nchera rogoro kerigerie nchera maate

When you have lost something, it is prudent for you to expand your search, not necessarily at the spot where you misplaced it. For instance, when you have lost a woman to another man, look for another at another place.

Geutere giote gekobamboka gekobambokere

Being nice to a person has a price. When he gets up and is able to support himself, more often than not turns against you. Beware.

Omosacha neritiro rire nyomba

In a household, the husband is like a pillar of that house. His importance cannot be disputed and therefore he should be given his due respect. Without him things can not run smoothly. Wives are warned to accord their husbands their due respect and integrity.

Borabu nswenta

When you leave a place and promise to come back in no time, beware because you never know what you will incur just after getting out of sight. In most cases, you end up taking more time to return than you promised.

Amasikani nabuete chiseneni chieching'ondi

It is gratifying to maintain respect towards other people and to yourself. A sheep's tail has some very delicious meat yet it cannot equal respect.

Marina marina maya, maika matamerana

It is easier to climb up a tree than to descend. Likewise, it is easier to create a problem than it is to find yourself out of the same.

Momenyi boko noyonga Gisore

Gisore was a polite man who was liked by all and sundry. When you are housed or accommodated by people, you have to live and behave just as well as Gisore. This will encourage your host to keep accommodating you till you leave most likey on your own accord.

Binto mbiang'ora, nsagasaga bikwanga

If you want good results, do something slowly but carefully. Speed often makes you overlook stupid mistakes.

Nyasi nere namato

Walls have ears. Avoid talking ill of people thinking that no one is hearing. Whatever you say, know that it will filter through to someone sooner or later. Same as getutu nkemaiso.

Egetakoria ngetakonyora

This is like a curse. Means one does not wish you well at all. If you evade trouble, then there was no trouble at all.

Obwate omwabo, obwatwa koboko ocha ka, otabwati omwabo obwata 'moraa mwomo

This refers to situations where people tend to get favours from friends and relatives when trouble strikes. In contrast, those who have no one to look to often get nothing and end up in problems. Omoraa omwomo (dry plant) implies hardship.

Nguru chia 'momura, nchogu egwatia 'mbara

This proverb is used to stress the importance of a male child in a homestead. He is likened to an elephant in the sense that he is strong by nature and will be able to fight and protect the family when need arises.

Getutu kere maiso

The bush in which you hide has eyes.

"A secret is compared to a bush in which a person hides, that is, your hiding place. On one level the proverb warns you to choose a good place to hide your secrets in order to make sure that your secrets are safe and will later be used for the well being of others. However, your bad secrets will be exposed. This Gusii proverb teaches the bad features of pretense, hypocrisy and hiding the truth.

"A scripture parallel is Luke 12:1b-3: "Beware of the yeast of the Pharisees, that is, their hypocrisy. Nothing is covered up that will not be uncovered, and nothing secret that will not become known. Therefore, whatever you have said in the dark will be heard in the light, and what you have whispered behind closed doors will be proclaimed from the housetops.

"This proverb is used mainly during ceremonies of initiation and during the appointment of people to leadership positions. The proverb encourages people not to pretend in carrying out their responsibilities, but rather to serve faithfully and openly. What they do in pretense or "in the dark" will always haunt them later."

Ekegusii Idioms and Meanings

Gotwera amate:

Literally "to spit saliva on," this idiom means "to bless someone or something".

Goaka ekeranya:

Literally "to cane someone," this idiom means "to be nominated for a specific task" or "to tell someone something vexing, be it true or false".

Koragera buna omosamaro:

Literally "to eat like someone from the Bosamaro clan," this idiom is used to describe "to eat a lot".

Omonto onga omogoye:

Literally "a person, like a string that has been stripped from a reed," this idiom describes "a thin person".

Enda n'esese:

Literally "stomach and a dog," this phrase is a warning to "eat and save for a rainy day," as one will always feel hungry again.

Bwanchani mbwa 'maiso, n'emioyo etamanyaini:

Literally "love is a dog's eyes, and hearts do not know," this idiom warns about "two-faced friends who pretend to be your friends while they hate you inside".

Binto mbiang'ora, nsagasaga bikwanga:

This proverb means "If you want good results, do something slowly but carefully".

Proverbs and idioms are cultural doctrines, cognitive tools, and oral archives. They refine communication, reinforce ethics, and preserve ancestral voice. Mastery of these expressions signals deep cultural literacy and philosophical insight.

4.10 Gusii Nomenclature: Naming as a Cultural Text

I. Introduction: Philosophy Behind Naming

Naming is sacred, weaving identity into:

  • Time (temporal awareness)
  • Place (spatial consciousness)
  • History (cultural memory)
  • Kinship (genealogical continuity)

II. Temporal-Based Naming: Diurnal Identity

Based on time of birth; reflects cosmological rhythm.

Sunrise Obare, Obara Nyairabu
Evening Mogoba Nyanchama
Night Nyamwamu, Motuka Nyamisa, Gesunte

Reflects societal or environmental conditions at birth.

Names signify location of birth, tying personhood to geography.

Names reflect birth conditions or physical signs.

VI. Twin-Based Naming: Duality and Order Recognition

Naming twins acknowledges cosmic dualism and birth order.

Twin Order Male Name Female Name
First Chacha, Motangi, Barongo Motangi, Barongo
Second Barongo Barongo

VII. Inheritance-Based Naming: Lineage and Legacy

Children inherit names from:

  • Deceased/living relatives, irrespective of gender norms.

Reflects:

  • Ancestral embodiment
  • Spiritual continuity
  • Family honor

A boy may bear a female name (e.g., Atieno), and vice versa.

VIII. Summary: A Complex and Symbolic Naming Tradition

The Gusii naming system is:

  • Philosophical — Reflecting time, space, spirit.
  • Sociological — Anchored in kinship and historical memory.
  • Geographical — Emphasizes locational identity.
  • Spiritual — Reveres ancestors and metaphysical lineage.
  • Contextual — A name is not a tag, but a narrative of origin.

Price Asking (rigori nirenga): Linguistic Economics in Gusii Culture

I. "Nirenga" as Core Economic Lexicon

  • "Nirenga" = Price/value; central in Gusii market and domestic discourse.
  • Utilized in formal commerce and informal bargaining alike.
  • Anchors conversations around value, exchange, and worth.

II. Asking Price: rigori nirenga Structures

Standard Format:

  • "Nirenga rigori?" – "How much does it cost?"
  • Structure emphasizes object → price sequence (noun precedes inquiry).
  • E.g., Rigori rie ritoke nirenga? – "What is the price of the banana?"

Alternative Structure:

  • Emphasis on chibesa (money) → "X chibesa irenga?"
  • Highlights currency over item.
  • E.g., Inaki okooni omosi? – "How much is the sugar-cane?"

III. Nduserie: The Art of Bargaining

  • Nduseria/ ngeerie = Negotiating price; socio-linguistic ritual.
  • Emphasizes:
    • Respectful disagreement with initial prices.
    • Polite insistence for discounts.
    • Mutual economic fairness.
  • Reflects relational intelligence, cultural etiquette, and market diplomacy.

IV. Socio-Pragmatic Importance

  • Rigori nirenga is a marker of linguistic fluency and economic tact.
  • Embeds economic action within social norms, relational speech, and communal expectations.

V. Summary: The Economics of Expression

  • Dual syntactic pathways:
    • Item-centered: "Rigori ndireri X ?"
    • Currency-centered: "chibesa irenga X ?"
  • Bargaining (nduserie rigori) is not confrontation but cultural dialogue.
  • Mastery reflects interpersonal finesse, market literacy, and social embeddedness.

I. Definition & Role

Prepositions express:

  • Spatial orientation (location, direction, position)
  • Relational dynamics (ownership, cause, companionship)
  • Instrumentality and purpose

III. Anatomical Origins of Spatial Terms

Prepositions rooted in body metaphors:

  • Ase obosio bwa ← (at the head)
  • Ase enda ya ← (at the stomach)
  • Ase okogoro kwa ← (at the foot)

Reflects a cognitively embodied spatial logic in Ekegusii.

IV. The Triadic Nuance of "For"

  • Asengencho – Denotes purpose or target
    • E.g., Ngokora emeremo asengencho yechibesa – I work for money
  • Ase – Implies cause, gratitude, or justification
    • E.g., Mbuyamono ase endagera – Thank you for the food
  • Korera – Used for institutions or organizations
    • E.g., Ngokorera UCRC – I work for UCRC

V. "With": Na vs. Amo na

Na – General use with nouns

  • Ngocha na Kerubo – I come with Achieng'
  • Cannot combine with pronoun suffixes.

Amo na – Used for people and pronouns

  • Ngocha nere – I come with him
  • Allows suffixation: nainche (with me), naintwe (with us)

VI. Possession and Classification: Role of "Ye"

  • Ye = of/possessive
  • Amabere y'omwana – the children's milk
  • Indicates ownership, association, and categorical relation.

VII. Summary: Prepositions as Cultural Grammar

Prepositions in Ekegusii:

  • Are metaphor-rich, rooted in physical embodiment.
  • Distinguish between types of companionship and causal relationships.
  • Offer syntactic flexibility for expressing spatial, social, and semantic relations.

Understanding them enables deeper command over Ekegusii relational dynamics and communicative precision.

I. Relative Pronouns in Ekegusii: "Erio" and Subordinate Syntax

Definition & Role

  • Relative pronouns link principal clauses to subordinate ones, expanding syntactic depth.
  • Central marker: "Erio" — denotes "that"/"so that" in English but is not a preposition.

Key Connector: "erio"

Moderate-tone "erio" = that/so that

Usage Functions

  • Introduces clauses of purpose, condition, indirect speech, and intentionality.
  • Often occurs as "na erio":
    • "Na" (so) + "Erio" (that) = Future intent or desired outcome

Examples:

  • Ngosaba erio inyore endagera – I pray so that I may get food.
  • Ere agokora emeremo erio abanto bache – He works so that people may come.

Structural Composition

Principal Clause + (na) erio + Dependent Clause

→ Enables intentional, hypothetical, and subjunctive constructions.

II. Causal Connectives: Expressing Justification and Motivation

Primary Causal Conjunction: "Asengencho"

  • Denotes explicit reason or cause (because).
  • Used in indicative contexts explaining current or past events.

Examples:

  • Ngominyoka asengencho inyorire – I run because I'm late.
  • Ere angete gokwana asengencho twomanete – He refused to speak to me because we quarreled.

Variants and Regional Forms

  • Asengencho – commonly used and semantically neutral.
  • Ekiagera – dialectal equivalent with similar causal force.
  • All are intelligible across Gusii dialects but carry regional coloring.

Comparative Functionality

Connector Function Example
(na) erio Future intent Togochenga erinde tobe n'omogoko
Asengencho Present/Past reason Ngoragera ekiagera inde inchara – I eat because I'm hungry

III. Conjunctions in Ekegusii: Syntax, Cohesion, and Logical Relations

Definition & Syntactic Role

  • Conjunctions bind words, phrases, clauses, and ideas, enabling discourse cohesion.
  • Comparable to English in syntactic function but distinct in cultural embedding.

IV. Categories and Semantic Functions

A. Additive & Coordinating Conjunctions

  • Amo na – And
    • → Ing'a eke amo na keria – Give me this and that
  • Amo na – With
    • → Ngogenda inde amo nere – I will go with him
  • Amo – Together
    • → Emphasizes joint action or unity
  • Naende – Also / Even
  • Gose – Or (alternative choice)

B. Disjunctive Usage Examples

Otagete ngende gose intigare – Do you want to go or stay?

C. Coordinated Pairings

  • Obokima na engoko – Ugali and chicken
  • Omoiseke na omomurai – Girl and boy

→ Shows syntactic symmetry and thematic pairing.

V. Cause and Purpose Distinction: Precision in Functional Conjunctions

A. Reason-based Conjunctions (Cause):

  • Asengencho/ Ekiagera → All denote "because"

→ Ngosoma Ekegusii ekiagera ntagete gokemanya – I study Ekegusii because I want to know it.

B. Purpose-based Conjunctions (Intent):

  • Erio → "So that" / "In order to"

→ Ngoragera erio ntware chinguru – I eat so that I may gain strength.

C. Semantic Delineation:

  • Asengencho → Causal justification (backward-looking)
  • Erio → Purposeful intent (forward-looking, often subjunctive)

VI. Imperative & Motivational Syntax

"Erinde" in Imperatives

Infuses directive force in statements:

→ Ore igaa erinde okore emeremo! – You are here to work!

VII. Pedagogical & Linguistic Significance

Syntax Mastery Requires:

  • Contextual use of causal vs. purposive logic
  • Competent coordination of logical, additive, and alternative structures

Educational Implications:

Proficient use of conjunctions enables:

  • → Advanced narration
  • → Persuasive argumentation
  • → Academic and functional fluency in Ekegusii

VIII. Concluding Insights: Functional Artistry in Ekegusii Syntax

Ekegusii's connective system reflects semantic precision, cultural reasoning, and grammatical elegance.

Command of erio, asengencho, na, amo na, and related conjunctions signifies linguistic maturity and narrative sophistication.

These tools are not just grammatical—they are gateways to coherent, persuasive, and culturally attuned communication in the Gusii world.
