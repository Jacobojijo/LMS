4.3.3 The Compliment of Terminal & Specification

1. The Complement of Terminality

Definition and Core Function

  • The Complement of Terminality identifies a destination or receiver of an action.
  • The action verb is directed towards a target, either in the form of a person, group, or location.
  • This structure highlights the endpoint of a verb's effect.

2. Structural Formula

Subject + Verb + Object + Preposition ("gochia") + Terminal Complement (Receiver/Destination)

3. Key Features

  • The preposition "gochia" serves as the grammatical marker that governs the terminal element (destination/receiver).
  • Not all verbs require "gochia" — some inherently imply terminality.

4. Illustrative Examples and Analysis

Nyabuto koerera are ekeene gochia ase abanachi ebina

  • Translation: Nyabuto is narrating the truth to the judges
  • Subject: Nyabuto
  • Verb: koerera (narrate)
  • Object: ekeene (truth)
  • Preposition: gochia
  • Terminal Complement: abanachi ebina (judges)

✧ The action (narrating) is transmitted to a judicial audience.

Makori oenanire riuko gochia ase amonyene.

  • Translation: Makori offers the letter to the owner
  • Verb: koenana (offer)
  • ✧ Verb oenanire typically requires the preposition "gochia" when a recipient is specified.
  • Object: riuko (letter)
  • Terminal Complement: omonyene (owner)

✧ Here, the verb's trajectory terminates at the receiver of the offering.

Inche ngomoande omoiseke ominto chinchugu.

  • Translation: I am giving my sister groundnuts
  • Verb: ngomoande (give)
  • ✧ The verb ngomoande does not require the preposition "gochia."
  • Recipient/Terminal Complement: omoiseke ominto (my sister)

✧ The terminality is implied within the verb's transference semantics.

5. Grammatical Insight

"Oenanire" vs. "Ngomoande":

  • Oenanire often pairs with "gochia" to express offerings to someone.
  • Ngomoande, being an inherently transitive verb, encodes terminality within its structure.

6. The Complement of Specification ("bwe")

Definition and Function

  • The Complement of Specification indicates possession, origin, or characteristic attribution.
  • It defines what belongs to whom, or what characteristic is associated with what object.

7. Structural Formula

Noun Phrase (Head) + "bwe" + Specifier (Possessor/Source)

8. Morphological and Semantic Features

  • "bwe" functions similarly to a genitive or possessive marker in English ("of").
  • The phrase after bwe identifies ownership, association, or origin.
  • Inanimate, animate, abstract, or natural elements can be specifiers.

9. Illustrative Examples and Analysis

Oborabu bw'omobaso

The light of the sun

Oborabu (light) is possessed by Omobaso (sun)

✧ Indicates natural attribution of a phenomenon to its source.

Emerindo y'enyancha

The waves of the lake

Emerindo (waves) originate from enyancha (lake)

✧ Signifies a natural possession or source-based specification.

Chinguru ch'ienchogu

The strength of the elephant

Emphasizes inherent attributes possessed by the noun enchogu (elephant).

Orotacho rw'ebunda.

The kick of the donkey

Signifies typical behavioral traits possessed by a creature.

Eng'enang'eni y'enyancha.

The star of the lake

Could represent a figurative or poetic possession, possibly a reflection or metaphor.

Amatuko y'emegati.

The Easter holidays

"ye" is a plural possessive determiner derived from "bwe"

Indicates the holidays belonging to or associated with Easter.

10. Comparative Observations: "Bwe" vs. "Ye"

  • "Bwe": Singular possessive particle
  • "Ye": Plural possessive (often paired with plural heads like ndalo)

Both forms convey association, possession, or relational linkage.
