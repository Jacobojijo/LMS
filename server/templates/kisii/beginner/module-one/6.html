1.4

Numbers and Numbering

1.4.1

Introduction

Cardinal numbers are essential in the Ekegusii language as they provide a means of counting and quantifying. Rooted in a decimal system, they reveal the linguistic and cognitive organization of the Abagusii people and reflect the cultural importance of structure and precision.

1.4.2

Cardinal Numbers

I. Basic Cardinal Numbers (1–10)

The Ekegusii language employs a decimal counting system whose foundational structure begins from numbers 1 through 10. These basic cardinal numbers are critical for both daily communication and the construction of higher numerals.

Ekegusii Number Number Term in Ekegusii Structure
a) 1 Eyemo Basic Unit
b) 2 Ibere Basic Unit
c) 3 Isato Basic Unit
d) 4 Inye Basic Unit
e) 5 Isano Basic Unit
f) 6 Isano nemo Compound: 5 + 1
g) 7 Isano na ibere / Isan'aibere Compound: 5 + 2
h) 8 Isano na isato / Isan'aisato Compound: 5 + 3
i) 9 Kianda Basic Unit
j) 10 Ikomi Base-10

The forms from 6 to 8 exhibit additive compounding, where "Isano" (5) acts as a base to which smaller units are added.

II. Construction of Compound Numbers (11–19)

Ekegusii forms compound numbers using an additive structure combining "Ikomi" (10) with the basic units.

Ekegusii Number Number Term in Ekegusii Structure
a) 11 Ikomi nemo 10 + 1
b) 12 Ikomi na ibere 10 + 2
c) 13 Ikomi na isato 10 + 3
d) 14 Ikomi na inye 10 + 4
e) 15 Ikomi na isano 10 + 5
f) 16 Ikomi na isano nemo 10 + 6
g) 17 Ikomi na isano na ibere 10 + (5 + 2)
h) 18 Ikomi na isano na isato 10 + (5 + 3)
i) 19 Ikomi na kianda 10 + 9

Each compound integrates the base-ten numeral "ikomi" with logical additions of subsequent units, maintaining consistency and linguistic clarity.

III. Multiples of Ten (20–90)

The term 'Emerongo' functions as the linguistic stem for denoting multiples of ten. Numerical prefixes are appended to generate the correct values.

40 Emerong'ene "Emerongo" + 4

90 Emerongo kianda "Emerongo" + 9

Note: The structural pattern from 60 onward reflects an extended additive compounding of 50 + units.

Ekegusii uses multiple lexemes to denote hundreds: Emia, Chimia, Rigana, and Amagana. These are attached to numerals 1–9 to signify scaled values.

V. Thousands: Prefix Chiribu

The prefix Chiribu (or Eribu for 1000) is used for expressing values in the thousands.

Number Term in Ekegusii
1000 Eribu eyemo
2000 Chiribu ibere
3000 Chiribu isato
4000 Chiribu inye
5000 Chiribu isano
6000 Chiribu isano nemo
7000 Chiribu isano na ibere
8000 Chiribu isano na isato
9000 Chiribu kianda

Compound values (e.g., 9999) are formed by additive concatenation:

  • 990 → Chimia kianda n'emerongo kianda
  • 9900 → Chiribu kianda n'echimia kianda
  • 9999 → Chiribu kianda, chimia kianda na emerongo kianda na kianda

VII. Practical Usage and Cultural Relevance

Ekegusii cardinal numbers serve diverse and essential functions in cultural and societal domains:

  1. Trade and Economy

    Purpose: Measurement, pricing, and enumeration of goods.
    Example: Abarai basimekire emete emerongo etano n'ebere ("Our leaders have planted seventy trees").

  2. Timekeeping and Agriculture

    Purpose: Marking calendar cycles, market days, seasonal planting.
    Linguistic Precision: Ensures accuracy in oral record-keeping.

  3. Family and Social Structures

    Purpose: Counting family members, assigning responsibilities.
    Example: Omokungu oyomo n'abasacha babere ("One woman and two men").

VIII. Applied Examples: Using Numbers with Countable Nouns

Below are applied instances where cardinal numbers are integrated with countable nouns:

Question Ekegusii Response Structure
a) How many goats? (5) Chimbori isano Goats – five
b) How many girls? (9) Abaiseke/ Chinyaroka kianda Girls – nine
c) How many homes? (15) Emechi/ Chinka ikomi na isano Homes – ten + five
d) How many children? (11) Abana ikomi noyomo Children – ten + one
e) How many houses? (10) Chinyomba ikomi Houses – ten

Note:
The construction [Noun] + Irenga + [Number] is a typical grammatical pattern for quantifying in Ekegusii.
