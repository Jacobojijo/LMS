3.4.3 Verb Conjugation (Verb to be) & (to have)

1. Introduction to the Verb "To Be" in Ekegusii

Ekegusii does not have a direct equivalent of the verb "to be" in present-tense declarative statements (e.g., "The food is tasty" becomes "Endagera nenyansu!" without a verb).

However, identity and location are conveyed through special constructions and verb forms like "noo-", "noonde", and copular structures with pronouns.

2. Personal Pronouns with "To Be" (Identity and Role)

Dholuo uses personal pronouns + contextual cues (often without a verb) to express identity.

Person Pronoun Translation
First Person Singular Inche I am
Second Person Singular Aye you are
Third Person Singular Ere he/she/it is
First Person Plural Intwe we are
Second Person Plural Inwe you (pl) are
Third Person Plural Barabwo they are

Examples:

Inche nepresident – I am the president.

Aye nomworokia– You are the teacher.

Intwe nabagusii– We are the Abagusii.

3. Subject-Pronoun Initial Consonants as Person Indicators

Ekegusii verb conjugation often uses initial letters of personal pronouns to indicate the subject:

Prefix Person
i- First person singular
a- Second person singular
E- Third person singular
i- First person plural
i- Second person plural
b- Third person plural

Examples in Coordinated Subjects:

Inche amo nere – I and him

Ere amo nabarabwo – He and they

Barabwo amo nainwe – They and you (pl.)

4. Expressing Presence and Location: The Verb "noo-"

The verb "noo-" means "to be located" or "to be present".

When a personal pronoun is the subject, it is used in full form before the verb.

Pronoun + tie Form Meaning
Inche Inche noonde I am present
Aye Aye noore You (sg) are present
Ere Ere noare He/she is present
Intwe Intwe nootore We are present
Inwe Inwe noomore You (pl) are present
Barabwo Barabwo noobare They are present

Location Examples:

Inche noonde igaa – I am here

Intwe nootore igaa – We are here

Barabwo noobare igaa – They are here

5. Special Case: "Noo-" for Overt Subjects

"Noo-" is used when the subject is explicit/named, not pronominal (e.g., not "I", "you", "he").

Examples:

Endagera neroo? – Is there food?

Ee, endagera neroo. – Yes, there is food.

Nooare igaa He is here.

Paul nooare igaa. – Paul is here.

6. The Opposite of Neroo: "Teiyo" (Negation of Presence)

Teiyo is used to indicate non-existence or absence.

Examples:

Endagera teiyo – There is no food.

Ere taiyo – He/she is not present.

7. Expressing Possession: The Verb "To Have"

Ekegusii expresses "to have" using the personal pronoun + ere, meaning "with".

Forms:

Structure Meaning
[Pronoun] + aye I/you/he has…
[Pronoun] + ere I/you/he has… (alt)
[Overt Subject] + inwe+ ba Subject has…

Examples with Pronouns:

Nimbwate endagera. – I have food.

Ere nabwate esese. – He has a dog.

Barabwo mbabwate abana. – They have children.

Inche nimbwate enyongo y'endagera. – I have a cooking pot.

Examples with Overt Subject:

Omorugi togokonyana emeremo nabwate abana. – My co-wife has children.

Note: "ni" is used only when the subject is explicit.

8. Cultural-Linguistic Note

These forms convey identity, location, and possession—core aspects of social and cultural representation.

In Ekegusii society, self-identification (e.g., Intwe Nabagusii), presence (nootore), and possession (ntobwate) express not only grammar but status, role, and community belonging.

9. Summary Table: Key Verb Forms

Function Form Explanation
Identity Pronoun + Inche "Inche nomworokia" → I am a teacher
Presence Pronoun + noooare "Ere nooare" → He is present
With overt subject Neroo "Endagera neroo" → Food is present
Absence Teiyo "Endagera teiyo" → No food
Possession (pronoun) Nimbwate "Nimbwate endagera" → I have food
Possession (overt subj.) Nabwate "Paul nabwate endagera" → Paul has food
