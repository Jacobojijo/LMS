3.6.2 Intransitive & Reflexive Verbs

1. Intransitive Verbs in Ekegusii

Definition:

Intransitive verbs are verbs that do not take a direct object.

In Ekegusii, these verbs stand independently and describe actions that are complete without affecting another noun.

Examples of Common Intransitive Verbs:

Ekegusii Verb English Meaning
Kora emeremo to work
Gotera to sing
Korara to sleep
Gotenga to dance
Kominyoka to run
Goisaransa to sit

Example Sentences:

Ngoisaransa ase ekerogo – I sit on the chair.
Barabwo nabakong'u as'emeremo – They work hard.

Note: The emphasis is on the subject performing the action without a direct object.

2. The Verb "To Have" in Ekegusii

A. Possession using "-bwate" (with):

In Ekegusii, the idea of "having" is expressed by combining a personal pronoun with the conjunctive form of "with" — -bwate.

The construction is compact: Personal Pronoun + -bwate = "To have"

Conjugation Table (Possessive with "te"):

Pronoun Ekegusii Form English
I Inche nimbwate I have
You (sg.) Aye nobwate You have
He/She Ere nabwate He/She has
We Intwe ntobwate We have
You (pl.) Inwe imobwate You (pl.) have
They Barabwo mbabwate They have

Examples:

Inche nimwate ekerogo – I have a chair.
Barabwo mbabwate omogondo – They have land.
Inwe imobwate enyomba – You (pl.) have a house.
Ere nabwate echikoni – He has a hut.
Ere nabwate omosacha oye – She has a husband.
Intwe ntobwate esese – We have a dog.

3. The Verb "To Have" as an Auxiliary Verb – Re

Usage in the Imperfect Past Tense:

The verb "re" expresses the idea of having done something or an action that has been completed in the past.

It is always used in compound verb constructions to indicate perfect aspect (have + past participle).

Conjugated Examples:

Ekegusii Form English Translation
Naetire amatemu I have passed the exam.
Naachire I have gone.
Narageire I have eaten.
Ere orarire He/she has slept.
Barabwo barageire They have eaten.
Inche nararire I have slept.
Intwe twamenyire We have stayed.
Aye gwachire Kisumu You have gone to Kisumu.
Ere ochire koruga She has gone to cook (She is married).
Ere nanyuomire (nomorugi) She is married (gone to marriage).
Nanyuomire She is married.
Ere okure He/She/It has died.

Note: The auxiliary "re" is sensitive to the tense, especially the past perfect, denoting an action completed recently or relevant to the present.

4. Reflexive Verbs in Ekegusii

A. Forming Reflexive Verbs:

Reflexiveness is expressed by adding the suffix -re to the infinitive verb.

This typically means the subject is doing the action to oneself or among themselves.

Infinitive vs Reflexive Comparison:

Verb Meaning Reflexive Meaning
Koruga to cook kweruga to cook itself
Rora to see kweorora to see oneself/each other
Somi to teach gosoma to learn (teach oneself)
Renda to keep Rendire to be kept

B. Reflexive Verb Conjugation Table (Gosoma – to learn):

Subject Ekegusii English
I Inche ngosomnde I am learning (teach myself)
You (sg.) Aye ngosomore You are learning
He/She Ere ngosomare He/She is learning
We Intwe ngosoma tore We are learning
You (pl.) Inwe ngosoma more You are (pl.) learning
They Barabwo ngosoma bare They are learning

C. Reflexive Verbs Used as Nouns – -esibi Form:

The suffix "-esibi" converts a reflexive verb into a noun form, representing the state or action itself.

Examples:

Reflexive Action Ekegusii English Translation
Washing oneself Gwesibi mbuya Washing oneself is good.
Not washing Ere tatageti gwesibi He does not want to wash himself.
Fighting Korwana aseintwe mbobe Fighting one another is bad.
Refusing to fight Mbatageti korwana ase barabwo abanyene They don't want to fight each other.

5. Special Notes and Clarifications:

  • Nyuoma (to marry): In Ekegusii culture, only a man marries, thus in linguistic usage the subject of the verb nyuoma is traditionally male.
  • No Object Marker: Ekegusii verbs do not use separate object pronouns—subject prefixes alone determine who is performing the action.
  • Conjunctions: Ekegusii uses subject prefixes rather than separate conjunctions; these carry most of the grammatical weight.
  • Important Morphological Point: Possessive constructions are written as single units in usage and should be read as such.
