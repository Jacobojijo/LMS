3.4.1 Introduction

In Ekegusii, verbs typically end in "-a", like "koragera" (to eat). Ekegusii verbs often have a complex morphology with various verbal extensions like applicative, locative, reflexive, reciprocal, and stative, which influence their meaning and usage.

Verb Infinitives:

Ekegusii verbs usually end in "-a," indicating the infinitive form.

Examples: "rika" (to write), "chaka" (to start), "soma" (to study), "genda" (to go), "ancha" (to like).

Verb Phrase Structure:

Ekegusii verb phrases can be quite complex, incorporating verbs, adjectives, nouns, prepositions, and adverbs.

For instance, " Kogendi egari enyia mambia ase erami mbwango" translates to "Driving a new vehicle in the morning on a tarmac road very fast," showcasing the various constituents that can be included in a Ekegusii verb phrase.

Verbal Extensions:

Ekegusii verbs can be modified with various extensions, which change their meaning and use.

These extensions include:

  • Applicative: Indicates the agent or instrument involved in an action.
  • Locative: Indicates location or direction.
  • Reflexive: Indicates the action is performed on the subject itself.
  • Reciprocal: Indicates an action performed between two or more people.
  • Stative: Indicates a state or condition.

3.4.2 Infinitive verbs

1. Introduction to the Indicative Mood in Ekegusii

The indicative mood is used to state facts, describe reality, or give declarative information.

In Ekegusii, the indicative mood often pairs personal pronouns with the verb "to be" (N) or other infinitive verbs to make clear statements.

2. Infinitive Verbs in Ekegusii

Most infinitive verbs in Ekegusii end in "-a" or "-bi".

These are the base forms of verbs, used for actions or states without indicating tense or subject.

Example endings:

  • Kora – to do
  • Renda– to keep
  • Isaransa– to sit/stay
  • Rengereria – to think

3. Exception: The Verb "To Be" (N)

The verb "to be" in Ekegusii is highly irregular.

It does not follow the "-a" or "-bi" ending pattern of other infinitive verbs.

It is marked simply by the consonant "N", with a personal pronoun prefix that varies depending on the subject.

Despite changes in the prefix, "N" remains constant in all present tense forms.

4. Present Tense Conjugation of "To Be" (N)

The structure is:

[Subject Pronoun] + [e/N] + [Object]

Second person sg. Aye Omworokia Aye n'omworokia You are the teacher

In Ekegusii, personal pronouns influence the form of the verb, especially the verb "to be" (N).

6. Structure of Simple Indicative Sentences

Pattern:

[Subject] + [Verb "to be"] + [Predicate/Object]

Examples:

Inche n'epresident → I am the president

Aye nomworokia → You are the teacher

Ere nomworokia → He is the teacher

Intwe nabagusii → We are the Abagusii

Inwe n'abakenya → You (pl.) are Kenyans

Barabwo nabanto ba Nairobi → They are people of Nairobi

7. Cultural-Linguistic Insight

In Ekegusii, identity statements (e.g., "I am a teacher") are fundamental in introducing oneself or others.

These constructions using "to be" (N) express roles, professions, ethnicities, and group belonging.

Example: Intwe nabagusii → We are the Abagusii (ethnic identity)

The verb "to be" functions as a core element in self-representation and social description.
