3.8.2 Present Tense

1. Definition and Function of the Present Tense in Ekegusii

  • (Present Tense) describes actions that:
    • Are happening now (present progressive)
    • Happen habitually (simple present)
  • In Ekegusii, there is no distinction between:
    • He eats (simple present)
    • He is eating (present progressive) ➝ Both are translated with the same verb form.

2. General Verb Form in Present Tense

  • Ekegusii verbs retain their infinitive endings), even when conjugated.
  • Present tense conjugation involves prefixation to the infinitive verb stem.
  • The infinitive form serves as the root for conjugation.

3. Present Tense Prefix Formation

Pronoun Prefix Conjugation (using chiemo - to eat) Meaning
Inche i- Inche ngoragerande I eat / I am eating
Aye a- Aye ragera You eat
Ere e- Ere ngoragerare He/She eats
Intwe in- Intwe ngoragera tore We eat
Inwe i- inwe ngoragera more You (pl.) eat
Barabwo ba- Barabwo ngoragera bare They eat

4. Overt Subject Rule

  • When a proper noun or overt subject (e.g., Wayne, Olum, Mama) is used:
    • The subject prefix is dropped.
    • The verb appears in its infinitive form without a subject prefix.

Examples:

Sentence Structure Meaning
Wayne nechiombe akorisi Overt subject (Wayne) + korisi Wayne is grazing the cows
Sylivia aaraire Sylivia + araire Sylivia is sleeping
Abuya n'emeremo agokora Abuya + emeremo Abuya is working
Abana nesukuru bakogenda Children + go to school Children go to school
Baba nyagitari akogenda Baba + go to hospital Mum is going to hospital
Kwamboka Ngooni Overt subject + infinitive verb Kwamboka sells

Compare with:

  • Kooni → She sells (No overt subject ➝ prefix used)

6. Verb Root Observations

  • Most verbs in their infinitive form end in:
    • -a (e.g., gokora, gotenga, gochiesa)
    • -i (e.g., kori)

These endings are retained in all present tense forms.

8. Complex Present Tense Usage in Narrative Context

  • Narrative example

Abuya okinire naende osibirie obosio bwaye. Nigo atagete kogenda egetii korisia chiombe

  • Translation:
    • Abuya has grown and washed his face. He wants to go to the field to herd cattle.
  • Even within present-tense structure, aspectual markers like -re (perfective marker) are used for completed actions.

9. Morphological Insight

  • Ekegusii's present tense system illustrates:
    • Prefixation for subject pronouns
    • Infinitive root retention
    • No morphological distinction between simple and progressive forms
  • The agglutinative nature of Ekegusii is evident in:
    • Embedded grammatical information (subject + tense) within a single verb form

10. Implications for Language Learning

  • Learners must focus on:
    • Correct prefixation based on the pronoun
    • Recognizing when not to use prefixes (with overt subjects)
    • Understanding that context determines whether the verb implies habitual or ongoing action
