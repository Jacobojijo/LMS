3.5.1 Summary of Adjectives & Verbs

Introduction to Comparatives in Ekegusii

Comparative constructions in Ekegusii are used to express:

  • Relative comparison (e.g., taller, sweeter)
  • Equality comparison (e.g., as tall as, as sweet as)

Core comparison strategies:

  • Direct comparative forms (shortened adjective forms)
  • "Kebwakaine" for similarity comparisons (similar to)
  • "buna" for equality expressions (as...as)

Comparative Forms of Adjectives

Morphological changes occur in forming comparatives:

Often involves syllabic reduction from the original adjective:

  • Egetambe → egetambe ake (tall → taller)
  • Enyansu → enyansu ake (sweet → sweeter)
  • Ekebe → ekebe mono (bad → worse)
  • ekiya→ ekiya ake(good → better)
  • omong'aini → omong'aini ake (wise → wiser)

Standard Comparative Construction:

  • Structure: [Subject A] [comparative adjective] kobua [Subject B]
  • kobua means "than"
  • E.g.: Eke negetambe ake kobua eke → "This is taller than that one."

Special note on 'egetambe ake' (tall/long):

  • Does not undergo morphological change in comparative use.
  • Its comparative force depends on structure, not form.

Equality Comparisons in Ekegusii

Two main forms used:

  • "Kebwekaine" → meaning "similar to"
  • "buna" → meaning "as...as"

Standard constructions:

  • eke [adjective] kebwekaine [comparison entity]
  • Kenga [adjective] buna [comparison entity]

Examples:

  • Eke negiansu buna eke → "This is as sweet as that one."
  • Oyo nomong'aini buna oyo → "This is as wise as that one."

"buna" functions as a flexible syntactic tool for equality in quality across entities.

Superlative Constructions in Dholuo

Formed with: [adjective] kobua bonsi → meaning "more than all" or "the most"

  • kobua = more than
  • bonsi = all

Structure: [Subject] [adjective] kobua bonsi

E.g.: Ere nomong'aini kobua bonsi → "His wife is the most intelligent."

Examples of Superlative Forms:

  • Ekiya kobua mono → the best
  • Omong'aini kobua bonsi → the most intelligent
  • Egiansu kobua bionsi → the sweetest
  • Egetambe kobua bionsi → the tallest / longest

Intensifiers: Expressing High Degree or Extremes

Intensifiers are context-dependent phrases used to express extreme quality:

  • Ekerabu mono → very white
  • Ekebariri mono → very red
  • Ekemwamu mono→ very black/dark
  • Egekendu mono→ very cold
  • Omong'aini mono → very clever

Illustrative Usage:

  • Ere nomong'aini mono → "He is very clever."
  • Omobaso obarire se → "The sun is very hot!"

Cultural-Linguistic Insights

Comparative expression in Ekegusii extends beyond physical traits to include:

  • Moral attributes (e.g., Ekebe – bad, Ekiya – good)
  • Intellectual traits (e.g., Omong'aini – wise, Omong'aini mono – very clever)
  • Sensory/emotional descriptors (e.g., egiansut – sweet)

The superlative form ("kobua mono") reflects a communal standard of excellence, where moral and intellectual virtues are emphasized alongside physical qualities.

These structures reveal deep cultural values rooted in:

  • Character and wisdom
  • Social respectability
  • Community-based evaluation of individual merit

1. Introduction to Ekegusii Verbs

Most Ekegusii verbs end in -a (e.g., endagera "to eat").

Verbs exhibit rich morphological structures, accommodating various verbal extensions:

  • Applicative (instrument/agent)
  • Locative (place/direction)
  • Reflexive (self-directed action)
  • Reciprocal (mutual action)
  • Stative (state/condition)

2. Infinitive Verbs in Ekegusii

Characteristically end in -a or -bi.

Used to express actions or states without tense or subject.

Common Examples:

kora (to do), Gacha (to keep), ikaransa (to sit), rengereria (to think), soma (to study).

3. Indicative Mood and Sentence Structure

Used for declarative statements or factual assertions.

Structure: [Subject Pronoun] + [Verb] + [Object/Complement]

E.g., Inche n'epresidenti → "I am the president"

4. Irregular Verb: "To Be" (Copular N)

Highly irregular: does not follow -a/-bi pattern.

Represented by N, modified with personal pronoun prefixes:

  • Inche Ne.... (singular)
  • Intwe Ne..., Inwe Ne..., Barabwo Ne... (plural)

Used to state identity, role, or classification.

Subject Pronouns and Copular Verb "To Be" (N):

5. Expressing Presence: Verb "noo-"

Denotes being present or existing in a location.

Pronoun-based conjugation:

Inche noonde igaa (I am here), noore igaa(you are here), ere nooare igaa (he/she is here), etc.

Often used with locative adverbials: ka (here), ko (there)

Examples:

  • Inche noonde igaa → I am here
  • Intwe nootore igaa → We are here
  • Barabwo noobare igaa→ They are here

6. Presence with Overt Subject: "Noo-re"

Used when subject is named or explicit, not a pronoun.

  • Endagera neroo? → Is there food?
  • Paul noaree. → Paul is present.

7. Negation of Presence: "teiyo"

Used to express absence or non-existence.

  • Endagera teiyo → There is no food
  • Ere taiyo → He/She is not present

8. Expression of Possession: "To Have"

No direct equivalent of English "have"

Expressed using:

  • [Pronoun] + ni (with)
  • [Overt Subject] + ni + mbwate

Examples with Pronouns:

  • Inche nimbwate endagera → I have food
  • Ere nabwate esese → He has a dog
  • Barabwo mbabwate abana → They have children
  • Nimbwate enyongo y'endagera → I have a cooking pot

Examples with Overt Subject:

  • Omorugi togokonyana nere emeremo nabwate abana → My co-wife has children

10. Cultural-Linguistic Insights

The verbs tie, nitie, and onge are foundational for expressing existence, identity, and absence.

Expressions like Intwe nabagusii (We are Gusii's) assert ethnic and communal identity.

Possession indicates social roles, responsibilities, and relationships.

These structures reflect the social fabric of Gusii culture, where roles, belonging, and presence are linguistically emphasized.
