3.8.6 Future Tense & Future Continuous Tense

1. General Overview of Future Tense in Ekegusii

  • The future tense in Ekegusii is constructed primarily using auxiliary verbs that precede the main verb.
  • The most common auxiliary used to express the immediate or definite future is "ngochi-", conjugated as "ngochande" ("will come").
  • The main verb in the construction remains in the infinitive and is not conjugated.
  • This structure semantically aligns with the English "will" or "shall" to express future intention, certainty, or prediction.

2. Primary Structure of Immediate Future Tense

Construction Pattern:

Subject prefix + ngochande + verb (infinitive form)
  • Infinitive Verb: Does not change based on subject or tense.
  • "Ngochande" Auxiliary: This is the only element that undergoes subject agreement (conjugation).

2.1 Examples Using "Koragera" (to eat)

English Ekegusii Contracted Form
I will eat Ngochinde koragera Ngochi koragera'nde
You will eat Ngochioro koragera Ngochi koragerar'ore
He/She will eat Ngochiare koragera Ngochi korager'are
We will eat Ngochitore koragera Ngochi koragerat'ore
You (pl.) will eat Ngochimore koragera Ngocho korageram'ore
They will eat Ngochibare koragera Ngochi koragerab'are

3. Morphological Structure of "ngochande" Construction

  • "Ngochande" is derived from the root "ngocha" (to come).
  • Conjugates per subject:
    i- (I), a- (you sg.), e- (he/she/it), in- (we), inw- (you pl.), ba- (they)

4. The "Ngocha-" Contraction Form

  • Contraction of "Ngocha" with the verb is common in casual or spoken Ekegusii.
  • Merges the subject prefix + ch- (shortened biro) + verb:
    • Ngochi koragerande → "I'll eat"
    • Ngochiore koragera → "You'll eat"
  • These forms are phonologically fused but retain full syntactic function.

5. Future of "To Be": Using "Ngochi"

  • "Ngochi" = to be, and remains uninflected in future constructions.
  • Future form: Subject prefix + ngocha + ngocha
  • Example:
    Ngochi konyorande abana babere → "I will have (be with) two children."
    Literal: "I come to be with children two."

6. Alternate Expressions of Future

These express various degrees of futurity, using different temporal markers before the verb.

6.1 Immediate or Imminent Future

Nabeire ang'e koirana→ "I'm about to return"
Literally: "I come to return"

6.2 Near Future (Later Today)

Ng'ora rero ngoirana inde → "Later today I will return"
Derived from: Rero ning'irane ("Today that I return")

6.3 Tomorrow

Mambia ngoirana → "Tomorrow I'll return"
From: Mambia ning'irane("Tomorrow that I return")

6.4 Indefinite Future

Mosuko/Rinde/Ngaki ende ning'irane→ "Sometime in the future I'll return"

7. Future Continuous Tense

  • Formed using the structure: ngochi + ko + verb
  • Expresses ongoing future actions (parallel to English "will be doing").
  • Examples:
    • Ngochinde komorora → "I will be going to visit him/her"
    • Barabwo ngochi koragera bare buya → "They will be eating well"

8. Expressing Obligation: "Gioka" Construction

  • "Goika" = must / should / have to
  • Used to mandate action or express moral, social, or grammatical necessity.
  • Structure:
    Goika + subject prefix + verb
  • Examples:
    • Goika okwane → "You must/should speak"
    • Goika oragere → "You must eat"
    • Goika tomorore ere → "We must see him"
  • Note:
    This construction carries strong obligation (akin to "must") but is also used for softened directives (akin to "should") depending on tone and context.

9. Syntactic Observations

  • The verb following "ngochande" or "ngochi" is always in the infinitive, and not marked for subject agreement.
  • The auxiliary verb "ngochande" or "goika" carries all agreement and tense information.
  • Temporal adverbials like are often used to specify the time frame of the future action.

10. Pedagogical Notes for Learners

10.1 Learner Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Do not conjugate the main verb in the future tense; only conjugate the auxiliary "Ngochi".
  • Avoid confusing contracted forms (e.g., obochiemo) with single-word verbs — recognize them as fusion of auxiliary + infinitive.
  • Understand that ngochande ≠ch-i; the former is an auxiliary used for tense marking, the latter is simply "come".
