3.3.2 Comparison of two things & Superlatives

1. Comparing Two Things (Relative Comparison)

a. Standard Comparative Structure

In Ekegusii, comparing two distinct entities (e.g., people, places, qualities) involves the phrase:

kobua = "than"

This phrase follows the adjective, forming the equivalent of "X is more [adjective] than Y".

b. Sentence Structure:

[Subject A] [adjective] kobua [Subject B]

Examples:

Eke negetambe kobua keria
→ This is taller than that one.

Eke nekieng'e kobua keria
→ This is shorter than that one.

Ekiane nekiya kobua ekiago
→ Mine is better than yours.

Omorugi oye nomuya kobua babo
→ His wife is better than theirs.

Omogondo one nomonene kobua oyogo
→ My farm is bigger than yours.

Enyomba yane nenyia kobua eyaye
→ My house is newer than his/hers.

Enchera eye nentambe kobua eyio
→ This way is longer than that one.

c. Special Note on "nentambe" (long)

Unlike most adjectives that change form in the comparative, "nentambe" (used to describe length or height) does not change morphologically.

Instead, the degree of comparison is shown purely by the structure, particularly with the inclusion of "kobua eyio".

Example: " Enchera eye nentambe kobua eyio " → "This way is longer than that one."

2. Comparisons Using "buna" (Equality)

a. Structure for Expressing Equality:

Eke [adjective] buna [second entity]

"as [adjective] as [that one]"

b. Examples of Equality Comparisons:

Eke negiansu kobua keria
→ This is as sweet as that one.

Eke nekiya kobua keria
→ This is as good as that one.

Oyo nomonyakieni kobua oria
→ This is as beautiful as that one.

This structure emphasizes parallel quality between two entities.

3. Superlative Forms in Gusii

a. Superlative Structure

The superlative degree is formed by:

[Adjective] + kobua bonsi

"kobua mono" = more than
"-onsi" = all

Together, "kobua mono bonsi" = "more than all" → i.e., the most / the best / the biggest, etc.

b. Sentence Structure:

[Subject] [adjective] kobua bonsi

→ "X is the most [adjective]"

c. Examples of Superlative Forms:

Adjective Superlative Meaning
Buya Buya mono the best
Omong'aini Omong'aini mono the most intelligent
Omoriri Omoriri mono the most stupid
Enyansu Enyansu mono the sweetest
Enene Enene mono the biggest
Entambe Entambe mono the tallest / longest
Chindeu Chindeu mono the weakest / laziest

Illustrative Sentence:

"Omorugi oye nomong'aini mono" → "His wife is the most intelligent."

4. Intensifiers: Expressing Extreme Qualities

a. High Degree Expressions

In Ekegusii, certain adjectives can be intensified with specific intensifier phrases that emphasize extremes.

b. Examples of Intensified Adjectives:

Adjective Phrase Meaning
Ekerabu mono very white
Ekebariri burure very red
Ekemwamu mono very black
'morero mono very hot
Egekendu mono very cold
Omong'aini mono very clever

Example Sentences:

Are omong'aini mono
→ He is very clever.

Omogaso osambire mono
→ The sun is very hot!

Erumu yamwamire mono
→ The room is very black/dark.

5. Summary of Key Grammatical Markers

Function Marker / Phrase Translation / Role
Equality Comparison buna as...as
Similarity Comparison Ebwekaine similar to
Comparative Degree Kobua than
Superlative Degree Mono kobua the most / more than all
Extreme Quality (intensity) Contextual phrase very [quality]

6. Cultural-Linguistic Insight

Gusii expressions of comparison are not confined to physical traits but extend into:

  • Moral (e.g., ekiya, ekebe)
  • Intellectual (e.g., omong'aini, Omong'aini ake)
  • Emotional or sensory qualities (e.g., enyansu for sweetness)

This reflects a deep cultural value placed on character, intellect, and ethical judgment in personal and social interactions.

The use of "kobua mono" for superlatives especially highlights communal awareness of excellence and comparison across domains.
