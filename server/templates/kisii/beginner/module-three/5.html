3.2.1 Summary of Adjectives

1. Morphosyntactic Structure of Ekegusii Adjectives

Function: Modify nouns to express qualities, states, or attributes.

Structural Formation:

Noun + eke- + Adjective Root

E.g., Eke rogo-ekebariri = "Chair that is red"

Prefix "eke-":

  • Acts as a linking particle meaning "that is/which is".
  • Converts descriptor roots into reduced relative clauses, anchoring the adjective to the noun.

2. Morphological Marker of Adjectives

Key Affix: Prefix "eke-"

  • Universally used to form adjectives (except a few lexical exceptions).
  • Function: Binds descriptive semantics to nominal reference.

Examples of Root Transformation:

  • Ekenene → Ebinene ("big")
  • Ekiya → Ebiya ("good")
  • Ekebe → Ebibe ("bad")
  • Ekerito → Ebirito ("heavy")

3. Semantic Classification of Adjectives

A. Physical Appearance & Size

  • Ekenene – big
  • Egeke – small/few
  • Egetambe – tall
  • Ebieng'e – short
  • Ekegare – wide
  • Enyerere – narrow

B. Color

  • Ekerabu – white

Omonto omurabu – "White person"

7.2 Ebinge

Gloss: many

Usage: Plural quantifier for count nouns

Example: Ebitabu ebinge – "Many books"

7.3 Ebike/ Amake

Gloss: few, little

Irregularity: Amake does not follow standard eke- prefixation

Example: Amabere amake – "A little milk"

7.4 Yaisanire

Gloss: enough

Usage: Sufficiency; eke- often dropped in speech

Example: Amache aisanire – "Enough water"

7.5 Enyinge

Gloss: numerous, abundant

Semantic Strength: Exceeds yaisanire in magnitude

Example: Abanto abange– "A multitude of people"

7.6 Obonge

Gloss: plenty

Cultural Nuance: Suggests ample provision or wealth

Example: Amabere as'obonge – "Plenty of milk"

8. Interrogative Quantifiers in Ekegusii

8.1 Mbirenga?

Gloss: How many?

Usage: Quantitative inquiry

Example: Ebitabu mbirenga? – "How many books?"

8.2 Naki anene?

Gloss: How big?

Usage: Measures size or magnitude

Example: Inaki obonene bw'ekerogo? – "How big is the chair?"

9. Linguistic and Cultural Insights

  • Descriptive Accuracy: eke- structures operate as relative clauses, anchoring adjectives to their referents.
  • Cultural Weight: Adjectives such as maber and marach carry moral, aesthetic, and evaluative implications.
  • Lexical Exceptions: Adjectives like ekebese bypass standard prefix rules.
  • Aspectual Semantics: Verb-derived adjectives reflect tense/aspectual distinctions, supporting rich temporal reference within descriptions.
  • Communicative Clarity: Ekegusii avoids "bare adjectives"; descriptors must be grammatically and contextually anchored.

I. Formation and Usage of Color Adjectives

A. Morphological Structure

  • Prefixation of "eke-" to color roots is the primary morphological process for deriving adjectives.
  • The resulting form functions as a descriptive modifier within the noun phrase.
  • Occasionally analyzable as "eke-" + root color, or "ebi-" + color root due to phonological elision.

B. Representative Color Adjectives

  • Embariri (Red)
    Example: Ekerogo ekebariri ("Red chair")
  • Emwamu (Black)
    Example: Eyanga emwamu ("Black cloth")
  • Endabu (White)
    Example: Omonto omorabu ("A white person")
  • Enchembera (Blue): From chabera
    Example: Ekerogo enchembera ("Blue chair")
  • Gesicha (Yellow): From Egesicha("egg yolk color")
    Example: Eyanga gesicha ("Yellow garment")
  • Machani (Green)
    Example: Omote machani ("Green tree")
  • Indigo(Indigo): From Indigo
    Example: Eyanga indigo ("Indigo clothing")
  • Motobu (Purple): From Nyamotobu
    Example: Ebitabu nyamotobu ("Purple books")
  • Matonto (Black and White): From Nyamotonto
    Example: Embori Nyamatonto ("Black and white goat")
  • Maraba (Brown): From raba
    Example: Omochanga maraba ("Brown sand soil")

C. Semantic Considerations

  • Color terms in Ekegusii frequently reflect cultural symbolism (e.g., endabu for purity).
  • Some derive from environmental or material referents
  • Dual-tone and patterned colors (e.g., Matonto) represent complex perceptual categories.

II. Pluralization of Adjectives in Ekegusii

A. General Rule of Agreement

  • Adjectives must morphologically agree with the number of the noun they modify.
  • Plural adjectives generally adopt a -o suffix, producing systematic variations.

B. Morphological Patterns of Pluralization

Singular Adjective Plural Adjective Gloss
Ekiya/ Omonyakieni Ebiya/ Abanyakieni good/beautiful
Ekebe Ebibe bad
Ekenene Ebinene big
Egeke Ebike small/few
Egetambe Ebitambe tall/long
Ekieng'e Ebieng'e short
Emwamu Chimwamu black
Endabu Chindabu white
Ekenge Ebinge many (plural-only)

C. Syntactic Concord

Both noun and adjective change form to reflect plurality:

  • Ekerogo ekenene ("A big chair") → Ebirogo ebinene ("Big chairs")
  • Omoiseke omonyakieni ("A beautiful girl") → Abaiseke abanyakieni ("Beautiful girls")

D. Invariant Adjectives

  • Some adjectives do not undergo inflection in the plural.
  • Typically belong to the color, taste, or emotion semantic fields.

Examples:

  • Chinyange chimbariri → Chinyange chimbariri ("Red bicycle(s)")
  • Amatoke agwansa → Amatoke agwansat ("Sweet banana(s)")
  • Omworokia omwenenu→ Omworokia omwenenu ("Cruel teacher(s)")

III. Semantic Categorization of Adjectival Inflection

A. Adjectives That Inflect

  • Align with standard grammatical agreement in plural constructions.
  • Often describe qualitative or measurable attributes: omuya, omoke, amake, obuya

B. Adjectives That Do Not Inflect

  • Typically, lexicalized or fossilized forms.
  • Include color terms, tastes, and affective descriptors: embariri (red), enyansu (sweet), 'moenenu (cruel)

IV. Structural and Functional Insights

A. Syntactic Regularity

  • Pluralization reinforces grammatical consistency and euphony.
  • Adjective–noun concord is obligatory, particularly in formal registers.

B. Morphological Productivity

Plural forms illustrate rich inflectional morphology, combining prefixation and suffixation strategies.

C. Cultural and Lexical Dimensions

  • Certain color adjectives reflect cultural or environmental realities
  • Invariant forms often carry symbolic, sensorial, or emotive weight, resisting grammatical transformation.

V. Broader Linguistic Implications

  • The plural agreement system aligns Ekegusii with broader Congo basin morphosyntactic typologies.
  • It exemplifies the interplay between grammatical structure and lexical meaning, highlighting the semantic depth of color and descriptive terms.
  • The resistance of some adjectives to pluralization underscores the lexicalization of culturally embedded expressions.
