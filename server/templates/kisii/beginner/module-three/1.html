3.1.1 Adjectives & Adjective Clauses

1. Definition and Morphosyntactic Structure of Adjectives in Ekegusii

Adjectives in Ekegusii serve as modifiers that describe the qualities, states, or attributes of nouns.

Morphologically, Ekegusii adjectives are formed by prefixing the particle "eke-" to a descriptive root or base.

The prefix "eke-" can be glossed as "which is" or "that is" in English, creating a relative adjective clause.

Syntax Structure:

Noun + eke- + Descriptor (Adjective Root)

e.g., Ekerogo Keria ekebariri= "Chair that is red"

2. Morphological Marker: "eke-" as Adjectival Prefix

The particle "eke-" is the defining grammatical marker of adjectives.

It links the noun and the descriptive root, thus forming an adjective phrase or a reduced relative clause.

4. Formation of Adjectives from Verbs

Some adjectives are derived from verbs using "eke-" (present participle/ongoing action) or "ma + past tense verb" (completed action).

This creates adjective clauses, functioning similarly to English relative clauses ("that is X-ing", "that has X-ed").

Rules:

  • eke + verb (root) = ongoing or habitual action
  • eke + ria-verb (past) = completed state/action
  • E.g., Keria giakure → "that has died"

Verb-to-Adjective Conversions:

Verb Gloss Derived Adjective Meaning
Tera to sing Ning'o ogotera who is singing
Gakwe to die Keria kegokwa which is dying
Gakwe to die Ning'o okure which has died (dead)
Gekorosigwa to make/fix Keria gekorosigwa which is being made
Kiarosigwe to make/fix Keria kiarosigwe tayari which is already made

Observations:

The structure offers temporal nuance:

  • kegokwa = present progressive
  • giakure = past perfective

6. Linguistic and Cultural Insights

The prefix e- enables adjectives to function as mini relative clauses, preserving ekeguii's preference for descriptive clarity.

Adjectives carry:

  • Descriptive weight (size, shape, color, morality)
  • Cultural importance (e.g., maber implies moral uprightness)
  • Semantic nuance, especially in verb-derived forms

In discourse, Ekegusii avoids bare descriptors; adjectives must be anchored to nouns or pronouns via e-.
