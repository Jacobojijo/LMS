3.8.5 Past Perfect Tense

Past Perfect Tense in Ekegusii – "Beire" Construction

1. Definition and Grammatical Function

  • The Past Perfect Tense in Ekegusii expresses an action or a state that had occurred before another past event.
  • It denotes a state of having been or having done something, often used for backgrounding in narrative or contrastive temporal sequences.
  • This tense integrates both pastness and completion prior to another past event.

2. Morphological Structure

2.1 Composite Construction

  • The tense is formed through a compositional structure:
    -Ete + subject prefix + r (auxiliary) + beire (participle)
  • Here:
    • "-ete": A past tense auxiliary indicating that the event took place in the past.
    • "-r-": A perfective auxiliary indicating completion (equivalent to "have").
    • "-Beire": The unchanging participial root meaning to be or to have been.
      • Not conjugated itself; remains constant across all persons.

3. Subject-Concord Conjugation Patterns

English Ekegusii Form
I had been Inche nabeire
You had been Aye kwabeire
He/She/It had been Ere obeire
We had been Intwe twabeire
You (pl.) had been Inwe mwabeire
They had been Barabwo babeire
  • Note: The subject prefix (i-, a- e-, in-, inw-, ba-) integrates with -r- to express the subject-specific perfective.

4. Semantic Characteristics

  • Indicates a state or activity that:
    • Started earlier and
    • Was still relevant or ongoing at a reference point in the past.
  • Often used to set the scene or describe contextual background before a main past event.

5. Syntactic Features

  • "-beire" functions as a non-finite participle, following the auxiliary construction.
  • Negation, temporal adverbials, or dependent clauses (like rireri = "when") may follow the construction.
  • Sentence structure:
    Subject + -eti + subject-perfective + -beire + (optional clause)

6. Exemplary Sentences and Analysis

6.1 Individual Conjugated Examples

  • Nabeire ngoganyete → I had been waiting for you
    • "na" = I have, "beire" = been; ngo ganyete = when you delayed.
  • Obeire okominyoka → He had been running
    • Used to express an ongoing action prior to a referenced point in time.
  • Yabeire egotwa → It had been raining
    • Emphasizes environmental condition before another event.
  • Babeire bagotarerana → They had been visiting one another
    • Suggests a reciprocal, prolonged action concluded before another.
  • Moraa obeire okomotarera → Moraa had been visiting her
    • Describes past background behavior of Moraa preceding another narrative moment.

7. Linguistic Observations

  • The participle "beire":
    • Does not inflect or take agreement markers.
    • Is grammatically stable and attaches only to auxiliary constructions.
  • The auxiliary -r-:
    • Functions semantically similar to the English "have" in the present perfect.
    • Indicates a completed or endured state that remains relevant at a past moment.
  • The pre-verbal "-ete":
    • Places the whole construction unequivocally in the past, removing any present relevance.

8. Pragmatic and Pedagogical Implications

8.1 Learner Caution Areas

  • Learners must not:
    • Conjugate beire independently.
    • Confuse Obeire ogo- (he had been) with obeire (he has been) — the presence of "ete" shifts tense.
    • Replace -r- with habitual -ko, which alters aspectual meaning.

8.2 Instructional Emphasis

  • Focus on the fixed pattern:
    -Ete + subject + -r + -beire
  • Encourage oral practice with subordinate time clauses (e.g., ko + verb) to deepen temporal awareness.
  • Train learners to recognize this form in longer narrative structures for backgrounding.
