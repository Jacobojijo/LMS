3.9.1 Summary of Tenses

1. Tense Overview

Ekegusii verbs are categorized into past, present, and future, each with:

  • Simple
  • Continuous (Progressive)
  • Perfect
  • Perfect Continuous

Present tense refers to:

  • Current actions (e.g., Wayne ngoragerare – Wayne is eating)
  • Habitual actions (e.g., Paul ngochare oragera chinsa chia'mambia– Paul usually eats in the morning)

2. Verb Structure and Conjugation

Verbs retain infinitive endings: typically -a or -i
(e.g., koragera – to eat, korisia – to graze)

Conjugation by prefixing subject markers to verb root:

Pronoun Prefix Example Translation
Inche i- Inche ngoragerande I eat/am eating
Aye a- Aye ngoragera ore You eat
Ere e- Ere ngoragera are He/She eats
Intwe int- Intwe ngoragera tore We eat
Inwe in- Inwe ngoragera more You (pl.) eat
Barabwo ba- Barabwo ngoragera bare They eat

3. Overt Subject Rule

With proper nouns or explicit subjects, subject prefix is omitted:

  • Wayne ngorggera are – Wayne is eating
  • Baba nyagitari agochi – Mum is going to hospital
  • Syliviah ngooni are – Syliviah' sells

4. Present Continuous vs. Simple Present

No morphological distinction:

  • Abuya ngotara are – Abuya walks / is walking
  • Contextual cues determine aspectual interpretation (e.g., time expressions or discourse)

5. Habitual Aspect – Use of "-ko"

"-ngochare" suffix marks habitual/repeated actions:

  • Paul ngochare oragera chinsa chia mambia – Paul usually eats in the morning
  • Omwana anarete korari nyuma yokoragera – The child usually sleeps after eating
  • Morphological clarity reduces context dependency

6. Morphological & Syntactic Features

Agglutinative structure:

  • Subject + tense often embedded in a single verb form
    (e.g., endagera = e- (ko) + koragera (eat))
  • Infinitives used both in overt-subject constructions and present tense conjugations

7. Pedagogical Insights

Learner focus:

  • Mastering subject prefixes
  • Using "-ko" for habitual clarity
  • Interpreting aspect through contextual clues
  • Challenge: Absence of explicit progressive marker requires contextual inference

Past Tenses

1. Simple Past Tense

Function: Marks completed past actions using the auxiliary "-ete" (or abbreviated "te").

Structure: Subject + te/ete + Verb + Object

Examples:

  • Dwallow nachiete korisia – Dwallow went to graze cattle
  • Nariete enswe – I ate fish
  • Baba nagorerete abakori emeremo esoda – Mother bought soda for the workers

Word Order Sensitivity: Pronoun and object positioning shifts meaning:

  • Ere namonyorete – He met him
  • Barabwo mbabanyorete – They met them

2. Contracted Past Forms

"te" merges with subject prefixes for concise forms.

Examples:

  • Inche inarigete → inarigete – I wrote
  • Ere narigete → narigete – He wrote

3. Immediate Past (Prefix "N-")

Used for just-completed actions.

Example: Naremire – I have just dug (now stopped)

4. Yesterday Past (Prefix "-renge/bwangoiga-")

Indicates actions that occurred specifically yesterday.

Example: Inarenge omorwaire igoro – I was sick yesterday

5. Extended Past Markers

Marker Meaning Example Translation
-renge Two days ago Inche enarenge omworwaire amatuko abere aerire I was sick two days ago
bwangoiga Recently Inche enarenge omorwaire bwangoiga I was sick recently
Bono- Just now Nabeire omorwaire Bono mbuyande I was just sick (now better)

6. Recent Past Perfective ("-r-" Infix)

Structure: Subject Prefix + -r- + Verb

Function: Denotes recently completed actions (Present Perfect equivalent).

Examples:

  • Inche narageire – I have eaten
  • Ere orageire – He/she has eaten

7. Past Continuous / Habitual

Structure: -ere + Subject Prefix + Verb + -o

Function: Repeated or ongoing past action.

Examples:

  • Inche ngoragere nare – I was eating
  • Inche ngosoma nare Ekegusii inka – I used to study Ekegusii at home
  • Syliviah nanchete gotengera ogotenga – Syliviah used to enjoy dances

8. Morphological Insights

  • "ere" = Auxiliary (unchanging)
  • Subject agreement still applies.
  • Agglutinative nature: Meaning encoded in particles/prefixes/suffixes.
  • Temporal interpretation is highly morphological.

Past Perfect Tense (Bedo Construction)

1. Definition & Function

  • Expresses actions completed before another past event.
  • Used for backgrounding and sequencing in narratives.

2. Morphological Structure

Formula: -ete + subject prefix + r + beire

ire = Past marker, -r- = Perfective aspect, bedo = Stable participle "to have been"

3. Subject Concord Patterns

English Ekegusii
I had been Inche nabeire
You had been Aye kwabeire
He/She had been Ere obeire
We had been Intwe twabeire
You (pl.) had been Inwe mwabeire
They had been Barabwo babeire

4. Semantic Features

  • Marks an action or state completed prior to a past reference point.
  • Often introduces contextual or background information in storytelling.

5. Examples

  • Nabeire ngoganyete – I had been waiting for you
  • Yabeire kegotwa – It had been raining
  • Babeire bagotarerana barabwo ase barabwo – They had been visiting one another
  • Syliviah obeire okomotarera ere – Syliviah had been visiting her

6. Linguistic Characteristics

  • beire is non-conjugatable, static in form.
  • -r- acts like English "have" – indicates completion.
  • ire fixes the action to the past.

7. Pedagogical Insights

  • Emphasize strict structure: -ire + subject + r + beire
  • Avoid misuse of bedo (no standalone conjugation).
  • Practice with temporal clauses (e.g., ko...) for narrative depth.
  • Differentiate:
    • -beire (has been – present perfect)
    • –ire beire (had been – past perfect)

A. Future Tense in Ekegusii

General Overview

  • Future tense is formed using auxiliary verbs, primarily "ngochi" ("to come").
  • Most common future auxiliary: "ngochande" ("will come").
  • The main verb remains in the infinitive, uninflected.
  • Semantically equivalent to English "will" or "shall".

Immediate Future Structure

Formula: Subject Prefix + ngochi + Infinitive Verb

  • Ngochi koragerande → "I will eat"
  • Ngochi koragera ore → "You will eat"

Contracted Future Form

Spoken Ekegusii contracts ngochi to bo:

  • Ngochi koragera'nde (I'll eat), ngochi koragera'ore (He'll eat)

Conjugation of "Ngochi"

Subject prefixes: i- (I), a- (you sg.), e- (he/she), int- (we), in- (you pl.), ba- (they)

e.g., Ngochi koragera bare → "They will eat"

Future "To Be" with "koba"

Ngochi = to be (remains uninflected).

Structure: Subject Prefix + Ngochande + Ngochi

Ngochinde koba n'abana babere → "I will have two children"

Degrees of Futurity

  • Immediate: Nabeire ang'e koirana → "I'm about to return"
  • Near Future: Kare reo ngoiranande → "Later today I'll return"
  • Tomorrow: Rero ngoiranade → "Tomorrow I'll return"
  • Indefinite: Mosuko ngoiranande → "Sometime I'll return"

Future Continuous Tense

Structure: Ngochi + ko + Verb

  • Ngochi kogenda inde komotarera ere → "I will be going to visit him/her"
  • Ngochi koragera bare buya → "They will be eating well"

Obligation with "Goika"

Goika = must/should

  • Goika oragere → "You must eat"
  • Goika tomorore → "We must see him"

B. Other Tense and Modal Constructions

"Should" Construction with "Nabo"

Expresses moral/expected obligation.

Structure: Nabo + Subject Prefix + Subjunctive Verb (drop final -o)

  • Nabo ngorika → "I should write"
  • Ngorikare (contracted)

"May" Construction with "Nenyarekane"

Expresses possibility or permission.

Structure: Nenyarekane + Subject Prefix + Full Verb

  • Nenyarekane indike → "I may write"
  • Nenyarekan'indike → "We may write"

"Would" Construction with "Nanga"

Indicates hypothetical/conditional past actions.

Structure: Nanga + Subject Prefix + Verb (with -o)

  • Nanga inarika → "I would write"
  • Nanga'narika (contracted)

Subjunctive Imperatives ("Let Me/Us")

Structure: Subject Prefix + Verb (subjunctive)

  • Tiga indike! → "Let me write!"
  • Tiga torike! → "Let us write!"

"Should Have" Past Obligation

Structure: Nabo + nanga + Subject + Subjunctive Verb

  • Nabo nanga narigete → "I should have written"
  • Nabo nang'arigete (contracted)

Past Perfect ("Had Done")

Structure: Nabo + Subject Perfective Marker + Verb

  • Nabo narigete → "I had written"
  • Nabo'rigete (contracted)

Causative Construction

Structure: Subject Prefix + namokorire (make) + Object + Verb

  • Namokorete arike → "I make him write"
  • Twagokorete orike → "We make you write"
  • Timokoreti arike→ "I don't make him write"

C. Morphosyntactic and Pedagogical Notes

Infinitive Usage

Main verb in future/modal constructions stays uninflected.

Subject-Verb Agreement

Agreement marked on auxiliaries (ngochi, goika, Nango), not main verb.

Subjunctive Mood

Formed by dropping final -o

Contractions

Common in speech/writing; maintain grammatical integrity

Temporal Markers

e.g., ninche, mambia, mosuko, kare help indicate futurity.

Teaching Recommendations

  • Use contrastive drills: full vs. contracted forms.
  • Emphasize subjunctive transformations.
  • Highlight modal distinctions: may vs. should vs. would.
  • Reinforce causative structures with object marking.
