3.1.4 Adjectives & their Plural

Plural Adjectives in Ekegusii

In Ekegusii, adjectives exhibit inflectional morphology that agrees with the number of the noun they modify. Singular and plural forms of adjectives must align with their respective noun forms in both syntax and morphology. Below is a detailed analysis of plural adjective formation and use:

1. General Rule of Pluralization

  • Ekegusii adjectives generally change form when modifying plural nouns.
  • Most plural adjectives end in -o (e.g., Ekiya → Ebiya, Ekenene → Ebinene).
  • Both the noun and the adjective must be inflected for plurality.

2. Morphological Transformation

Singular Form Plural Form Meaning
Ekiya Ebiya good, beautiful
Ekebe Ebibe bad
Ekenene Ebinene big
Egeke Ebike small, few
Egetambe Ebitambe tall, long
Ekieng'e Ebieng'e short
Ekemwamu Ebimwamu black
Ekerabu Ebirabu white
Ekenge Ebinge many (plural only form)

3. Concordial Agreement in Plural Forms

  • When a noun becomes plural, its modifying adjective must also change into the plural form for grammatical and semantic agreement.

Examples:

  • Ekerogo ekenene— "A big chair" → Ebirogo ebinene — "Big chairs"
  • Omoiseke omonyakieni — "A beautiful girl" → Abaiseke abanyakieni — "Beautiful girls"
  • Ekerandi egeke — "A small gourd" → Ebirandi ebike — "Small gourds"

4. Adjectives That Do Not Change in the Plural

Certain adjectives in Ekegusii remain invariant regardless of the singular or plural status of the noun they describe. This is typically the case with adjectives derived from color roots and certain descriptive qualities.

Examples:

  • Enyange embariri — "A red bicycle" → Chinyange chimbariri — "Red bicycles"
  • Omworokia omwenenu — "A cruel teacher" → Abaorokia abaenenu — "Cruel teachers"
  • Egesukari— "Sweet banana" → Ebisukari— "Sweet bananas"

5. Semantic Categories of Adjective Agreement

A. Adjectives That Inflect for Plurality

These adjectives follow standard agreement patterns:

  • Ekiya → ebiya
  • Ekebe → ebiya
  • Egeke → ebike
  • Entambe → ebitambe
  • Ekieng'e → ebieng'e

B. Adjectives That Do Not Inflect

These adjectives remain invariant and typically describe:

  • Coloration (especially in fossilized or culturally rooted forms): makuar
  • Taste/Emotion: enyansu (sweet), ekeroro (bitter), omwenenu (cruel)

6. Notes on Usage and Syntax

  • Adjective–noun agreement is mandatory in standard Ekegusii grammar, especially in formal or written usage.
  • This rule reinforces number agreement, clarity, and euphony within noun phrases.
  • The noun changes morphologically (e.g., Ekerogo → Ebirogo, Omoiseke → Abaiseke) alongside its adjective.

8. Linguistic Implication

  • The system of plural adjective agreement in Ekegusii mirrors the syntactic richness found in many Congo Basin languages.
  • It reveals a productive morphological strategy where both prefixation and suffixation are used to maintain grammatical consistency.

The invariant forms demonstrate a lexicalized resistance to inflection, often tied to cultural, emotional, or sensory descriptors.
