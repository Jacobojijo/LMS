3.8.4 Past Tense

1. Simple Past Tense

1.1. Definition and Function

Expresses actions that were completed in the past.

Indicates an event that occurred and ended before the time of speaking.

The auxiliary particle "-ete" (abbreviated as "te") is prefixed to the subject-verb structure.

1.2. Structure

Subject + -ete + Verb + Object/Predicate

1.3. Examples

Wayne nachiete korisia – Wayne went to graze cattle

Inche nariete enswe – I ate fish

Inche inanyorete emeremo – I found work

Baba nagorete esoda as'abakori emeremo – Mother bought soda for the workers

Omworokia agogete ekero abana bairaneretie amabori– The teacher was happy when the children answered questions

Mary narugete obokima – Mary cooked ugali

Kerubo nachiete komorora ng'ina moke – Adoyo went to see her aunt

1.4. Word Order Sensitivity

Subject and object placement significantly affect meaning.

Example:

Ere namonyorete – He/she met him/her

Aye ngwamonyorete– You met us

Barabwo mbabanyorete– They met them

2. Contracted Past Tense Forms

2.1. Verb Conjugation with "te"

Common verbs combine "te" with the subject prefix for compact expression.

English Ekegusii Contracted Form
I wrote Inche naarigete Naarigete
You wrote Aye ngwarigete Ngwarigete
He/She wrote Ere narigete Narigete
We wrote Intwe ntwarigete Ntwarigete
You (pl.) wrote Inwe mwarigete Mwarigete
They wrote Barabwo mbarigete Mbarigete

3. Immediate Past: 'n-' Prefixation

3.1. Definition

The prefix "n-" marks a just-completed action, often indicating recentness or immediacy.

3.2. Example

Narure korema – I have just dug (but now stopped)

4. Yesterday Past Tense

4.1. Function

Indicates an action that took place specifically yesterday.

4.2. Example

Inche narwarete igoro – I was sick yesterday

5. Extended Past Tense Markers

Marker Meaning Example Translation
Aerire Two days ago Inche narwarete amatuko abere aerire I was sick two days ago
Bwango iga Recently Inche narwarete bwango iga I was sick recently
i- Just now Inche inarwarete ko nabwenire I was just sick (but recovered)

6. Recent Past Perfective – '-a-' Infix

6.1. Structure

Subject prefix + -a- + verb root

6.2. Function

Marks an action that has just been completed (equivalent to English present perfect).

6.3. Examples

English Ekegusii
I have eaten Inche narageire
You have eaten Aye kwarageire
He/she has eaten Ere orageire
We have eaten Intwe twarageire
You (pl.) have eaten Inwe mwarageire
They have eaten Barabwo barageire

7. Past Continuous / Habitual Tense

7.1. Definition

Describes ongoing or repeated actions in the past.

Combines "-ete" with habitual suffix "-o" to express repetition or continuity in the past.

7.2. Structure

-Ete + Subject Prefix + Verb + -o

7.3. Examples

Inche ngoragera nare – I was eating (habitually or during that time)

Nanarete gosoma Ekegusii inka– I used to study Dholuo at home

Mogaka anarete gotengera ogotenga – Mogaka used to like attending dances

Inche nanarete gokwana Ekegusii buya– I used to speak Dholuo well

Kare abaiseke ebanarete komenya ase echikoni – Long ago, girls used to sleep in the siwindhe (girls' hut)

Magokoro ominto anchete esoda – My grandfather used to like soda

8. Morphological Observations

-Ete is an auxiliary that does not inflect but co-occurs with the verb for past meaning.

Subject-verb agreement still applies even when "-ete" is used.

Ekegusii expresses past events via a combination of pre-verbal particles, inflectional prefixes, and suffixes (agglutinative morphology).

Tense interpretation is heavily reliant on prefixes, suffixes, and adverbial particles.

9. Pedagogical Implications for Learners

Learners must distinguish between:

  • Plain past (-ete + verb)
  • Recent or immediate past (n- prefix)
  • Yesterday/timed pasts (-na)
  • Habitual past (-ete + verb + -o)
  • Perfective past (-a- infix)

Emphasis on mastering word order and understanding tense based on particle usage and morphological integration.

Learners should practice interpreting subtle shifts in aspect using minimal morphological cues.
