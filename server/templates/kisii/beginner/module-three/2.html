3.1.2 Adjective of Quality and Quantity

1. Adjectives of Quality in Ekegusii

These adjectives express inherent characteristics, aesthetic features, or moral evaluations of nouns. They often carry strong cultural and semantic nuance, particularly in value-laden or evaluative contexts.

1.1 Omuya, Omonyakieni

  • Gloss: good, beautiful, nice, attractive
  • Morphological Structure: omo- (adjectival prefix) + muya (root for goodness/beauty)
  • Expanded Meaning: Carries both moral and aesthetic value; may imply physical attractiveness, ethical uprightness, or social approval.
  • Example: Omoiseke omonyakieni — "Beautiful girl" Rikori rigiyar — "Good path" Mbuya mono — "Very good" or "Extremely beautiful"

1.2 Ekebe, egetioku

  • Gloss: bad, ugly, loathsome, undesirable
  • Morphological Structure: e- + kebe (root for badness)
  • Expanded Meaning: Indicates negative character, undesirable appearance, or moral failing. The form ekebe adds intensification.
  • Example: Omonte omobe — "A bad person" Ekerengererio ekebe mono— "A very bad thought"

1.3 'Taiyekeri, teratoba/ Amabese

  • Gloss: uncooked, unripe
  • Note: Does not follow the omo- prefix pattern; appears as an adjective inherently.
  • Expanded Meaning: Describes incomplete natural or culinary states. It may apply to both food and natural development.
  • Example: Enswe etaiyekeri — "Uncooked fish" Amapera amabese— "Unripe guava"

2. Adjectives of Quantity in Ekegusii

These adjectives specify amount, extent, or degree in terms of count or mass. They are crucial for quantification, comparison, and measuring sufficiency.

2.1 Ekenene

  • Gloss: big
  • Morphological Structure: eke- + nene' (large)
  • Semantic Note: Refers to size, but can also metaphorically indicate importance.
  • Example: Eng'ondi enene — "Big sheep" Omonto bw'engencho — "Important person"

2.2 Ebinge

  • Gloss: many, a lot
  • Morphological Structure: e- + binge (many/much)
  • Usage: Plural quantifier; used with count nouns.
  • Example: Abanto abange — "Many people" Ebitabu ebinge — "Many books"

2.3 Ebike, enke

  • Gloss: few, small, little
  • Morphological Structures:
    • e- + bike (few) = ebike
    • enke is irregular and does not follow standard prefixation
  • Usage: Describes small quantity or number
  • Examples: Emori enke — "Small calf" Amabere amake — "A little milk"

2.4 Bisaine

  • Gloss: enough
  • Morphological Structure: bi- + saine (suffice)
    • Spoken usage often drops the prefix, yielding bisaine
  • Semantic Use: Indicates sufficiency
  • Example: Amache aisaine — "Enough water" Amabere amange — "Sufficient milk"

2.5 Aichire, Aisaine

  • Gloss: numerous, abundant, many
  • Morphological Structure: e- + binge (abundance)
  • Semantic Emphasis: Stronger than mang'eny; implies overflowing or excess
  • Example: Ebiasi ebinge — "Numerous potatoes" Abanto abange — "A large multitude"

2.6 Ase'obonge

  • Gloss: plenty
  • Morphological Structure: o- + bonge (plentiful)
    • Like obonge, the prefix is often dropped: ebinge
  • Semantic Usage: Highlights richness or ample provision
  • Example: Amabere as'obonge — "Plenty of milk" Erongori enyinge — "A lot of porridge"

3. Interrogative Quantifiers in Ekegusii

These forms elicit quantitative information, either in terms of number or dimension.

3.1 Mbirenga?

  • Gloss: How many?
  • Usage: Seeks a numerical response.
  • Example: Ebitabu mbirenga? — "How many books?"

3.2 Ebinene ang'ana?

  • Gloss: How big?
  • Usage: Measures size or extent.
  • Example: Ekerogo ekenene ang'ana? — "How big is the chair?"
