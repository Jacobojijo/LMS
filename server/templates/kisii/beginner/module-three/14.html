3.7.1 Summary of Verbs

Transitive Verbs in Ekegusii

1. Nature of Transitive Verbs in Ekegusii

  • Transitive verbs require a direct object to complete their meaning.
  • These verbs direct an action toward an entity (person or object).
  • Most verbs in Ekegusii can function as both transitive and intransitive, depending on syntactic context.
  • Antipassivity is used to shift focus from the object, effectively turning a transitive verb into an intransitive form.
  • Verbal extensions such as applicative and locative can add arguments, allowing intransitive verbs to become transitive.

2. Syntactic and Morphological Properties

  • Verb-Initial Syntax: Ekegusii exhibits a verb-initial word order, although nouns and subjects may precede for emphasis or clarity.
  • Agglutinative Structure: Verb forms integrate subject (prefix) and object (suffix) into a single morphological unit.
  • Conjugation Principle:
    • Prefix marks the subject.
    • Suffix marks the object.
    • The verb root remains central and consistent.
Example (Verb "Goaka" – to beat):
  • Inche namwakire = Inche-namwaki-re → "I have beaten him/her"
  • Ere Omwakire = Ere-omwaki-re → "He has beaten him/her"
  • Olum ngomoakare = Subject noun + verb-object compound → "Olum is beating him/her"

3. Subjective Pronouns in Ekegusii

  • Denote the agent (doer) of the action.
  • Always appear as prefixes attached to the verb root.
  • Function independently in non-conjugated contexts or as auxiliaries.
  • Forms: Inche (I), aye (you sg), ere (he/she/it), intwe (we), inwe (you pl), barabwo(they)
Example (Intransitive Verb):

Intwe-togotera → "We sing"

4. Objective Pronouns in Ekegusii

  • Indicate the recipient of an action.
  • Always appear as suffixes attached to the verb root.
  • Format: Subject Prefix + Verb Root + Object Suffix
  • Forms: -i (me), -a (you sg), -e (him/her/it), -I (us), -in (you pl), -ba (them)
Example:

Mosomi → "Teach him/her/it"

5. Conjugation Case Studies

Verb "ita" – to kill:
  • Batoitire → "They have killed us"
  • Ere obaitire → "He has killed them"
  • Aye kwang'itire → "You have killed me"
Verb "Ancha" – to love:
  • Ningwanchete → "I love you"
  • Barabwo mbabanchete→ "They love you (pl)"
Verb "Nyuoma" – to marry:
  • Ngogonyuoma inde → "I am marrying you"
  • Mbanyuomete → "They are married"
  • Ere namonyuomete → "He married her"

6. Syntax of Transitive Verb Conjugation

  • Canonical word order: Subject Pronoun + Verb Root + Object Pronoun
  • Verbs are fully fused (single unit encodes full clause: subject, verb, and object).
  • Reflects synthetic morphological typology with high grammatical density.

7. Pedagogical and Linguistic Implications

Mastery of subjective and objective pronouns is essential for:

  • Accurate verb conjugation
  • Sentence clarity and interpretation
  • Listening comprehension and fluent communication

The prefix-suffix verb morphology enhances:

  • Linguistic efficiency
  • Compact encoding of syntactic roles
  • Deep integration of grammar with meaning

Intransitive Verbs in Ekegusii

Definition:

Intransitive verbs do not take a direct object; the action terminates with the subject itself.

Characteristics:

  • Stand independently without requiring another noun.
  • Emphasize the subject's performance of an action without transferring the action to an object.

Examples of Common Intransitive Verbs:

Dholuo Verb English Translation
Gokora emeremo To work
Gotera To sing
Korara To sleep
Gotenga To dance
Kominyoka To run
Goisaransa To sit

Illustrative Sentences:

  • Inche ngoisaranse ase ekerogo – I sit on the chair.
  • Barabwo nabakong'u as'emeremo – They work hard.

2. The Verb "To Have" in Ekegusii

A. Possession with "-bwate" (with)

Structural Construction:

  • Expressed as: Personal Pronoun + -bwate (meaning "with")
  • Indicates possession, analogous to "to have" in English.

Conjugation Table:

Pronoun Dholuo Form English Translation
I Inche nimbwate I have
You (sg.) Aye nobwate You have
He/She Ere nabwate He/She has
We Intwe ntobwate We have
You (pl.) Inwe imobwate You (pl.) have
They Barabwo mbabate They have

Examples:

  • Inche nimbwate ekerogo – I have a chair.
  • Barabwo mbabate omogondo – They have land.
  • Ere nabwate echikoni – He/She has a hut.
  • Entwe ntobwate esese – We have a dog.

Important Note:

  • Inche n'esese literally means I and the dog, and is not appropriate for expressing possession.
  • Use -bwate construction for correct possessive statements.

3. The Verb "To Have" as an Auxiliary Verb – re

A. Imperfect Past Tense Use

Functionality:

  • Denotes completion of an action in the recent past (perfect aspect).
  • Conveys the equivalent of the English construction "have + past participle".

Form and Use:

  • Se is incorporated as a prefix in compound verbs.
  • The prefix is sensitive to subject and tense agreement.

Examples:

Ekegusii Form English Translation
Inche naetire amatemu I have passed the exam
Naachire I have gone
Narageire I have eaten
Ere orarire He/She has slept
Intwe twamenyire We have stayed
Inwe mwachire Kisumu You have gone to Kisumu
Ere nanyuomire She is married (gone to cook)
Ere okure He/She/It has died

4. Reflexive Verbs in Ekegusii

A. Morphological Structure

  • Formed by attaching the suffix -re to the base infinitive verb.
  • Denotes self-directed actions or mutual actions among subjects.

B. Infinitive vs Reflexive Form Examples:

Base Verb Meaning Reflexive Form Reflexive Meaning
Ruga To cook -rugire To cook itself
Rora To see -rorire To see oneself/each other
Somi To teach -somire To learn (teach oneself)
Gacha To keep -gachire To be kept

C. Conjugation Table of Puonjore (to learn):

Subject Ekegusii Form English Translation
I Inche ngosomande I am learning (myself)
You (sg.) Aye ngosomore You are learning
He/She Ere ngosomare He/She is learning
We Intwe ngosomatore We are learning
You (pl.) Inwe ngosoma more You (pl.) are learning
They Barabwo ngosoma bare They are learning

5. Reflexive Verbs as Nouns – -esibi Nominalization

  • The suffix -ruok nominalizes reflexive verbs into abstract nouns or verbal nouns.
  • Used to express states or concepts derived from reflexive action.

Examples:

Action Ekegusii English Translation
Washing oneself Gwesibi n'okuya Washing oneself is good
Not washing Tatageti gwesibi He does not want to wash himself
Fighting each other Korwana inwe ase inwe mbobe Fighting one another is bad
Refusing to fight Mbatageti korwana barabwo ase barabwo They do not want to fight each other

6. Additional Linguistic Notes

Cultural Semantics:

The verb nyuoma (to marry) is culturally male-exclusive in Ekegusii usage; only men are grammatically positioned as the marrying subject.

Absence of Object Markers:

  • Ekegusii does not use separate object pronouns.
  • Subject prefixes (e.g., Inche, aye, ere) imply both the actor and the action's directionality.

Grammatical Weight of Subject Prefixes:

  • Subject prefixes serve as both pronouns and functional markers in verb constructions.
  • They carry tense, aspect, and subject agreement features.

Morphological Compactness:

  • Possessive constructions such as Inche nimbwate ekerogo are functionally and orthographically treated as units.
  • These are syntactically fused and should be interpreted holistically.
