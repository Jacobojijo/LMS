2.7.1 Interrogative Pronoun

Interrogative pronouns are pronouns used to introduce questions, replacing nouns when the identity of the noun is unknown. In Ekegusii, the main interrogative pronouns are "what," "which," "who," "whom," and "whose". These pronouns function as question words, replacing nouns or noun phrases in a sentence to seek specific information

1. Basic Interrogative Pronouns:

How –Inaki?
Used to inquire about the manner or process of something.
Example:

Inaki ore? — How are you going?

Why –Naseki?
Used to ask for reasons or causes.
Example:

Naseki okoanga — Why do you refuse?

How many –Nirenga?
Used to inquire about quantity, especially in the case of countable nouns.
Example:

Abanto mbarenga bare? — How many people are they?

When –nindi?
Used to ask about time or when an event is happening.
Example:

Nindi orache — When will you come?

Who –Ningo?
Used to ask about a person or people, identifying an individual.
Example:

Ning'o omboretie — Who asked for me?

Ning'o— Who?

What –Ninki/Kereki
Used to inquire about things or actions.
Example:

Ninki eke? — What is this?

2. Common Questions: Present Tense

These questions are used to inquire about the current action of a person or group.

  • Are you going? –Ngogend'ore?
  • Is she/he going? –Ngogend'are?
  • Are you (pl) going? –Ngogend'ore
  • Are we going? –Ngogenda tore?
  • Are they going? –Ngogenda bare?
  • Are you coming? –Ngochore?

Example of more meaning using "ekeene" (for emphasis):

Ekeene ngochore? — Are you really going?
Ekeene ngochare? — Is she/he really going?

3. Tag Questions (Expressing Agreement or Disagreement):

Tag questions are used to confirm or deny a statement. The structure involves asking a question and adding a tag at the end to expect confirmation or disagreement.

Ngogenda ore, igo? — Are you going, aren't you?
Ngogenda are, igo? — Is she/he going, Isn't she/he?
Ngogenda bare,igo? — Are they going, aren't they?
Nobamanyete,igo? — Do you know them, don't you?
Igo tori kogenda? — Aren't you going?
Tamanyeti gokwana,igo? — Doesn't he/she speak, does he/she?

4. Specific Question Examples (Exploring deeper meanings of questions)

Ning'o otagete?
Who is willing?
Asking about a person's willingness or readiness to do something.
Ninki kiagerete ngwacha?
Why did you fail to come?
This question seeks to understand the reason behind not attending an event.

5. Location-related Questions (Where?):

Where? – Ngai?
A general question asking the location of something.

Ngai omoyio ore— Where is the knife?
Omoyio ngai ore? — Where is the knife?
Both are variations of the same question, asking about the location of the knife, with slight differences in phrasing.
Ngai ekerogo kere? — Where is the chair?
Ngai ekerogo kebererete? — Where is the chair located?
The difference here is in the use of "Kebererete", which emphasizes the exact location.

6. Important Notes:

  • Ekeene is used to add emphasis in questions (like "really" or "are you sure?").
  • The use of "Igo" is crucial for creating tag questions in Ekegusii, making the question more conversational and inviting confirmation or contradiction.
  • Nindi and Ndireri? are key examples of using "When" in both immediate and future contexts.
