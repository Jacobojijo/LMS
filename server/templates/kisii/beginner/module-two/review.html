2.6.1 Summary of Pronouns (Part 01)

Demonstrative Pronouns (Ekegusii)

Definition:

Pronouns used to point to or refer to specific nouns, indicating proximity (near or far).

Singular Demonstratives:

  • Eke → This (near the speaker)
Example: Eke negetanda → This is a bed.
  • Ekio → That (near the listener)
Example: Eria n'egari yane → That is my chair.
  • Keria → That over there (far from both speaker and listener)
Example: Oria n'omorisia → That is a herdsman over there.

Plural Demonstratives:

  • Aba → These (near the speaker)
Example: Aba nabamura → These are boys.
  • Ebio → Those (near the listener)
Example: Ebio mbiribi? → What are those?
  • Baria → Those (far from both)
Example: Baria nabarisia → Those are herdsmen.

Demonstratives in Questions:

  • Eke ninki? → What is this?
  • Ebio mbiribi? → What are those?
  • Ekio ninki? → What is that (far over there)?

Demonstratives with Nouns:

Used before or after nouns to specify them:

  • Eye ne'nka → This is a home.
  • Eye nechikoni yane → This is my hat.

Demonstratives + Possessive Pronouns:

Combines with possessive pronouns for specificity:

  • Eke nekerogo kiane → This is my chair.
  • Echi nechiombe chiago → Those are your cows.

Specific Demonstrative Nouns:

  • Omote oyo → This tree
  • Omomura oyo → This boy
  • Omorwani oyo → This fighter
  • Endani eye → This pond

Demonstratives Indicating Place:

  • Igaa → Here
  • Ase aya → This place
  • Ase aria → That place (far)
Examples: Enyomba eye → This house, Enchera eye → This path.

Demonstratives Indicating Time:

  • Bono → Now
  • Rero → Today
  • Igoro → Yesterday
  • Mambia → Tomorrow

Demonstratives for Speed/Action:

  • Bwango → Quickly
  • Ng'ora → Slowly

Distributive Pronouns

Definition:

Pronouns that refer to individuals within a group, emphasizing each member separately.

"Each One" (Person):

Kera Omonto → Each person individually

Example: Kera omonto ngochia koragera are → Each one will eat.

"Each One" (Thing):

Kera egento → Each item individually

Example: Kera egento ngebwate engencho yaye → Each thing has its meaning.

"Any One" (Person):

Onde bwensi → Any one person (unspecified)

Example: Omonto onde bwensi nabo akogenda isiko → Anyone can be a winner.

"Any One" (Thing):

Kende gionsi → Any one thing (random)

Example: Bea kende gionsi ase emesa → Put any one on the table.

"All":

Bionsi/ Bonsi → All (emphatic form)

Example: Namaene endagera yonsi eyarietu → Absolutely all the food was eaten.

"Anything":

Kende gionsi → Anything, open-ended

Example: Reta kende gionsi oranche → Bring anything you like.

"Something":

gento gete → Something (non-specific)

Example: Inaigwete egeto gente ime ase enyomba → I heard something in that house.

Introduction to Ekeusii Pronouns

Definition of Pronouns:

Pronouns replace nouns to simplify speech and avoid repetition.

Types of Pronouns in Ekegusii:

  • Personal Pronouns: Refer to specific people or things (e.g., I, you, he).
  • Possessive Pronouns: Indicate ownership (e.g., mine, yours).
  • Demonstrative Pronouns: Point to specific things (e.g., this, that).
  • Interrogative Pronouns: Used for asking questions (e.g., who, what, which).
  • Relative Pronouns: Link clauses and refer to previously mentioned nouns.

Personal Pronouns (Subject Form):

  • Inche = I
  • Aye = You (singular)
  • Baria = He/She/It (gender-neutral)
  • intwe = We
  • Inwe = You (plural)
  • Barabwo = They

Object and Possessive Pronouns:

  • Often implied or context-driven.
  • Can be formed using suffixes or word placement.

Pronoun Use in Sentences:

  • Subject pronouns are typically included in verbs (e.g., Aye genda = "You go").
  • Emphasis can be added by explicitly stating the pronoun before the verb (e.g., Inche ngochande = "I will come").

Interrogative Pronouns (For asking questions):

  • Ning'o = Who
  • Inaki = What
  • Naseki = Why
  • Ndireri = When
  • Arari = Where

Personal Pronouns in Ekegusii

What Are Personal Pronouns:

  • Personal pronouns represent specific people or groups.
  • Often used in Ekegusii, but can be dropped if context or verb form makes the subject clear.

Demonstratives + Pronouns:

Combine demonstratives with personal pronouns to specify things or people.

  • Inche ninche = I, this (emphasizing "I am the one")
  • Ooria nere = He/She is that one (far)
  • Aba mbarabwo = They are that/those ones (near you)

Simple Sentence Structure:

Basic structure: Subject Pronoun + Verb + (Location/Object/Demonstrative)

Examples:
  • Inche ngogenda inka → I am going home.
  • Aye gwachire bono → You are coming now.
  • Barabwo nabo bakoruga → He/She can cook.
  • Twaisaransire igaa → We are sitting here.
  • Inwe mwensi karwe ase'aywe → You (all) leave that place.
  • Barabwo ngosoa bare nyomba → They are entering the house.

Possessive Pronouns and Cases in Ekegusii

Overview:

  • Possession is indicated by adding a suffix to the noun.
  • Changes depend on:
    • Person (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
    • Number (singular/plural)
  • "one" (prefix) = "of/belonging to"

Singular Possessive Forms:

First Person Singular (I): Suffix -one or -ane

  • Omwana one = my child
  • Egeticha nekiane = the garden is mine

Second Person Singular (You): Suffix -ogo or -ago

  • Omwana ogo = your child
  • Egeticha nekiago = the garden is yours

Third Person Singular (He/She/It): Suffix -oye or -aye

  • Omwana oye = his/her child
  • Egeticha nekiaye = the garden is his/hers/its

Plural Possessive Forms:

First Person Plural (We): Suffix –ito, -ato (inclusive/exclusive)

  • Omwana oito = our child
  • Egeticha negiato = the garden is ours Egeticha negiato

Second Person Plural (You all): Suffix –u -ino

  • Omwana oino = your (plural) child
  • Egeticha nekiaino = the garden is yours (plural)

Third Person Plural (They): Suffix –obo, -abo

  • Omwana obo = their child
  • Egeticha kiabo = the garden is theirs

Use of "-ne":

"-ne/-no" = shows possession ("of/belonging to").

Examples:
  • Omwano bw'odhiambo = Adhiambo's child
  • One = mine
  • Oino = yours (singular)
  • ye = his/hers
  • oito = ours
  • Baino = yours (plural)
  • Babo = theirs

Real-Life Sentences (using "Egeticha" – Garden):

  • Egeticha nekiane → The garden is mine
  • Egeticha nekiano → The garden is yours (singular)
  • Egeticha nekiaye → The garden is his/hers/its
  • Egeticha negiato → The garden is ours
  • Egeticha nekiabo → The garden is yours (plural)
  • Egeticha nekiabo → The garden is theirs

Important Notes:

  • Gender Neutrality: The third-person singular form (-oye or -aye) is gender-neutral.
  • Flexibility: Choose between suffixes (e.g., omwana) or "one" + pronoun depending on clarity and emphasis.
  • Context: Some suffixes appear in both singular and plural forms. Context and verb agreement help distinguish them.
