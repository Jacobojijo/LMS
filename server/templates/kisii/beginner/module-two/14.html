2.5.3 Possessive Pronouns and Cases

1. Overview of Possessive Pronouns in Ekegusii

  • Possession in Ekegusii is shown by adding a suffix to the noun.
  • These suffixes change depending on:
    • ✅ The person (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
    • ✅ The number (singular/plural)
  • You may also use "oyo" to express "of/belonging to," like Oyomoraa = "Adhiambo's"

2. Singular Possessive Forms

✅ First Person Singular (I)

  • Suffixes: -one or -bane
  • Examples:
    • Omwana one = my child
    • Egeticha nekiane = the garden is mine

✅ Second Person Singular (You)

  • Suffixes: -ogo or bago
  • Examples:
    • Omwana ogo = your child
    • Egeticha nekiago = the garden is yours

✅ Third Person Singular (He/She/It)

  • Suffix: -oye or -aye
  • Gender-neutral
  • Examples:
    • Omwana oye = his/her child
    • Egeticha nekiaye = the garden is his/hers/its

3. Plural Possessive Forms

✅ First Person Plural (We)

  • Suffix: -ito (used for both inclusive & exclusive)
  • Examples:
    • Omwana oito = our child
    • Egeticha negiaito = the garden is ours

✅ Second Person Plural (You all)

  • Suffix: -ino
  • Examples:
    • Omwana oino = your (plural) child
    • Egeticha nekiaino = the garden is yours (plural)

✅ Third Person Plural (They)

  • Suffix: -bo
  • Examples:
    • Omwana obo = their child
    • Abana babo = the garden is theirs

4. Use of "Mar" – Expressing Belonging/Ownership

The prefix "oyo" is used with names and pronouns to emphasize possession.

Works like "of" or "belonging to" in English.

Phrase Meaning
Omwana Bw'omorisia Elder's child
oyone mine
oyogo yours (singular)
oyoye his/hers
oyoito ours
oyoino yours (plural)
oyobo theirs

5. Real-life Sentences Using "Egeticha" (Garden)

Sentence Translation
Egeticha nekiane The garden is mine
Egeticha nekiago The garden is yours (singular)
Egeticha nekiaye The garden is his/hers/its
Egeticha negiaito The garden is ours
Egeticha nekiaino The garden is yours (plural)
Egeticha nekiabo The garden is theirs

6. Important Notes

  • Gender Neutrality: Third-person singular form does not change for male or female.
  • Flexibility: Speakers may choose between suffixes (like omwana one) or "One" + pronoun (like oito) depending on emphasis or clarity.
  • Context Matters: Some suffixes (like -bo) appear in both first person singular and plural. Context and verb agreement help distinguish them.
