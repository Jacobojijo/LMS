2.3.3 Gusii Traditional Foods and Vegetables

1. Key Gusii Traditional Dishes

  • Oboba/Amandegere – Wild mushroom harvested during rainy seasons (e.g., Oboba - large, Amandegere - small).
  • Enyama eigekeire – Traditional smoked or sun-dried beef, preserved without refrigeration.
  • Risosa – Tender pumpkin leaves (It susa) cooked with groundnut paste or milk.
  • Omena – Silver cyprinid (small dried fish), a staple source of protein eaten fried or boiled.
  • Ekerimu – Rich traditional ghee, used for flavoring kuon (ugali) and stews.
  • Erongori – Fermented or fresh porridge, often made from millet, sorghum, or maize flour.
  • Egesare – Cowpeas leaves, highly nutritious and commonly prepared (stir-fried).
  • Richore – Wild pheasant, hunted and traditionally roasted.
  • Chinkware – Quail, valued for their meat and sometimes farmed today.
  • Chinsaga – Spider plant leaves, either boiled or stir-fried.
  • Kamongo – Marbled lungfish, cooked in stews or smoked for preservation.
  • Enderema – Amaranth greens, cooked simply to retain nutrients.
  • Rinagu – Black nightshade (Osuga), slightly bitter, often cooked with milk or butter.
  • Engoko – Chicken, commonly roasted or boiled with native spices.
  • Enyama – Beef from cattle, roasted (nyama choma) or stewed.
  • Engonye – Mudfish, an important protein, often smoked or boiled.
  • Emboga – Jute mallow, mucilaginous when cooked, very nutritious.
  • Omuogo – Cassava tubers, either boiled or dried and ground into flour.

2. Traditional Fruits (Amatunda)

  • Ripopo – Pawpaw (papaya), eaten ripe or used in juice.
  • Ritunda – Orange, consumed fresh or juiced.
  • Amaembe – Mango, either eaten fresh or dried.
  • Ritoke – Banana, eaten ripe or cooked (plantain style).
  • Rinanasi – Pineapple, enjoyed fresh or juiced.
  • Abukato – Creamy fruit often eaten with ugali or as a snack.

3. Typical Gusii Food Staples

  • Ribuoni – Potato, boiled, fried, or mashed.
  • Enduma – Yam, traditionally roasted or boiled.
  • Omwogo – Cassava (Mariwa), a major carbohydrate source.
  • Erongori – Porridge made from different grains.
  • Obokima – Ugali, stiff porridge made mainly from maize flour.

Traditional Gusii Vegetables: Categories and Preparation Styles

1. Domesticated Traditional Vegetables

  • Chinsaga – Spider plant; cooked "aboka" (fermented) or "aruda" (stirred).
  • Rinaga – Black nightshade; essential vegetable, lightly boiled or cooked with milk.
  • Omonyenye – Slender leaf; preferred mixed with other vegetables due to slight bitterness.
  • Egesare – Cowpea leaves; stir-fried "aruda" style, very rich in iron and fiber.

2. Domesticated but Rarely Consumed for Leaves (Mainly for Other Parts)

  • Amato y'eyarare – Chilli leaves; very spicy, rarely used in cooking.
  • Amato yechinyanya – Tomato leaves; used cautiously due to mild toxicity.
  • Amato yebiasi – Potato leaves; cooked "aruda" or "aboka."
  • Amato y'omwogo – Cassava leaves; edible after proper preparation to remove toxins.
  • Amasosa – Pumpkin leaves; commonly used in Luo traditional recipes.
  • Amato ya Ripapaya – Papaya leaves; sometimes used medicinally despite bitterness.
  • Mrenda – Sesame leaves; occasionally used in traditional stews.
  • Amato y'enduma – Arrowroot leaves; edible when young and tender.

3. Wild and Cultivated Vegetables

  • Enderema – Jew's mallow; popular for its slimy texture in soups.
  • Amanagu – Also found wild, besides being cultivated.
  • Emboga – Wild sesame

4. Edible Mushrooms

  • Oboba – Large mushrooms, often grilled or stewed.
  • Amandegere – Small mushrooms, common after rains.
