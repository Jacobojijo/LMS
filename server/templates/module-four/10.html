4.6 Advanced Listening, Interpretation and Writing

4.6.1 Introduction

Folk Tales (Sigendni)

Stories, legends, riddles, and proverbs are an important part of Luo culture. They are traditionally recited in the [siwindhe], which is the home of a (widowed) grandmother.

Luo boys and girls gather there in the evenings to be taught the traditions of their culture. In the evenings, after people have returned from their gardens, they gather to tell and listen to stories. In the siwindhe, however, grandmothers preside over storytelling and verbal games.

4.6.1 Techniques for organizing and delivering speeches

Sigand Luanda Magere

AN: Aduaro gano nu sigand Luanda Magere.

UN: Ero gan nuaye sigandi ma mit no.

AN: Chon gi lala ne nitie ng'at moro ma ne iluongo ni Luanda Magere. Luanda ne Jaluo ma otumo wow adier. Onge gima ne oluoro kata achiel. To mana kaka Joluo duto, Luanda ne ohero, kendo ogeno Pinyluo ahinya. Luanda ne en jalweny ma ka ne jomoko otemo ni monjo kata ni temo mayo Joluo lowo, to onge kaka ne inyalo loyo Joluo ka wod Gem no ne nitie. Luanda ka ne owinjo yuak bul ma nyiso ni wasigu omonjo Pinyluo, kata ni oma oganda Luo gimoro, to ne odhi to oyudho mana okumba mare. Ae to otingo tong', athero to kod ojala mare. Lwanda ne wok gi Joluo wadgi. Kendo ne ogore gi wasigu ma odhir jomonj go duto woko mar Pinyluo. Ka po ni wasigu ne omayo Joluo njamni kata cham, to Lwanda Magere ne gore kar jo pier abich kata ma ohingo mago. Teko ne ne ng'eny ma ne omako mana dhog ji. Ne ogore ma nyaka oyud, kendo oduok dhok, rombe, diek to kod gik moko duto ma ne wasigu omayo Joluo.

Kware Joluo ma chon ne owacho ni kata bed ni kwe nitie, to oganda ok onego par ni wasigu bende mor gi bet mar kwe e chunygi. Joluo nyaka ng'e ni ndalo ma udak e kwe, nyalo bet ni wasigu to ok mor ni un gi kwe. Kendo kanyo ema yudore ni wasigu odich loso gike mag lweny mondo obi oma u gimoro amora ma oduaro. To, moloyo, gima wasigu biroga kawo e piny Luo to en mana lowo, piny to gi Thuolo ma Nyasaye Jachwech ma ne ochweyo polo gi piny ne omiyo kware gi daye Joluo ma chon.

Chieng' moro ne Luanda Magere odhi rango tugo mar adhula ma ne gore loka ma Uyoma. Adhula ne ni kind Joluo kod dho ot moro ma ne biro ka oa pinygi ma ni machiegni gi Afrika ma Mlambo. Ka Luanda wod Gem ne ochopo e pap mar tugo, to dichwo moro ma ne en achiel kuom welo, ne ochung' ma owene kom mondo obed ye. Luanda ne obuok ka wuoro ni, to kendo ere kaka ng'at ma wendo nyalo wene kom? Ne oloso gi wendo no:

"To apenji wendo, en ang'o kendo ma iwena mana kom ma nende oketni ka eni, ni mondo ibedie. Yaye, in ema in wendo ei Pinyluo."

Ng'ano ne odwoke.

"Da! ok owinjore mondo abed piny to in ichung'. Jalweny, in ng'at ma dhano oluoro ahinya niketch ka in e lweny to ok lo Joluo."

Luanda ne dhi kama tamore bet e kom no. To mak mana ni, nyako moro ma ber ahinya ma ne obet e kom ma luwo mare ne owachone ni,

"Wod Luo, an nyar piny ma bor ahinya. To kata kamano, humbi wawinjo kuonde duto. An ase bet ka agombo biro piny ka mondo anenie mana gi wang'a. Yie mondo ibed piny buta ka. An gi penjo mang'eny ma aduaro penji."

Luanda wod Gem, ka ne owinjo wach ma ne nyako ma ber ahinya no ne owachone to pache ne olal chuth. En koro ne oduaro mana kaka obed machiegni mondo owinj duond nyako no. Luanda ne obet e kom ma nene okuongo otamore betie chande.

"Nyako nyar Afrika ma Mlambo. Wachnae, bende en adier ni ise bet ka igombo nena?"

Nyar Afrika ma Mlambo ne odwoke.

"Luanda Magere, bende ing'eyo ni kawuono en chieng' ma wiya ok no wil kodo? Amor ndi neni gi wang'a."

Tugo mar adhula ne ogore kuom kinde ma lach. To kata kamano, Luanda to ne wang'e ok orango pap ma ituge, kata ma tin. Ne wang'e rango mana wang' nyako, nyar Afrika ma Mlambo. Tugo to ne otugore ruok. To haw marach, jo go adhula ma welo ne oloyo Joluo, rang'ach adek kuom achiel.

Luanda ne ohero mondo ochak one nyako no kendo. Omiyo, koro kar dok Gem thurgi, to Luanda Magere ne odhi nindo ei achiel kuom udi ma ne olos ni mondo welo onindie. Nyako nyar Afrika ma Mlambo to nyono mana kar ma Luanda Magere nyono.

Ka otieno ne ochopo, ma koro ne gin a pien, to nyako ne ochako loso ka penjo Luanda penjo mang"eny:

"Iwacho ni ihera ahinya, ok kamano? Hera en ang'o ka ok iduar wachona kar ma teko mari ma omako dho piny ngima nigatie? Luanda, yawa, wachnae kar ma teko ni yudore. An to eri anyisi ni ka ise miya duoko, to abiro yie bet kodi ka eni nyaka kiny odikiny. Bang'e to kiny nadog pinywa mondo anyis jonyuolna kanya achiel gi wedena ni iduaro kenda."

Luanda ne otamore wachone kar ma tekone nitie.

"Nyar ber, ka ahulo ni kar ma teko na nitie to mano ema biro bet giko ngima mara. An Jaluo, ok anyal nyisi gik ma gik Luo. Ka ihera kaka an aheri, to ok ane gima omiyo iduaro mana ni nyaka ahulni kendo apukni ng'eyo mag kware gi daye Joluo machon ma ok hulga ni jowoko."

Nyako ne onyiero ma tek.

"Yaye, un Joluo utamowa ng'eyo koru. Jaluo kata tum nadi to ok yie hulo ng'eyo mag daye gi kware Joluo ma chon. Wan gik ma chon duto ne wa se wito. An aneno ni Joluo bende onego wit timbe gi kit kware gi daye gi ma chon mondo kid bedie gik ma mono wa ng'eyo pachgi."

"Ah! yawa nyako, mano to kendo wach mane?"

Nyako ne otwenyo dhoge ae to oloso.

Luanda Magere. An to ero ase wacho ni ma oriere ni ka itamori wachona gima adwaro ng'eyo to awok e odni kar ma alose ni."

Nyako ne otumo kama ayo. Ne ochung' ka ochiko ka dho ot.

"Nyar kar ma bor, yie mondo iduog. An ok ane gima omiyo ijiwore ni iduaro ng'eyo gima ibor kodo. Jaber, yie mondo ibi inind e batha."

Nyako ne odhi ma onindo e bath Luanda Magere. Obudho ma tin to nyar piny ma bor ochako ruayo dend Luanda Magere gi mo ma ne okelo ka oa kodo pinygi ma ni yor Afrika ma Mlambo. Luanda ne mor ahinya. Nyako ne orange mos ka mbalo wange ne, ae to omedo loso kode gi duol ma mit ndi:

"To iwachona gima aduaro ng'eyo, kose? Ka ok iduar wachona to kara akan morni. Luanda, ka ok iduar wachoni kar ma teko mari nitie to kara we ruayo ni dendi."

Luanda ne odwoke ma piyo.

"Ero ling awachni. An teko na ni e tipo mara. Ka ng'ato ochuoyo tipo na gi tong', to alokora kidi."

Nyako ne mor ahinya gi wach no. Ne odhi nyime ruayo dend Luanda Magere gi mo ma ne okelo ka oayo kodo pinygi ma Afrika ma Mlambo. Jo ariyo go ne nyiero kendo goyo mbaka gi mor ma ng'eny' ma ok wachre. Ka ne gi se ol ahinya, to nindo ne otero Luanda Magere. Kanyo, nyako ne owok e ot ba oringo woko. Kuro, ne oyudo ka Jopinygi ma ne obiro tugo to kod ma ne ok obiro tugo, duto, ne orite. Jo piny ma bor ne ogoyo ng'wech ma tek ka ochiko woko mar Pinyluo.

Kinyne odikiny, ka ne gwen ose kok, to Luanda Magere ne olokore e pien ka luongo nying nyar piny ma bor. To kata kamano, ne oyudo ni kara nyako ne obuto othieno ka onindo. Luanda ne ochung' ba oweyo pien.

Ka ne odhi ni oyawo dho ot mondo owog woko, to ne opo mana ka bul yuak ma tek. Bul ne gore ma piny Uyoma kar ma ne Luanda Magere ne odhi nene tugo mar adhula ne oyiengore aming'a. Luanda ne ong'eyo ni yuak bul ma ne owinjo no ne en mar lueny.

Bul ne yuak ma tek ma kata Asembo bende ne iwinje. Jo Asembo ka ne owinjo kamano, to ne gibende giyudho bul ae to gichako goyo gocho ma nyiso ni wasigu omonjo Pinyluo. Jo Sakwa ka ne owinjo bund lweny to gibende gichako diemo margi. Jo Gem ne ogoyo bul mondo yawot Gem ochokre kendo odhi lweny. Jo Ugenya ne ogoyo bul ae to Jo Alego bende oluwe. Jo Usonga ne ogoyo margi. Jo Imbo ne uluwe. Jo Karsumo ne bende koro owinj ka bundgi dikore ma tek. Jo Kano ne ogocho. Jo Nyakach bende ne odonje. Jo Karachuonyo ne ogoyo bul ma tek ma piny omor mana ka polo. Kuonde duto yawot Luo ne wok e ute ka oruako lepgi mag lueny, kendo ka gi ting'o gik moko duto mag lweny.

Luanda Magere ne orikni ka temo yudo ng'at ma openj gima tumore. To kata kamano, chiegni ne oneno ka chuo duto ose ruako lep lweny kendo otingo tonge, athero, ojala to kod gik moko bende mag lweny. Luanda ne rango koni gi kocha. Haw ma ber, dhako moro ne onene ka oluorore ka ng'at ma olal. Dhako no ne odhi ba owuoyo gi Luanda.

"To ok eri chalna ni, in Luanda Magere? En ang'o ma kendo irito to chwo weteni ringo dhi riembo wasigu? Ret piyo idhi e iod yuora ma duong'. Ka ichopo kuro, to ibiro yudo lep lueny to kod gik moko duto mag lueny. Tum piyo!"

Luanda Magere, kany achieng gi Joluo duto ma ne obiro ka oayo loka duto mag Pinyluo, ne oromo Karsumo Nyahera. Kuro ema ne gilose chanro mar lweny ma ne dhi riembo kendo dhiro wasigu ma bor. Bang'e to ne gi dhi ma gigore gi wasigu. Goch ne okawo odiechieng' gi othieno ang'wen. To chieng' mar abich, kar odhiambo kanyo, ja Afrika ma Mlambo ma ne owen Luanda Magere kom e paw tugo adhula ne ogore ka sudo but Luanda Magere. Ka ne ose chopo machiegni, to ng'ano ne ochwoyo tipo Luanda Magere gi tong'. Kanyo gi kanyo, Luanda Magere wod Luo ne owinjo rem ma duong'. Wod Gem ne opodho piny ma tek. Kar ma ne opodhe no, ne piny oyiengore ma tek ma jolweny duto, kamoro amora ma ne gin tie, ne bende opodho ae to ochung' mana bang'e. Luanda Magere to ne olokore kidi.

Luanda Magere (English)

Long ago, there were many battles in Kano between the Nandi/Lango and the Luo. Nandi warriors terrorized the Luo, and with each defeat the Luo were further shamed. Dignity was restored only by the emergence of the hero, Luanda Magere (also Lwanda Magere). In one battle after another, this skilled warrior led the Luo to victory against their enemies.

Because of his courage and prowess in war, many women were attracted to him, and he captured some of his wives from vanquished enemies. In all, he had 25 wives, including Lango and Luo wives.

For years, he successfully led the Luo against the Nandi and Lango, and the Lango sought ways to overcome him. Lango elders deliberated long, and finally had a solution: as a gesture of peace and friendship, they offered Luanda Magere a wife, a beautiful Lango princess.

But her true mission was to discover the strength of Lwanda Magere's strength and to report this to her people. Luo elders pleaded with Magere not to accept the wife, but he refused to listen. Soon, she began to ask him the source of his strength. After much seduction, he could keep his secret no more.

He told his wife that his strength in battle was in his shadow; no arrow or spear aimed at his body could harm him, even if it penetrated his shield. To protect his shadow, he always chose to fight his battles in the evening or at night.

The Lango wife and spy immediately reported this secret to her people. She told them to lure Luanda Magere to a daylight battle and to aim their spears directly at his shadow. On the day of battle, the Luo elders cautioned him about going to war. But his pride would not allow him to listen to them.

Taking his shield, he led the Luo warriors to war. And the strategy of the Lango worked. The Luo were defeated, and Luanda Magere was hit where he was weakest - his shadow was assaulted. He died on the battlefield that day. His body turned into a rock which the Luo claim is still there to-date.

Legend of Kit Mikayi

Kit-Mikayi (also spelled Kit Mikayi, Kitmikayi, and Kitmikaye) is a rock formation, ator, around 40 m high situated about 29 km west of the town of Kisumu in westernKenya. It is about 1 km from the Kisumu-Bondo road. The sign board is on the gate of Kit Mikayi primary school and entrance is via N'gop-Ngeso primary school. Kit-mikayi means "the stone of the first woman", or "stone of the first wife", in Dholuo, the Luo language.

The legend behind Kit Mikayi, which in Luo dialect means "the stone of the first wife", is that: Long time ago, there was an old man by the name Ngeso who was in great love with the stone. Every day when he woke up in the morning, he could walk into the cave inside the stone and stay there the whole day, and this could force his wife to bring him breakfast and lunch everyday. The old man became passionately in love with this stone to the extent that when people asked his wife his whereabouts, she would answer that he has gone to his first wife (Mikayi) hence the stone of the first wife (Kit Mikayi).

An explanation of the shape of this unique stone is that the structure represents the Luo cultural polygamous family which had the first wife's house (Mikayi) built further in between on the right hand side was the second wife's house (Nyachira) while the third wife's house (Reru) was built on the left hand side of the homestead. This rock also is seen to have a nuclear family whereby the father (Ngeso) being the middle stone followed by the bulky Mikayi (first wife), then Nyachira (second wife) followed by Reru (third wife) and further in front they have the child which is representing Simba (which is the house for the first born boy in the homestead). From a long time, this stone has been a sacred place for the villagers to worship in times of trouble.

Locals living around the stones are known as the Luo-Kakello clan. The site is associated with sacrifices and many legends from pre-Christian times, especially stories explaining the meaning of the name.

Kit-Mikayi is a regional point of sightseeing interest, especially among the neighbouring Luo tribes. It also has become a popular localpilgrimage site for followers of the Legio Maria sect who come to the rock to pray and fast for several weeks at a time.

Singing (Music) in Luo Culture

The Functional Nature of Luo Music

Music in Luo culture was never created merely for entertainment; it served specific functional purposes, aligning with the community's needs and practices. Music was a unifying force that accompanied the Luo people through ceremonies, rituals, labor, and social interactions.

Ceremonial Music

Luo music was prominently featured in various ceremonial contexts:

Funerals (Tero Buru): Songs were used to praise the departed, console the bereaved, keep mourners awake at night, and express sorrow. Music was also integral to cleansing rituals and the expulsion of spirits.

Celebrations: Events such as beer parties (Dudu), ohangla dances, and communal gatherings featured vibrant musical performances. These gatherings also welcomed returning warriors or accompanied wrestling matches (Ramogi).

Courtship and Socialization: Music served as a tool for courtship and fostering social ties, often enhancing the allure of communal celebrations.

Work Songs

Both communal and individual work activities were accompanied by music. Communal tasks like building and weeding featured group songs to foster teamwork, while individual activities like pounding cereals or winnowing included rhythmic melodies that synchronized with the task.

Ritualistic and Religious Music

The Luo used music in spiritual and ritualistic contexts:

Chasing Away Evil Spirits: Songs like Nyawawa were believed to deter night spirits from villages.

Rainmaking and Divination: Music played a critical role in these practices, often facilitated by spiritually attuned individuals.

Healing Rituals: Songs accompanied divinations and other healing ceremonies, emphasizing their spiritual significance.

Characteristics of Luo Music

The unique qualities of Luo music reflect the community's lifestyle, history, and aesthetic values.

Melodic and Rhythmic Features

Lyrical Melodies: Luo songs often featured lyrical melodies with vocal ornamentations, especially when conveying significant messages.

Syncopated Rhythms: Complex syncopation was a hallmark of Luo music, characterized by anacrusic beginnings (where phrases start before the downbeat).

Call-and-Response Style: Most songs were performed in a call-and-response format, creating a participatory dynamic between the lead singer and the audience. Solo performances, such as chants (Gueyo), often introduced dances like the Dudu.

Performance Styles

Pakruok (Self-Praise): This unique tradition involved an individual halting the musical performance to recite a chant glorifying themselves. This often marked a climax in the performance.

Sigalagala (Ululation): A distinctive ululation frequently marked the height of musical and emotional intensity.

Dance and Movement

Luo dances were characterized by grace and synchronization with the syncopated beats:

Movements often included the shaking of shoulders or the coordinated movement of legs and waist to the rhythm of music.

Dances like the Dudu were introduced by chants and performed with significant energy.

Musical Instruments

The Luo people developed and mastered a diverse range of musical instruments, categorized into three main types:

Percussion Instruments:

Drums, clappers, metal rings (Ongeng'o/Gara), and shakers provided rhythmic foundations.

String Instruments:

The Nyatiti, an eight-stringed lyre, was central to Luo music, often accompanying elegant dance movements.

The Orutu, a type of fiddle, added melodic layers to performances.

Wind Instruments:

Instruments like the Tung' (horn), Asili (flute), and Abul (trumpet) enriched the harmonic texture of musical pieces.

Evolution of Luo Music

Traditional to Modern Transitions

Over the years, Luo music transitioned from traditional forms to modern styles such as Benga music. Emerging in the 1950s, Benga fused traditional rhythms with Western instruments, particularly the guitar, which replaced the Nyatiti as the primary string instrument.

Characteristics of Benga Music:

Lively guitar riffs.

Songs performed in Dholuo, Swahili, and English.

Popularity extending beyond Luo communities, becoming Kenya's distinctive pop sound.

Preservation of Cultural Identity

Despite modernization, the essence of Luo music remains evident in its lyricism, rhythms, and cultural significance. Contemporary musicians continue to draw inspiration from traditional forms, ensuring the longevity of Luo musical traditions.

Symbolism in Costume and Ornamentation

Traditional Luo music performances were complemented by elaborate costumes and ornaments:

Women's Attire:

Sisal skirts (Owalo), beads (Ombulu/Tigo), and clay (red or white) enhanced their beauty and movements.

Men's Attire:

Skins (Kuodi/Chieno), headgear (Ligisa), shields, spears, and reed hats symbolized masculinity and cultural pride.

Costumes were crafted from locally available materials, reflecting the Luo's resourcefulness and aesthetic sensibility.
