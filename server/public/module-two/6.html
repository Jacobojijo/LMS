2.4 Review & Practice

2.4.1 Summary of Verbs & Adpositions

Verbs in Dholuo

Verbs in Dholuo function as symbols for events, changes of state, processes, actions, and speaker intentions. Unlike English, where a verb is essential for a well-formed sentence, Dholuo allows verb-less constructions, as seen in:

Peter japuonj ('Peter is a teacher')
Peter ber ('Peter is good')

Criteria for Identifying Verbs in Dholuo

  • Cognitive Function: Verbs represent events or states of affairs.
  • Morphological Behavior: Verbs show aspectual distinctions tonally or segmentally.

For instance, in:

Juma ofuwo ('Juma is foolish'), the verb fuwo takes a perfective marker (o-).
Juma ber ('Juma is good'), ber does not show aspectual variation, marking it as an adjective.

Tense in Dholuo

Tense encodes the temporal relation between an event and the time of utterance. In Dholuo, it is expressed both lexically and grammatically. Key lexical markers include:

  • Past: Nene (remote), Nende (near past), Nyoro (yesterday), Nyocha (day before yesterday)
  • Future: Kiny (tomorrow), Orucha (day after tomorrow), Chieng' (remote future)
  • Present & Immediate Timeframes: Kawuono (today), Sani/Tinende (now), Ang'/Wang' (near future)

Idiomatic expressions also modify tense, such as a-a ('just now') and a-biro nene ('soon'). Some markers, like sani and koro, are interchangeable in certain contexts.

Negation in Dholuo

Negation is primarily marked by ok, which negates not just the verb but often the entire proposition.

Ok adwar chiemo ('I don't want food') negates the full statement Adwaro chiemo ('I want food').

However,

chiemo e ma ok adwar ('It is food that I don't want') narrows negation to chiemo ('food').

Similarly,

Opiyo e ma ok dwar chiemo ('It is Opiyo who doesn't want food') restricts negation to Opiyo.

Reflexive Verbs in Dholuo

Reflexive verbs in Dholuo are marked by the morpheme -rV, where -r- is the reflexive marker and -V is a person-specific vowel:

  • -a (1st Person Sg.)
  • -i (2nd Person Sg.)
  • -e (3rd Person Sg. /Pl., 1st Person Pl.)
  • -u (2nd Person Pl.)

The form -e is unmarked and can replace the other person markers.

For example:

Ahinyor-a/e ('I have hurt myself')
Ibinyor-i/e ('You have hurt yourself')
Ohinyor-e ('He/She has hurt himself/herself')
Wahinyor-e ('We have hurt ourselves')
Uhinyor-u/e ('You have hurt yourselves')
Gihinyor-e ('They have hurt themselves')

In citation, reflexive verbs typically end in -e (e.g., hinyore 'to hurt oneself'), but native speakers often use -ruok constructions (e.g., hinyruok 'self-harm'). This distinction is evident in phrases like:

Hinyruok rach ('To hurt oneself is bad')
Tuonruok tek ('Self-denial is difficult')

True Reflexive and Reciprocal Constructions

True reflexives in Dholuo occur when the subject and object refer to the same entity, as in:

Dhiang' nang'ore ('The cow is licking itself').

Reciprocal constructions indicate mutual actions between participants:

Otieno gi Apiyo oherore ('Otieno and Apiyo love each other').

Ambiguity arises in cases like

Dhok nang'ore ('Cows are licking themselves/one another')

where context determines whether the meaning is reflexive or reciprocal.

Impersonal Constructions

Dholuo reflexive verbs also form impersonal constructions, shifting focus from the actor to the action itself. These are categorized as:

  • Event-oriented: Emphasizes the occurrence of an action (Odi mukore – 'Your house is crumbling').
  • Actor-oriented: Identifies an unspecified actor (Imuko odi – 'Your house is being knocked down').

A scale of actor-explicitness exists, with

Ng'ato muko odi ('Someone is knocking down your house')

being more actor-oriented than

Imuko odi

Some reflexive constructions function figuratively to emphasize large-scale events

Kong'o madhore ka ('People are drinking beer here')

often implying social commentary.

Additionally, reflexive verb forms indicate an object's capacity for action:

Chuma ni ng'adore ('This metal can be cut').

Adpositions in Dholuo

Adpositions establish relationships between linguistic elements, indicating their syntactic and semantic connections. In Dholuo, only prepositions exist, marking locative, instrumental, directional, and beneficiary relationships.

1. Locative Prepositions

Locative expressions in Dholuo often use the copula ni combined with e (a general locative marker) and optional elements (X) for specificity. Examples include:

Pala ni ka – 'The knife is here.'
Rombe ni e pap – 'The sheep are in the field.'

The preposition kuom ('on') is used for living beings, whereas e remains for inanimate objects.

2. Instrumental Prepositions

The preposition gi ('with') introduces an instrument:

Oloo kunyo bur gi beti – 'Oloo is digging a hole with a machete.'

It also conveys means

Wadhi Mombasa gi gari – 'We are going to Mombasa by train'

accompaniment

Nyathi nindo gi min – 'The child is sleeping with its mother'

and equipment

Odhi e pwodho gi kweru – 'He is going to the farm with a hoe'

3. Directional Prepositions

Dholuo generally omits prepositions when movement is unspecified

Dhi nam – 'Go to the lake'

but e is used for specific locations

Dhi e dho nam – 'Go to the lakeshore'

The preposition ir ('to') is applied only for human destinations

Nyithindo dhi ir japuonj – 'The children are going to the teacher'

while yo ('towards') indicates movement without a definite destination

Japuonj odhi yo Kisumu – 'The teacher has gone towards Kisumu'

4. Beneficiary, Loser, and Sufferer Roles

The preposition ni/ne marks various roles, depending on the verb:

Ng'ato ong'iewo ne nyathi pala – 'Someone has bought a knife for the child' (beneficiary).
Ng'ato olalo ne nyathi pala – 'Someone has lost the child's knife' (loser).
Ng'ato ogolo ne nyathi pala – 'Someone has drawn a knife to attack the child' (sufferer).

2.5 Adverbials

2.5.1 Introduction

In Dholuo grammar, adverbials are linguistic elements that modify verbs, adjectives, or entire sentences by providing additional information about time, place, manner, frequency, degree, reason, and purpose. Adverbials help specify the circumstances of an action, making speech more precise and contextually rich.

2.5.2 Predictions over Events, Actions and Propositions

Predictions over Events, Actions

This includes such adverbial functions as:

Locative - which indicates where something is located, e.g.

Ng'ato nindo e tiend yath ('Someone is sleeping under the tree')

Directional - which indicates the physical orientation or direction of a static or moving body as the case may be, e.g.

Od sikul ochung' ka ochomo nam. ('The school building stands facing the lake.')
Nyithindo dhi (e) sikul ('Children are going to school.')

Durative - which shows the duration of a given event, e.g.

Ne gitiyo (kuom) higni apar ('They worked for ten years.')

Extent - which shows the spatial equivalent of duration, e.g.

Asewuotho mael abich ('I have walked five miles.')

Point time - which marks the time at which an event takes place, e.g.

Kenyatta notho higa mokalo ('Kenyatta died last year.')

Manner - which indicates the way in which an event takes place, e.g.

Gari ringo matek ('The train goes fast.')

Instrument - which indicates what is employed (usually as a tool) in carrymg out something, e.g.

Andiko gi kalam ('I am writing with a pen.')

Accompaniment - which indicates that one nominal acts as an accompanying participant to another nominal, e.g.

Aloo dhi nam gi wuon ('Aloo is going to the lake with her father.')

The word gi 'with' in (a) above is homophonous to the gi 'and' which occurs in the following sentence.

Aloo gi wuon dhii nam ('Aloo and her father are going to the lake.')

where Aloo gi wuon is a compound subject.

Equipment - which, although introduced by the same form gi, indicates what one equips himself with for some intended purpose, e.g.

Aloo dhi nam gi ndoo. ('Aloo is going to the lake with a pail' (e.g. to draw water))

Means - which shows how one achieves a certain purpose (in expressions which adopt an adverbial strategy for showing it), e.g.

Adhi ikul gi tienda ('I go to school on foot')

Predictions over Propositions

Unlike in the above cases, where the scope of the adverbial element is limited only to some part of the proposition, usually the action or state designated by the verb, in the case we are dealing with now the sentence in question has a whole proposition which can be shown to fall withm the scope of a given adverbial expression. The adverbial functions under this category include:

Factive - which indicates that a given proposition is believed by the speaker to be a fact, e.g.

Kiwacho adieri (to) Jo-Kenya ohero pesa. ('Truly speaking, Kenyans love money.')
Ka ok ariambo (to) koth biro chwe kawuono ('If I don't lie, it will rain today.')

Modality - which indicates the speaker's attitude towards what he says, e.g.

Ka nyalore (to) Onyango biro duogo. ('If (it is) possible Onyango will come back.')
Ka de to ngàto de okonyo ng'atni. ('It would be good if someone could help this man.')

Concession - which introduces a proposition contrary to what should be expected from known facts, e.g.

Kata aonge pesa to nyaka abed gi ot. ('Even if I lack have money I must have a house.')
Wayie ni okwelo to ok unyal nege. ('We agree that he has stolen, but you can't kill him.')
