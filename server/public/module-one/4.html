1.2.1.4 Relative Clause

When the reference of a noun cannot be clarified satisfactorily by any determiner, then Dholuo language use a device known as the relative clause.

A relative clause is a sentence embedded into a noun and marked in some way as subordinate to the particular noun for which clarity of reference is sought.

Now, linguists generally distinguish between two of relative clauses, namely restrictive and non-restrictive relative clauses. These two types of relative clause are exemplified in sentences 1 and 2 respectively.

1. Ruoth ma onge dhako rach.

'A chief who does not have a wife is bad.'

2. Kata Otieno, ma onge dhako, bende dwaro bedo ruoth

'Even Otieno, who does not have a Wife, wants to become the chief.'

In each of the above sentences [ma onge dhako] is the relative clause. However, in (1) it is restrictive, i.e. states the necessary condition for being the referent of a noun, while in (2) it is non-restrictive and simply provides additional information about the noun. In stipulating the reference of a noun, only restrictive relative clauses are relevant.

In Dholuo, a relative clause is introduced by the morpheme [ma/ma]. The morpheme is usually free but often occurs as a clitic, especially in one-word predicates e.g. [diel matin] 'a small goat', [nyako maber] 'a good/beautiful girl'), and generally loses its vowel when attached (as a clitic) to a vowel initial word e.g. [mofuwo] 'foolish' [ma + ofuwo]; [makelo] 'which I have brought' [ma + akelo] etc.
